Summary:

Satoshi Nakamoto, the creator of Bitcoin, chose a ten-minute block time for Bitcoin as a balance between confirmation time and wasted work due to chain splits. However, there may be an opportunity for optimization in this number due to advances in technology and the absence of a rigorous formula to determine the optimal rate. While 10-second block times would result in many chain splits, going lower than 10 minutes may not be an issue. Jonathan Sterling raises the question of whether this is something that should be explored further and asks to be directed to relevant resources if he is mistaken.Blockchains that produce blocks quickly tend to have reduced security due to high stale rates. The propagation time of a block through the network may cause miner A's block to be wasted if miner B mines another block before miner A's block reaches B, thus affecting network security. Additionally, if miner A is a mining pool with 30% hash power and B has 10%, A will have a higher risk of producing a stale block compared to B. As a result, blockchains that produce blocks quickly are likely to lead to one mining pool having a large percentage of the network hash power.Reducing the average block time may also require reducing the block size accordingly to prevent the blockchain from growing at twice the rate or to avoid transaction fees going down, making it easier for spammers to bloat UTXO sets. There have been numerous proposals to overcome the downsides of faster blocks, such as the "Greedy Heaviest Observed Subtree" (GHOST) protocol. Adán Sánchez de Pedro Crespo, CTO of Stampery Inc., based in San Francisco and Madrid, sees no benefit for Bitcoin from faster blocks and asks for valid points on why 10 minutes is the optimal rate, other than "if it ain't broke, don't fix it."
