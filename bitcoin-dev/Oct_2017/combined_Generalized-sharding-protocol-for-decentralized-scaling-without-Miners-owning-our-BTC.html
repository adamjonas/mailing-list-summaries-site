Summary:

In October 2017, a proposed generic sharding protocol for blockchains, including Bitcoin, was discussed on the bitcoin-dev mailing list. The proposal aimed to allow users to transfer their coins from one chain to another by burning them on the original chain and creating minting transactions on the destination chain. This method would require minimal modification to the Bitcoin protocol and enables scalability without sacrificing security or decentralization.The proposed protocol is fully decentralized and can be used by thin clients, nodes, and miners. The verification of transactions is done client-side, and users do not need to give up ownership of their coins to miners to achieve scalability. This distinguishes it from other proposals, such as Drivechain, which offers objectively worse security and forces users to give up their coins to a single group for whatever sidechains they interact with.Despite receiving support from the community, there are concerns raised about the proposal. Technical arguments and questions include whether it would change the number of Bitcoins in existence and whether miners would be forced to issue coins twice. Discussions also revolve around the implementation of two sidechains and how it may lead to loss of money for users.The discussion also touches upon the Tao Effect's one-way peg proposal, which is criticized for making it impossible for any side-to-main transfer to succeed. In contrast, a two-way peg would be more effective. One participant in the discussion argues that this method would not work since it is impossible to have a fixed rate of Bitcoin and a second way to mint coins at the same time.Lucas Clemente Vella argues that if the new chain is Pareto optimal and better in some aspects than the original chain, the initial resistance is just an unstable equilibrium. He compares this to a herd of buffaloes attacking a lion, where the first buffalo to attack is at a disadvantage, but if enough of the herd follows, the movement succeeds beyond turning back, benefiting both those who attacked and those who didn't.Overall, the discussion reflects an ongoing effort to find solutions for scaling Bitcoin while maintaining its security. The proposed generic sharding protocol offers a potential solution, but further technical exploration and discussion are required. Tao Effect refused to respond to any more emails unless they were from core developers.
