Summary:

In an email exchange between Matt Corallo and another individual, they debated the possibility of removing certain bits in Bitcoin transactions that are redundant and have not been used. Corallo believes that these bits are unnecessary, while the other individual argues that removing them could harm users who may have transactions with these bits already included unless the security of the Bitcoin protocol itself was under existential threat.The discussion highlights the debate around whether certain aspects of the Bitcoin protocol should be removed via a soft-fork and the potential consequences of doing so. The lack of usage of these bits indicates that they are not needed and compares them to OP_NOPs, which are unlikely to be included in transactions requiring their execution. Corallo suggests that perhaps the reason these bits are not seen in use on the blockchain is that users trying to use them are caught up in the fact that they are not being relayed by default and having difficulty redeeming them. He argues that it is not acceptable to first make transactions non-standard and then use the fact that they are not being used to justify a soft-fork. On the other hand, the other individual counters that he knows of users who have their funds tied up due to changes in Bitcoin Core's default relay policy and believes that permanently destroying their funds before they have a chance to get their transactions mined would be unfair. They also believe that there is no precedent for soft-forking out working transactions with these bits, excluding those secured by Scripts using the reserved OP_NOP1-OP_NOP10. The two individuals seem to disagree on whether or not the potential harm caused by removing these bits outweighs the benefits of simplifying the transaction format. It is important to consider all potential consequences before implementing any changes to the Bitcoin protocol and to weigh the benefits against the potential risks to users' funds. The discussion also touches on the issue of default relay policies and how changes to them can affect users' ability to use certain transactions.
