Summary:

The Bitcoin alert system, which allowed trusted parties to send messages to wallet software and remotely stop transactions, has been completely removed from Bitcoin Core due to problems with scalability, governance, and accountability. Additionally, there was a risk of misuse due to a lack of knowledge about the origin of messages sent through the system. Gregory Maxwell plans to send an alert message triggering a maximum sequence alert to eliminate any further potential of reputation attacks and then disclose the private key in public to prevent any future misunderstandings. A proposal has been made to retire a key shared by many people, and the plan involves sending a message with a maximum sequence number minus one to notify all nodes of the retirement date. After a few months, the private key will be published.The email from Gregory Maxwell to Peter Todd discusses the issue of alert propagation on the P2P network becoming less reliable as nodes upgrade to software. It is suggested that the final alert be sent to peers using old protocol versions to ensure that it is received by all nodes. In an email chain on bitcoin-dev, Johnson Lau suggested waiting a few months before publishing the private key after the final alert, which raised questions about why the wait was necessary. However, Lau explained that this would give time for all users to update their software and for developers to create patches or solutions to any issues that may arise after the alert is disabled.Overall, this highlights the importance of careful planning and communication when managing security risks in complex systems like Bitcoin. Although there may be some small disruptions to production systems, it will not be worse than if an alert were sent to advise about a new system upgrade. The disclosure of the private key in public will eliminate any further potential reputation attacks and diminish the risk of misunderstanding the key as some special trusted source of authority.
