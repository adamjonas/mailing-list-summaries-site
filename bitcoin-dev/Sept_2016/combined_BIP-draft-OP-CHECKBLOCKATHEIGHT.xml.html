Summary:

Luke Dashjr proposed a new opcode for the Bitcoin scripting system called OP_CHECKBLOCKATHEIGHT to address reissuing bitcoin transactions when the coins they spend have been conflicted or double-spent. Russell O'Connor expressed concern about the proposal potentially destroying an important property of Bitcoin and suggested a three-argument version for comparing gbits-to-compare, blocknum, and hash. There is uncertainty about the endian. The discussion also raised concerns about a script feature that allows for back referencing transactions, which could cause a fungibility hit.Dashjr's proposal aims to solve the issue of double-spending and conflicts in bitcoin transactions by allowing users to create UTXOs that are only valid in specific blockchains. However, the proposed operation, OP_CHECKBLOCKATHEIGHT, has some issues as it refers to spending outputs with a small number of confirmations and cannot protect unconfirmed outputs. Dashjr also recommended preventing overuse and enforcing a maximum survivable reorg to ensure that users could handle exposed coins sensibly. He suggested not allowing overly short back references to avoid the issue of persistent chain forks.Another BIP proposes a new opcode known as OP_CHECKBLOCKATHEIGHT for the Bitcoin scripting system to address the issue of reissuing bitcoin transactions when the coins they spend have been conflicted or double-spent. The full details of this proposal can be found on Luke Dashjr's Github page. While the new opcode enables victims of double-spends to pay untrusted creditors without the risk of paying them twice, some argue whether the added complexity is worth the reward of protecting Bitcoiners from untrusted creditors. In a scenario discussed, Alice pays Bob with tx1a, which gets N confirmations before Bob pays Charlie from tx1a's output using tx2a. A reorg eliminates the block that tx1a existed, and a conflicting tx1b is mined instead, making tx1a and tx2a invalid. Bob pays Charlie again with tx2b, whose inputs do not conflict with tx2a. Another reorg eliminates tx1b, allowing tx1a, tx2a, and tx2b to all be mined, resulting in Charlie being paid twice. As two reorgs are required for this scenario to be applicable, it's easier to wait for tx1b to be confirmed sufficiently deeply in the chain.Overall, the proposals present innovative approaches to longstanding problems in the Bitcoin network. However, there is uncertainty about their implementation and potential impact on the network.
