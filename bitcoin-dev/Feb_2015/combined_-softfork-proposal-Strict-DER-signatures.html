Pieter Wuille, a Bitcoin Core developer, has proposed a softfork to make non-DER signatures illegal due to recent events that have made clear the risks of depending on OpenSSL for consensus rules. The proposal involves replacing the dependency on OpenSSL with the libsecp256k1 library, which will provide signature verification and public key recovery functionality. The draft BIP text and implementation can be found on GitHub, and comments and criticisms are welcome.Furthermore, Pieter Wuille proposed an improvement to the Bitcoin protocol that would replace OpenSSL with libsecp256k1. The proposal was made in light of recent events that revealed the risks of depending on OpenSSL for consensus rules.In January of 2015, Pieter Wuille proposed a softfork to make non-DER signatures illegal in order to fix the issue of transaction malleability and reduce the risk of depending on OpenSSL for consensus rules. The proposal was assigned the BIP number 0066 and received positive feedback from several implementers. However, there were concerns raised about the possibility of an nlocked spend with an invalidly encoded signature.In February of that same year, Wuille proposed a one-line change to BIP66's DERSIG that would softfork but later retracted it after it was pointed out that there were still edge-cases that weren't covered. Instead, a cleaner solution was proposed: only allow an empty byte vector as an invalid signature. Pieter also suggested individual R and S length restrictions that would limit them to no more than 32 bytes, excluding the padding 0 byte in front, to standardness requirements.The proposed changes were shared on the Bitcoin Development mailing list along with sample codes and test cases to ensure compatibility. Lastly, Pieter Wuille has proposed replacing OpenSSL with libsecp256k1, which will provide signature verification and public key recovery functionality, which are currently handled by OpenSSL.
