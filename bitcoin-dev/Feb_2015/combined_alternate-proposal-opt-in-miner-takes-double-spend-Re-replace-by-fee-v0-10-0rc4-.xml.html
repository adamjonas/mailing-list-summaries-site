In discussions dating back to 2015, Bitcoin experts have warned about the risks associated with relying on zero-confirmation transactions and the potential for double-spending vulnerabilities. Various solutions were proposed, including the use of multisignature notaries as the most practical approach for actual security for unconfirmed transactions. However, some argued that a general-purpose scripting language improvement could eliminate the need to rely entirely on inherently unreliable P2P networking.Despite these warnings, people are still using zero-confirmation transactions for low-value transactions, exposing themselves to potential losses. Todd suggests implementing fidelity bonds as protection against double-spends, but there are questions about how they would function and who would pay out in the event of a double-spend. The discussion also touches on the use of multisignature notaries for high-value transactions with untrusted customers, and the possibility of predicting and pricing risk associated with different types of transactions. Matt Whitlock recounts an incident where a merchant at the Bitcoin 2013 conference lost money due to zero-confirmation transactions that depended on other unconfirmed transactions that never went through. While this risk can be priced in when enough data is available, further investigation into the situation could reveal valuable insights. This feature would be opt-in and soft-forkable, meaning high-value coins would not be at risk of failure. The aim is to improve practical security of fast confirmation transactions.
