Summary:

In 2011, the Bitcoin development community had several discussions regarding the block generation method and timestamping within the blockchain. One of the proposed methods involved having each node generate the most difficult block possible while simultaneously listening for the most difficult block generated before a certain time. However, there were concerns about miners cheating the system by falsifying timestamps. The proposed solution was to reject blocks outside of an accepted timestamp band by checking the timestamp of a received block against the node's own clock and the average network clock. Fixed difficulty block generation was ultimately chosen due to its clarity in adjusting to a target difficulty, although concerns were raised about large power drops. Additionally, there were worries that miners could hold onto extremely hard blocks before announcing them, leading to irreparable network splits. Gavin Andresen also pointed out the dangers of a "hardest block found in X minutes" scheme. Despite recognizing the benefits of this result, he ultimately abandoned the suggestion because it would not work.In November 2011, Dr. Andy Parkins proposed a system to prevent miners from cheating their reported datetime (T) and increase their probability of mining a block through false timestamps. He argued that time makes no difference with chain difficulty summed correctly, and the hardest chain always wins. His system required the two-hour window to be reduced and a lower limit to be added. Furthermore, Dr. Parkins noted that his system prevents an attack possible with current Bitcoin where one recalculates the entire chain.The importance of a deterministic way to determine whether to accept or reject a block and the role of network clocks in the process were emphasized throughout the discussions. It was recognized that the clients use a community clock as "current," which is established from the time they receive from peers in the "version" message. However, miners are not bound to use this clock, but if they do not use time that approximates what their peers are using, their blocks would be rejected. NTP was mentioned in the source as an alternative to the imperfect decentralized clock used by the client.Overall, the discussions demonstrate the ongoing efforts to improve the functionality and security of blockchain technology. The concept of the "hardest chain" was discussed, and while it is more secure, invalidating a chain would still be difficult regardless of the approach taken. The community recognized the need for a decentralized clock and a way to measure total chain work.
