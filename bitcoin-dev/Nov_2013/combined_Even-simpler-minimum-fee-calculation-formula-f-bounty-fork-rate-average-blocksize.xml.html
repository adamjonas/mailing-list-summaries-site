The document is a technical discussion on the expected return on a block in bitcoin mining, which argues that increased centralization leads to higher returns on investment per unit hashing power. The author provides a detailed derivation of $P(Q,L)$, which is the probability that a block of size $L$ produced by a miner with relative hashing power $Q$ will be orphaned. The assumptions and conditions for this derivation are also given. The probabilities and outcomes of a miner finding a block during full consensus are discussed.In an email conversation between Michael Gronager and Peter Todd dated November 13, 2013, they discuss the average fees for Bitcoin transactions over the last 90 days. Gronager found the average fee to be approximately 0.0003BTC per transaction, close to the theoretical minimum of 0.00037BTC per transaction. However, Todd cautioned against quoting this figure as it could be misleading for newbies trying to understand the concept of fees in Bitcoin transactions.Gronager suggests that participating in a mining pool can be advantageous due to the economy of scale it provides, even though miners have to pay a fraction of their income to the pool owner. Todd agrees that there is an incentive for miners to join pools and centralize, which can only be countered by regulation or social pressure. The equations show that the incentive exists at any amount of hashing power, but p2pool might be an exception since it doesn't require a linear blockchain to function.Several email exchanges between Gronager and Todd in November 2013 discuss the speed of Bitcoin in terms of alpha, with Todd calculating it to be 39.62 microseconds per byte or 24 kilobytes per second. They also discuss the impact of pools on earnings, with Gronager suggesting that smaller miners are better off in a pool, but larger Q gives higher returns, and there is a threshold above which it is more profitable to mine alone. There is an incentive to centralize all the way up to one miner with 100% hashing power, although P2Pool might be an exception.Gronager posted a write-up explaining why transaction fees were eight times too low due to the optimal block size. He used formulas from last week to get a measure for alpha as a function of the average fork rate and an average block size. Using this formula he found that the minimum fee should be f > P_fork*E_bounty/S_average. Gronager discovered that blockchain.info keeps historical graphs of the number of orphaned blocks per day, averaging 1.5 over the past year.The network-wide orphan rate has been steady apart from the March blockchain fork. Reduction in the size of new block announcements can be achieved through transmitting and relaying as "merkleblock" with full bloom filters. Mining revenue is a fixed-size pie; hence, if everyone agrees to accept somewhat higher orphan rates for more transaction volume, there is no difference. However, this is a classic free-rider problem because a miner could defect to try to get a lower orphan rate. Thus, relaying all blocks in a race is probably the right thing to do.The average fee for Bitcoin transactions is currently very close to the modeled minimum orphan cost, which may be attributed to the power of inefficient markets to arrive at a correct price. The messy market with tens of thousands of individual decisions may have arrived at the 'correct' price, even if nobody involved has any clue how or why it happened. However, it could also just be a coincidence. Overall, the all-important number in this case is alpha, the network latency that is dependent on various things such as interconnectivity, bandwidths, and software quality. Standard clients can be set up to choose a better fee, as all parameters in the formula are easily measured.
