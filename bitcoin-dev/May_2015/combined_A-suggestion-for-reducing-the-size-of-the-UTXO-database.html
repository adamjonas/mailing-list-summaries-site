The issue of unspent transaction outputs (UTXOs) and their growth in the Bitcoin network has been a topic of discussion for quite some time now. Many solutions have been proposed to address it, including designing wallets that consolidate unspent outputs, picking highly common denominations of bitcoins, aggregating them to obtain these denominations, and then using several consolidation targets in a wallet to avoid over-aggregation problems. However, there are challenges to this proposal, including the fact that spending unconfirmed outputs requires a different security model on the part of the receiver, and wallet software that refuses to spend unconfirmed outputs.One possible solution proposed by a wallet developer is to make free transactions more reliable so that wallets can submit free transactions to consolidate UTXOs at night or during periods of low demand and fit them into available block space. Another suggestion is to use replace-by-fee, which allows for double-spending pending defragmentation transactions if they are still unconfirmed when needed. Additionally, large wallets that have lots of addresses owned by multiple users like exchanges, blockchain.info, and Coinbase can consolidate UTXOs very effectively when building transactions.The developers of popular wallets like Core, Electrum, Armory, Mycelium, Breadwallet, Coinbase, Circle, Blockchain.info, and others have a vested interest in the continued usefulness of Bitcoin and should not be opposed to changing their UTXO selection algorithms to one that reduces the UTXO database instead of growing it. Miners benefit from these types of transactions as it reduces the amount of storage they need to dedicate to holding the UTXO. Relays could enforce this type of behavior by refusing to relay or deprioritizing the relay of transactions that don't use all of the available UTXOs from the addresses used as inputs.Overall, the conversation highlights the need for developers to consider privacy concerns when building wallets and improving algorithms. The issue of UTXOs and their growth in the Bitcoin network remains a challenge, with various proposals being suggested to address it. Another issue discussed is the problem of having to wait for confirmations before making a second payment. This issue occurs when a user only connects to their own trusted Bitcoin node, which limits the ability to receive instant confirmations according to the Bitcoin protocol. To solve this issue, it is suggested to modify the logic of the app so that if a trusted node is set, the app assumes that the user knows what they are doing and transactions made by the user always work.The email thread discusses the issue of UTXO growth and proposes a solution to reduce the size of the UTXO database. The typical wallet selects only the minimum number of unspent outputs when building a transaction, leaving lots of unspent outputs that are left unused. The proposal suggests designing wallets to consolidate unspent outputs by selecting all of the UTXOs from a particular address or wallet, sending however much needs to be spent to the payee, and sending the rest back to the same address or a change address as a single output.Wallets need to do a better job with defragmentation in order to avoid high fees when making payments with many tiny UTXOs. However, the reason why wallets don't prioritize this issue is due to manpower. A solution proposed by a wallet developer is to make free transactions more reliable so that wallets can submit free transactions to consolidate UTXOs during low demand periods. This would help defragment UTXOs and reduce transaction fees.Various email exchanges and mailing list discussions have taken place regarding the issue of unspent transaction output (UTXO) growth in Bitcoin wallets. One suggested solution is to consolidate UTXOs by selecting them all for a transaction and sending back the rest as a single output, which could limit the growth of the UTXO database over time. Miners and relays could enforce this behavior by refusing to relay or deprioritizing transactions that don't use all available UTXOs from input addresses. However, there is a complex trade-off between UTXOs, fees, and privacy that is hard to optimize for all use cases.The age of UTXOs is also a factor in determining fees, with some proposing modifications to select UTXOs based on how many are available at each address and their age instead of just their age.
