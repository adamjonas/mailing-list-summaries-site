Bitcoin's importance should not be compared to a theoretical perfect peer-to-peer system, as it competes with traditional bank notes and credit cards. Although Bitcoin may not be the ideal instant peer-to-peer serialization mechanism, it still holds value and relevance in the current financial landscape. Despite facing challenges in terms of proof of work, Bitcoin's significance remains undiminished.Double spending is a common issue in the world of cryptocurrency, where a person spends the same digital currency twice. However, Bitcoin has been designed to prevent this from happening, but concerns arise when someone could double spend if not for Bitcoin's technology. Successfully double spending does not lead to getting money back, but instead results in being chased down by an angry shopkeeper, calling the police, or being blacklisted from the store.In a discussion on the Bitcoin development mailing list, Chris Pacia expressed his disapproval of a proposed scheme in which a payment system requires users to have double the funds they need to make a purchase, calling it an "ugly hack." He argues that not everyone will have the extra funds available, making it inconvenient for them to use the payment system. The idea of reserving a higher amount to cover risks is not new, as seen in hotel or car rental payments. The question arises whether it is important for a payment system to allow users to quickly spend their last penny. However, it was noted that the scheme would not be mandatory and users could still opt to wait for confirmations or rely on existing trust. Mike Hearn responds to the issue of double spending fraudulent activities in payment networks, acknowledging that while it is a concern, the risk of getting caught and blacklisted makes it unlikely for attackers to attempt such an attack. However, he acknowledges that a dishonest mining pool could potentially execute such an attack for a fee, but this would not be feasible for high-value items due to the need to wait for block confirmation.Peter Todd discusses the implementation of replace-by-fee and child-pays-for-parent policies, stating that they are more rational for miners to prioritize transactions. He also points out that replace-by-fee does not protect against sybil attacks and does not directly solve Finney attacks. Enforcing hashing visibility of block contents can help detect Finney attack double-spend and broadcast it, which is beneficial for decentralization.The author suggests asking local businesses if they would be willing to accept a form of payment that allows anyone to steal from them by paying double the purchase price to some other random person as a solution to Finney attacks. However, this proposal is met with skepticism as no business would willingly participate in such a scheme.In conclusion, the context highlights proposed solutions to the problem of 0 confirmation transactions and Finney attacks in Bitcoin. While some of these solutions have their limitations and challenges, Bitcoin still holds value and relevance in the current financial landscape when compared to traditional bank notes and credit cards.
