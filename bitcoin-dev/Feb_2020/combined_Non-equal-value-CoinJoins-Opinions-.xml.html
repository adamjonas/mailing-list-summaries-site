Summary:

The CashFusion protocol is a privacy-enhancing technique for Bitcoin Cash transactions. It allows for multiple inputs and outputs while obfuscating transactions. The protocol proposes using arbitrary amounts of inputs in the neighborhood of ~0.1 BCH to create a transaction with 10 parties and 100 inputs, resulting in 10 outputs of around 1 BCH. This makes it virtually impossible to link inputs and outputs. However, equal-valued CoinJoins can be used as an alternative, where the equal-valued outputs are completely unlinked from the inputs.The email conversation between Yuval and ZmnSCPxj highlights some privacy concerns related to CoinJoin transactions that need to be addressed. The CashFusion research uses combinatorics to avoid amount linkages in non-equal value coinjoins, but this idea is not similar to the one presented in the Knapsack paper in 2017. The number of valid transactions grows so incredibly fast that it is impossible to know which ones are real even if all partitions are found. There are still doubts about the effectiveness of the proposal's numbers.Ádám Ficsór and ZmnSCPxj recently engaged in an email conversation where they discussed whether non-equal value CoinJoins can be relied on for privacy. The conversation revolved around a paragraph from the CashFusion specification, which claims that with 10^92 possibilities, there is essentially no linkage. However, ZmnSCPxj argues that most users will not have nearly the same output of "around 1 BTC" when deployed on a real live mainnet. He further explains how change outputs of an equal-valued CoinJoin would have similar analyses to CashFusion since the same analysis "around 1 BTC" can be performed with the CoinJoin change outputs "around 0 BTC". He argues that if the math requires that one has "around 1 BTC" outputs per user, it might as well use equal-valued CoinJoins. Additionally, he explains that the change outputs of an equal-valued CoinJoin have the same linkability as the outputs of the equivalent CashFusion transaction.The email thread discusses the limitations of CoinJoin and ZeroLink protocols regarding their inability to fully prevent linkage between pre- and post-mix coins, as well as bloat that arises from multiple rounds with many inputs and outputs per user. The author proposed a new protocol that introduces chaumian reissuable/redenominatable tokens as an intermediary stage between input/output phases, allowing for unequal amounts while retaining the advantages of fixed denominations. This protocol includes several subtypes of mixing rounds: decompose, mix, optimize, and combine, which would require mining fees subsidized and batching to alleviate bloat. The author suggests that this approach can be made size-efficient by keeping low hamming weight outputs virtual.In another email exchange, nopara73 responds to the combinatorics and proof sections of a document about CoinJoin-based privacy called "The Breaking of Mixing Secrets." They express concern about perspectives that focus on linkability information revealed by a single coinjoin transaction in isolation and mention how overhead could potentially be mitigated with OP_CHECKTEMPLATEVERIFY and Taproot. Finally, they question the proof and its applicability, expressing doubt about the privacy of mixing based on a dubious hardness assumption and a computationally bounded adversary.To summarize, in unequal inputs/outputs coinjoins, Knapsack is currently the best solution available, but it is not as effective as equal-outputs transactions. The CashFusion proposal is difficult to dismiss entirely and has potential attacks that need to be addressed. However, there are still doubts about its effectiveness, and equal-valued CoinJoins can be used as an alternative.
