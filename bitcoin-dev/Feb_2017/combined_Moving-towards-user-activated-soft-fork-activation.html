Summary:

The Bitcoin-dev mailing list has been the platform for numerous discussions around the scalability of mining pool operator complexity with regards to soft forks and the possibility of hash-minorities attacking hash-majorities. Soft forks are entirely optional to use post-activation, and relay policy rules prevent non-standard and invalid transactions from being relayed and mined by default. Users can activate a soft fork permissively, where miners do not have to produce new version blocks and non-upgraded miners' blocks will not be orphaned. The BIP9 "versionbits" soft fork activation method is also permissive. A combined approach of a user activated soft fork and BIP9 can leverage the warning systems in BIP9 and offer the option of a faster hash power coordinated activation or activation by flag day.A User Activated Soft Fork (UASF) is being discussed as a potential tool when miners are opposed to broader economic interests. However, there are debates over its effectiveness for new features as miners can orphan non-standard transactions affected by the proposed rule change. A carefully executed UASF is riskier than a soft fork but far less risky than a hard fork. The success of a UASF relies on major economic hubs agreeing to upgrade and exclusively accept UASF coins. On the Bitcoin-dev mailing list, discussions were had regarding the scalability of mining pool operator complexity with regards to soft forks. In a discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, participants debated whether a "hashpower activated soft fork to censor transactions" could be seen as censorship. One member argued that Bitcoin's defence against censorship and disruption is due to the broad distribution of over 50% of hash power among a large number of people. The conversation then turned to the idea that miners have no technical or ethical obligation to follow any particular set of rules. Instead, security is based on decentralisation, not well-behaved people or software.Bitcoin developer, Shaolin Fry, has proposed an alternative method called "flag day activation" to activate soft forks for Bitcoin. The current methods, IsSuperMajority and BIP9, have limitations including the need for trust in hash power validation, natural veto power, upgrade inertia, and unnecessary attention on miners. The flag day activation method does not require a supermajority or veto power. A minority-hashrate soft fork requires more social support than the widespread agreement typical of softforks. The security of the Bitcoin system depends on the level of decentralization in its two aspects, mining and payment validation. A small consensus is less secure than a large one as it is more prone to change or forks. Mining is highly centralized, which makes it easy to enforce a fork via miner collaboration but hard to do so without it. Therefore, enforcing a fork via the economy, specifically through economic hubs, is being discussed. The author of BIP9 has proposed an extension to the existing protocol that introduces an additional activation parameter for soft forks. The new parameter, called "activationtime", specifies a minimum median time past of a block at which the deployment should transition to the locked-in state. This allows full nodes to coordinate synchronized activation based on a median past time using the BIP9 state machine.Overall, the discussion highlights the challenges and potential risks involved in proposing and implementing changes to the Bitcoin protocol. The debate over different forks of Blahcoin illustrates the subjective nature of opinions and lack of objective measures to resolve disputes. The conversation also touches on the idea that miners have no technical or ethical obligation to follow any particular set of rules, and security is based on decentralization. Concerns over centralization in Bitcoin, both in terms of mining and the economy, and the potential for negotiations to make Bitcoin purely political money, are also highlighted.
