Summary:

The security of Taproot is a topic that has been discussed in various contexts. One conversation between Lloyd Fournier and Tim Ruffing explored the benefits of Taproot and its security. Combining Schnorr and Tapscript signature schemes offers new opportunities for non-interactivity and fungibility, but understanding how different crypto components interact would improve our understanding of it. LLFourn's poster presentation at the Financial Cryptography conference 2020 demonstrated the security requirements for the tweak hash function in Taproot. Taproot combines two signature schemes - Schnorr and Tapscript - into one public key. Three scenarios/games are considered when evaluating whether Taproot is secure in the context of Bitcoin: Taproot Forge, Covert Taproot, and Second Covert Taproot. To prove that Taproot is a binding commitment scheme in the Random Oracle Model is straightforward. However, modeling key generation in this game can be cumbersome.The email thread discussed the use of an interactive key generation protocol similar to coin tossing, which generates a uniformly random key. This was compared to MuSig signing, which requires coordination and interaction to arrange getting into an n-of-n in the first place. The benefits and drawbacks of reducing communication rounds for creating signatures during offchain operation were also discussed. A counter to this is using Taproot with an n-of-n internal key and tapscript that has n `OP_CHECKSIG` operations.Overall, Taproot offers many benefits but also adds complexity to making security claims. With further research and understanding of its interplay with other crypto components, it could greatly benefit Bitcoin as a whole. While the cost of Taproot is mostly borne by theoreticians as they consider its implications, the user and Bitcoin as a whole benefit from the complexity it adds to making security claims in the GGM.
