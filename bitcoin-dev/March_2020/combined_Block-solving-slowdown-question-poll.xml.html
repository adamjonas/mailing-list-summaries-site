Summary:

In a recent email thread on the Bitcoin Protocol Discussion group, various issues concerning the security and stability of the Bitcoin network were discussed. One of the major concerns was the possibility of a difficulty adjustment trigger being built into Bitcoin software for when the expected block production rate is far behind its expected value. The proposal suggests that miners detecting that the expected 2016 blocks have not been mined after twice the expected wait time (4032 * 10 minutes = 28 days) should signal their recognition in any block they produce, leading to an expedited difficulty adjustment.Another concern raised in the email thread was the potential drop in mining power that could make the time until the next retarget much longer. Two practical solutions were suggested: do nothing or hard fork a difficulty reduction. If most mining equipment is still available for operation and bitcoins retain a small fraction of their value compared to the previous retarget period, then doing nothing is probably best as block space becomes scarcer. However, if the Bitcoin price has plummeted more than 99% in two weeks with no hope of short-term recovery or if a large fraction of mining equipment has become unusable, then it's worth discussing a hard fork to reduce difficulty to a currently sustainable level.The author also posed a question about the long-term plan for Bitcoin's security once the block reward drops too low. The user suggested implementing an adjustable block reward where bitcoin holders can vote on the inflation rate, balancing their incentives to keep the reward low while also incentivizing mining to keep the network secure. However, ZmnSCPxj, an expert on the Bitcoin protocol, noted that this already happens implicitly through the transaction fee mechanism. The difference between the sum of all inputs and the sum of all outputs creates a "field" for Bitcoin holders to vote on how much security they need, making it costly for them to do so. This mechanism is also the long-term plan for lowered block rewards in the future and is currently the best-known solution to tackle this problem.The email thread also included discussions around other concerns such as the tragedy of the commons, where individuals acting in their self-interest can lead to an undesirable outcome for everyone involved. The example of fish farms in a lake was used to explain this concept. There were also concerns raised about the possibility of a 51% attack if mining profits drop low enough for a state actor to mount such an attack. The security of UTXOs and the importance of transaction fees in maintaining security were also discussed with the idea of a voting system for determining the cost of security proposed as a possible solution. Finally, the discussion included potential solutions for handling a drop in mining power, including holding a lottery for mining and hard forking to reduce difficulty to a sustainable level. The idea of increasing the Bitcoin supply beyond 21 million was considered fundamentally broken as it would change the meaning of Bitcoin and create a contradiction in its use. However, there may be holding companies with specialized scripts that cost $500 - $1000 to add to the blockchain and allow them to handle transactions for thousands of customers. The email thread overall provided insights into the challenges and solutions related to ensuring the security and stability of the Bitcoin network in the long run.
