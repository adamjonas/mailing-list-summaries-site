Summary:

The discussion on /r/bitcoin focuses on the legal implications of owning an asset, particularly in relation to Bitcoin wallet software authors and their potential custodial relationship with users. The concept of custodial relationship in financial laws is defined as having the ability but not the right to dispose of an asset, and this has potential legal implications for wallet providers. Discussions also highlighted the importance of preventing losses through malicious auto-updates and the need for peer-to-peer solutions. There were debates on the implications of possessing private keys and the difference between ownership and the ability to deal with an asset, which can vary under different jurisdictions.One of the consequences of a custodial relationship is that some legal authority might try to force seizure of user funds. Therefore, it’s important to make sure that the process by which an update happens is controlled by more than one person and there are mechanisms in place to create good audit logs of how exactly an update happened. Tamas Blummer explains the difference between ownership and ability to deal with an asset in German law, mentioning that knowing the private key and owning linked coins is not necessarily the same in front of a court. This means that being able to deal with an asset does not make one the owner. Moreover, Tamas Blummer suggests that the legal hurdle to force confiscation through a wallet provider might be lower if the target user is not domestic. In addition, discussions highlight the importance of preventing losses through malicious auto-updates and suggest creating systems where multiple people have to sign off and review an update before it can spend user funds. It is also recommended that companies should not be located in the same country as their users or that users should strongly encourage getting their wallet software from companies not located in the same country as them. In a conversation between Peter Todd and a lawyer with a finance law background, they came to the conclusion that Bitcoin wallet software authors may have a custodial relationship with their users, especially if auto-update mechanisms are being used. This has potential legal implications as custodial relationships are highly regulated by financial laws in most jurisdictions. The example of a malicious software update seems more like theft rather than a true custodial relationship. The email thread discusses the concerns about the jurisdiction of wallet providers and the challenges faced in doing business, with a recommendation to move towards peer-to-peer solutions like Core, Electrum Wallet, Mycelium, Local Trader, and Open Bazaar which avoid centralizations and do not collect user data or require identification. Finally, the conversation suggests that although technology can be a strong argument before a court, it may not always fully align with legal definitions.
