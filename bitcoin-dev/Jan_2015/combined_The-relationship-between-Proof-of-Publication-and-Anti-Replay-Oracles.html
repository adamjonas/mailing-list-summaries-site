Summary:

In discussions about cryptocurrency design, various topics were covered. One of these was the use of proof-of-publication systems to implement anti-replay mechanisms. Peter Todd proposed a specific implementation of an anti-replay system based on proof-of-publication and discussed its advantages in trade scenarios. However, there are concerns over the security of some decentralized exchange systems, including Freimarkets, which do not require proof of publication of bids or asks. The conversation also emphasized that determining who is in possession of the message is crucial for proof-of-publication, which cannot be achieved with anti-replay alone. Todd suggested extending the definition of proof-of-(non)-publication to include a predicate P to efficiently prove that no message m such that P(m) holds has ever been published. Additionally, the limitations of anti-replay as a tool for proof-of-publication were discussed, with Todd arguing that it does not provide information on ownership or possession of messages.In email exchanges, the advantages and disadvantages of proof-of-publication markets were debated, with some arguing that they provide accurate unbiased price information while others argued that other models for peer-to-peer (p2p) markets are also secure. Philosophical differences between proof-of-publication and anti-relay do not necessarily have practical implications. In a discussion on "disentangling" blockchain design, candidate layer properties that could be used to construct a decentralized PoW-chain assured immutable history based ecash system were outlined. The two properties identified were time-stamping and namespace.Todd proposed two cryptographic primitives that operate on message, public key, and a valid signature. He suggests extending the definition of proof-of-(non)-publication to take in some predicate P to prove that no message m such that P(m) holds has ever been published efficiently. In a discussion about decentralized exchanges, Mark Friedenbach suggests that decentralized exchanges would be possible today using SIGHASH_SINGLE if only the protocol supported multiple validated assets.Adam Back and Peter Todd debated the security implications of using new primitives operating on message, pubkey, and signature. Todd suggested using a trusted third party to implement AntiReplaySign() would be more secure than trying to prevent double-spending with math alone. Back suggests an alternative method using one-show signatures, but Todd argues that verifying ECDSA signatures directly through Bitcoin scripting would work just as well.Mark Friedenbach and Peter Todd discussed the security issues surrounding Freimarkets, with Todd arguing that sybil attacks leading to front-running are incentivized in distributed blockchain systems. Todd also contends that global ordering of bids and asks is necessary for real businesses using these systems. The conversation ends with Todd warning against the potential dangers of sybil attacks on the entire system.Jorge Timón discusses the advantages of proof-of-publication systems in trade scenarios. He provides an example where Alice sells 1 unit of A for 100 units of B and Bob is willing to pay up to 200 Bs for 1 A. In a proof of publication system, Alice publishes her order and Bob could publish his order at 200 Bs, which would execute at 150 Bs. After seeing Alice's order, he knows he doesn't need to pay that much, so he publishes an order buying for 100 Bs. This way, Alice gets 100 Bs, and Bob pays less than what he was willing to pay, making everyone happy. Jorge explains that proof-of-publication makes tradeoffs and options available and lets end-users pick the right one for their needs. He also suggests that different mediums will become used for the proof-of-publication needed, ranging from directly on a major blockchain to centralized-but-audited proof-of-publication schemes - AKA centralized exchanges - to standard exchanges.Overall, the discussions revolve around the use of proof-of-publication systems and their limitations in cryptocurrency design, as well as the security concerns surrounding decentralized exchanges.
