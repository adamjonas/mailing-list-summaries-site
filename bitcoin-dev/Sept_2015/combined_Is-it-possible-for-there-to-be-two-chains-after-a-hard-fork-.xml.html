Summary:

The Bitcoin network is currently facing discussions about the possibility of hard forks causing a split in the system. The Schism Hardfork section of BIP99 discusses this issue, stating that market forces dictate that hashrates will follow users instead of users following the hashrate majority. Users who disagree with hardfork rules may decide not to upgrade, resulting in a split between two chains that could persist for more than a few days.However, there are concerns about the feasibility of continuing to use the old version of Bitcoin in case of a hard fork if a small percentage of miners refuse to go along with the majority. This could result in enormously long transaction confirmation times, a huge backlog, and a massive price drop for coins on the fork. The basic security assumption of Bitcoin is that a supermajority of miners are honest, but this does not mean they are infallible or always make reasonable decisions.Gavin Andresen, a well-known Bitcoin developer, expressed doubts and concerns about the feasibility of a hard fork in an email thread. He questioned whether anyone would stick to the 5% fork in the face of enormously long transaction confirmation times, a huge backlog, and a massive price drop for coins on the smaller fork. Andresen also discussed Bitcoin's security assumption that a supermajority of miners are honest and pointed out that the reliance on miners' honesty is not exclusive to simplified payment verification (SPV).The ongoing discussion among developers highlights the potential risks associated with hard forks in the Bitcoin network. While some users may support smaller forks due to ideological reasons, this may lead to a significant loss in value and transaction backlog for the smaller fork. The discussion on the mailing list also revolved around Bitcoin's basic security assumption that a supermajority of miners are honest, and how relying on miners' honesty is necessary even if one runs a full node.Gavin Andresen initiated a discussion on the possibility of two chains persisting for longer than a few days after a hard fork. Allen Piscitello responded by stating that mining empty blocks is not fraud and requested clear definitions of terms such as "honest miner" and "fraud." Mike Hearn added to the conversation, stating that even running a full node does not protect against a dishonest miner majority committing fraud. Gavin suggested moving the discussion to a more general list and emphasized the importance of miners following the rules of the protocol.Gavin went on to explain how the creation of smaller forks could cause a significant drop in Bitcoin's price as people rush to sell their coins on the smaller fork. He outlined how a send-to-self transaction could be created on the 95% fork, which is only valid on the same chain but could still transfer the same coins to the 5% exchange, allowing users to sell them at whatever price they can get while owning the coins on the 95% fork. Hard forks are unlikely to happen at the 95% threshold, but at the 75% threshold, it is possible with mostly rational users, especially on contentious issues such as block size.Some individuals may support smaller forks due to ideological reasons, such as their belief that even 1 MB is too large and making financial sacrifices to make a small-block parallel fork a reality, true to their vision of what's needed to make Bitcoin pure. However, this could lead to a significant loss in value and transaction backlog for the smaller fork. Gavin's arguments highlight the challenges and potential risks associated with hard forks in the Bitcoin network.
