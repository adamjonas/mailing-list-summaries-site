Summary:

On May 22, 2017, shaolinfry via bitcoin-dev shared the Barry Silbert agreement, which proposed changing the bit used to signal as a way to activate SegWit through a different deployment. However, some members of the community expressed concerns over the technicalities of the proposal and argued that it did not address the need for a capacity increase beyond 2MB nor replay protection. There were several options discussed for activating SegWit, including waiting for 95% of hashrate to signal, deploying BIP148 UASF, redeploying SegWit on a new bit or making BIP148 miner-triggered (MASF) with a lower threshold above 50%. James Hilliard suggested the fourth option, which would effectively lower the threshold from 95% to 65%, giving an improved chance of activating SegWit quickly. However, this still leaves the activation in the hands of miners, which could be a disadvantage.Jacob Eliosoff proposed a suggestion to avoid incompatibility with existing nodes by triggering BIP141 signaling when Silbert/NYC Segwit2MB proposal's lock-in supports 80% for bit 4. This would minimize the incompatibility with existing nodes, but problems such as an aggressive hard fork schedule and possible chain splits between SegWit2MB and BIP141 nodes remain. The email exchange on bitcoin-dev discusses the activation of SegWit and the proposed Barry Silbert agreement. Some members express concerns over the technicalities of the proposal and suggest activating BIP141 immediately and then forking to 2MB in September 2018 or 2019, together with forcenet/spoonnet. The email also mentions various activation strategies, including mandatory signaling, BIP149, and a new proposal by James Hilliard. Tom Zander suggests a solution to the main issue causing problems with 0.13.1+ having many segwit related features already active. In addition, Zander recommends replacing the bit1 activation code with a bit4 activation logic, which is based on 80% and has no end-date, in order to eliminate worries about bip9 bit1 needing only 55% hashpower to be triggered. BIP91 or similar to activate BIP141 via mandatory signaling is suggested as the fastest safe path forward. This would allow for the development of HF code based on the assumption that BIP141 is active, so that only the BIP141->HF upgrade/activation codepath has to be tested. Rolling out some features together makes sense, but a HF has required features that would conflict with features in the segwit rollout causing major complexity/testing issues. By doing a staged rollout of segwit and then a HF, we get a single testable upgrade path. On May 26, 2017, a discussion was held regarding the feasibility of scheduling a hard fork (HF) in Bitcoin development. James Hilliard suggested that it would not be practical to schedule any HF until BIP141 is fully active. However, Tom Zander argues that this should not be an issue since BIP141 has been in development and testing for two years.The email emphasizes the need for safe and collaborative solutions to move forward and highlights ongoing discussions around Bitcoin's scalability. If the community is unable to form consensus around segwit alone for political reasons, further research into hard fork design may help, but even forks tied together would nearly certainly need to activate months apart. The writer believes that closed-door agreements between selected participants are not the way to garner consensus to change a $30bn decentralized system. The writer's purpose is to assist in the immediate activation of SegWit, which only requires hashrate to participate, and provide technical input.
