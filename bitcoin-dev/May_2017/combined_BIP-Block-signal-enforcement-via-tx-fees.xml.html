Summary:

The email thread discusses various proposals and discussions related to the deployment of softforks in Bitcoin without resorting to extreme measures such as UASF. Luke-Jr proposed a BIP draft for OP_CHECKBLOCKVERSION to put economic pressure on miners, but Peter Todd disagreed, stating that version signaling is unenforceable and users ultimately adopt rules, not miners. Another proposal suggests rewarding activation instead of mere signaling, and an opcode for an assurance contract for activation of a softfork. Discussions also revolve around the inadequacy of English language specifications and the need to use the "reference implementation" as the specification.Additionally, there are concerns about mining centralization, the risk of false signaling, and the need to specify a timeout for versionbits. False signalling miners have a two-week LOCKIN period to upgrade, otherwise they could already lose money. The discussions highlight the challenges and complexities involved in deploying softforks and the need for careful consideration and collaboration among developers and stakeholders in the Bitcoin community.In a separate discussion on Bitcoin, one person argues that paying someone with better Bitcoin to hashrate conversion via fees may be more economically logical for some, due to differences in electricity rates by country. However, another person argues that reducing the complexities of mining and validation should be the focus of developers rather than finding ways for people to avoid participating in these distributed roles. The discussion also involves potential issues with nVersion signaling, with users being responsible for adoption of rules and concerns over false signaling.Eric Voskuil argues that mining is broken and difficult to influence miners without a huge expense. The author of a post suggests that individuals simply need to mine themselves to influence miners. In response, Luke Dashjr comments that enforcement remains primarily by users and it is unlikely that users will pay for signaling of softforks prematurely. Luke Dashjr has proposed a new BIP draft for OP_CHECKBLOCKVERSION to provide an alternative to BIP 8 and enable the community to put economic pressure on miners to deploy softforks. The proposal involves using a script system for construction, but it is suggested that it would be better to use the transaction version number instead by soft-forking in a rule. However, some feel this is still a bad idea because it may encourage miners to falsely signal readiness, which undermines both BIP 9 and BIP 8. Peter Todd stated that version signaling is already technically unenforceable. Thus, enforcement remains primarily by users. In addition, he commented on the inadequacy and unreadability of English language specifications in the proposed BIP8 specification. Russell O'Connor expressed his concern that implementing a construction based on opcode would encourage miners to false signal readiness. However, Luke-Jr argued that this was not the case. He suggested that rather than using the script system for this construction, it would be better to use the transaction version number instead by soft-forking in a rule. He also mentioned that making use of the transaction version number is superior to adding an opcode because it does not interfere with caching of script validity. Eric Voskuil argues that paying people to run nodes or signal readiness is not beneficial from either personal/economic or security/decentralization standpoint.
