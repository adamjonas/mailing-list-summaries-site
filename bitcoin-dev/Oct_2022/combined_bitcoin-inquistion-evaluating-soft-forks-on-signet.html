Summary:

In recent email exchanges on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, Bitcoin developers discussed various approaches to evaluating and implementing soft fork proposals in the Bitcoin community. One proposal was to test soft forks on custom public signets to avoid unintended side effects on mainnet operation. Another idea was to use a separate testnet like Segnet or Signet for testing soft forks outside of core since it does not require community consensus.Matt Corallo laid out a four-step process for progressing a soft fork, including finding a champion to move it forward. The lack of champions following steps one to three is the issue with progressing a soft-fork. Michael Folkson is enthusiastic about enabling proposed soft fork functionality on signet, which he believes is a good idea.The email exchange also discussed proposed consensus changes on signet, such as enabling Taproot, which is expected to improve privacy, scalability, and security. However, concerns were raised about potential disruption to users who may not be interested in testing soft fork proposals.Anthony Towns proposed a new activation method for consensus changes called "bitcoin-inquisition," designed to simplify and expedite the process of activating proposals. The proposed approach involves research, development, evaluation, and deployment. Bitcoin-inquisition would allow for up to 2**29 signals with a standard signal consisting of 16 bits for specifying a bip number and 8 bits for specifying a version of that bip. While some argue that the lack of progress is due to a lack of test frameworks, others feel that the issue is a lack of champions following all the necessary steps. Matt encourages those who want to contribute to Core and become a champion for a soft-fork to reach out for mentorship. The importance of community involvement and participation in the development of Bitcoin is emphasized throughout the conversation.Overall, the discussions among Bitcoin developers focus on finding ways to evaluate and implement proposed soft fork proposals more efficiently and effectively, with less disruption to users. The proposed bitcoin-inquisition approach may help consensus changes be easily backported once they move into the deployment phase. However, concerns are raised about the pressure faced by gatekeepers to merge unfinished or buggy soft fork proposals on the default signet, which may disrupt all default signet users.
