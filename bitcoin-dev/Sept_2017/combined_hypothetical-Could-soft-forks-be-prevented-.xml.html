Summary:

The Bitcoin-dev mailing list has been discussing the possibility of preventing soft forks in cryptocurrency software that strives for immutable consensus rules. Soft forks, which apply further restrictions on Bitcoin, are a concern as they may drag people along with them, even those who oppose the changes and never upgrade. It is suggested that hard forks are the only way to prevent soft forks, as seen with Bcash. However, hard forks require people to opt-in, whereas soft forks drag people along with them.The discussion also explored the idea of creating a cryptocurrency with immutable consensus rules to offer investors great certainty and predictability. To make such a cryptocurrency immune to soft-forks, it would have to restrict all transactions to isStandard at the consensus layer, which may prevent new features from being added via soft-forks. There are concerns about the practicality of preventing soft forks entirely, as anyone-can-spend transactions make it difficult to ban them entirely.Designing for maximal fungibility and transaction structures that minimize interaction with the blockchain could minimize the surface for transaction censorship, which aligns with the goal of creating an un-soft-forkable cryptocurrency. Ethereum is mentioned as a blockchain that can't soft-fork; they have to always hardfork.There are concerns about the potential manipulation of the community through political means, leading to the degradation of consensus rule change. It is important to note that while hard forks are always possible, they are a clear split, and something that people must opt into. Each party has to make a choice, and inertia is on the side of the status quo.ZmnSCPxj argues that it is impossible to prevent soft forks in a cryptocurrency. They provide examples of anyone-can-spend scripts that are currently disallowed from propagation by IsStandard but could still be included in a block by a miner. The only way to prevent soft forks is to hard fork against them, according to ZmnSCPxj.The discussion involves the use of "anyone-can-spend" transactions and how they are required for soft forks. It is important to note that in principle, a soft-fork can also be soft-forked out, meaning that a publicly known soft fork done by miners only that user node software did not upgrade for first by opt-in adoption. It depends on what software that the general user-base is using (especially exchanges). If a majority of miners have deployed a hidden soft fork, then the soft fork will only last as long as they can maintain their majority.In conclusion, the possibility of preventing soft forks in cryptocurrencies has been discussed extensively on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list. While hard forks are always possible, they require people to opt-in, whereas soft forks drag people along with them. The practicality of preventing soft forks entirely is still uncertain, but designing for maximal fungibility and transaction structures that minimize interaction with the blockchain could minimize the surface for transaction censorship.
