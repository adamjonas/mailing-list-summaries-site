Summary:

Bitcoin developers have been discussing the possibility of adding an expiration time to Bitcoin addresses in order to prevent the reuse of old addresses, which can lead to privacy and operational issues. Peter Todd proposed adding a UI-level expiration time to the new BIP173 address format as a solution to this problem. He suggested using hour or month resolution for the expiration time and suggested that at least hour-level precision could help services like exchanges reduce the risks of losses after a hack.The proposal raised concerns about UI and terminology, but hour or month resolution could be used for the expiration time, giving enough time for the UI to display an exact time or to round off to the nearest day in the local timezone. However, some participants feel that address expiration should not be part of the protocol, but rather a guidance feature for sending wallets and the ability for receivers to generate invoices.In addition, there were conversations about potential issues with making payments to Bitcoin addresses, such as errors in the address resulting in larger payment amounts. Todd proposed embedding the amount in the address itself or explicitly adding the amount to the address checksum to prevent errors. There were also discussions about baking expiration policy into the protocol, disallowing transactions that get confirmed too late to remove the need for refund logic or asking customers to pay extra. However, this proposal has severe downsides, including making it difficult for non-upgraded nodes to broadcast such a transaction.Some users argued that this feature is already included in the payment protocol and is redundant, while others pointed out that the payment protocol hasn't caught on and doesn't cover all use cases that addresses do. The discussion also covered the resolution required for setting the expiration time, with hour or month resolutions suggested as alternatives to seconds-level precision. There were also discussions about whether an expiry date could be included in a BIP-173 address within a payment request, and some participants felt that email or printing a Bitcoin address was simpler than using the BIP-70 payment protocol, which involves back-and-forth communication. However, others suggested that the payment protocol already includes an expiry date and adding it to the address would be redundant. To achieve seconds-level resolution, it was suggested that a large range would be more useful than high precision. In this context, relative time does not make sense. The author proposed that using five characters would provide roughly two-minute resolution that lasts for 100 years. To avoid running out of space, another character and a range that won't expire anytime soon can be used without creating a need for more checksum space.Overall, the conversation on the bitcoin-dev mailing list focused on the technical details and implications of using different address formats in BIP-70 payment requests and implementing address expiration in Bitcoin. There were concerns about privacy issues with redirects, scalability, and the potential for miners to game the system.
