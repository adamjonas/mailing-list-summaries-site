One proposal is to set a maximum fee rate calculated as a filter over past block rates to prevent "outlier behavior." The Monopolistic Price Mechanism proposed in the RSOP paper was also debated, with concerns about its real-world implications for fee selection UX. One proposal suggests moving to a "pay lowest winning bid" model, where all transactions pay only the smallest fee rate paid by any transaction in the block. Another proposal is for a new feature called "rebateable fees," which would allow transactions to specify an implicit fee as well as one or more explicit rebateable fees. However, some members of the mailing list have expressed doubts about the real-world implications of these proposals.There are also concerns about the potential for miners to limit the number of transactions in a block to capture the maximum fee, which could lead to network congestion. A proposed fix to the fee model that is not easily broken by paying the miner out of band was discussed, but there were concerns that it could also lead to centralization. Overall, the discussion highlights the need for further exploration and optimization of the Bitcoin network's fee structure and incentives for miners.Time sensitivity is an important factor to consider, as the fee users are willing to pay often changes over time. The RBF loop was discussed as a possibility for adjusting fees based on time sensitivity. Reliability-based-price-discrimination might also allow higher mining revenue via having txes with differing fee rates in the same block. The Bitcoin community has been discussing various proposals to fix the current fee model. However, there are concerns that such models could lead to centralization and reduce visibility of fees. Proposed solutions include a maximum fee rate calculated as a filter over past block rates or establishing a marginal fee rate and allowing for rebateable fees to contribute towards meeting the remaining marginal fee requirement.
