Summary:

The Bitcoin-dev mailing list recently discussed the concept of transaction confirmations and what it means for an unconfirmed transaction to be trusted or not. According to the response, a transaction is considered trusted if it is final and cannot be replaced through conventional means, not in a block that was reorged out, the 'spend zero conf change' option is set, it's in the mempool, and all inputs are from us. However, one participant pointed out that even though it cannot be replaced through conventional means, it is always possible to replace an unconfirmed transaction by asking a miner to mine a conflicting transaction directly.Another topic of discussion on the mailing list was the use of full replace-by-fee (RBF) by miners. Peter Todd mentioned in an email on April 11, 2018 that a significant minority of miners appear to be using this method. RBF is used to replace an unconfirmed transaction with a new one that includes a higher fee. However, there is uncertainty about whether final transactions with a sequence of 0xffffffff can be replaced using RBF. ZmnSCPxj clarified in another email thread that miners can ignore nSequence when adding transactions to blocks, meaning that "full replace-by-fee" where nSequence is ignored for replace-by-fee purposes is expected to become the norm. This means that wallets should be designed to only trust transactions that have confirmations, making it difficult for malicious actors to exploit the replace-by-fee feature.The ListTransaction RPC call was also discussed in an email thread on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list. The sender had three questions related to the call, including what it means for a transaction to be "trusted", when the "confirmations" field can be -1 (conflicted), and what happens if they add a watch-only address to their bitcoind process that is not part of their wallet. The email thread discusses these questions in-depth, as well as opinions on the security and reliability of zero-conf transactions compared to credit card preauths or checks.Overall, the email conversations, discussions, and clarifications provide insight into how unconfirmed transactions are handled in bitcoin, as well as the use of full replace-by-fee and its potential impact on wallets and security. The ListTransaction RPC call is also explored in-depth, including questions about what it means for a transaction to be trusted, when confirmations can be conflicted, and what happens in cases of Transaction Malleability.
