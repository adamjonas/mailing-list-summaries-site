Summary:

The article focuses on using Proof of Work (PoW) to prevent Denial of Service (DoS) attacks in cryptocurrencies. While PoW has been used before, it did not work well against bots who have more patience than humans. The article suggests that users should be given the choice to prove their dedication through a connecting PoW challenge to find their way through a DoS-imposed partial outage. Three measures nodes can use for throttling misbehaving clients include scaling up connection PoW, throttling the connection on the work queue, and throttling the IP on the work queue. It is important to note that misclassification of reasonable behavior as harmful could lead to shooting oneself in the foot. Users should be notified when something unusual occurs.The article also discusses the use of cookies and Proof of UTXO as ways to rank users and combat DoS attacks, but these approaches have their drawbacks in terms of user privacy. Additionally, the conversation between Mike and Isidor touches on the impact of DKIM and PoW/hash-cash in email businesses and how they may relate to Bitcoin over Tor.The article proposes a PoW-based approach to prevent DoS attacks on Tor exit nodes for Bitcoin. The discussion revolves around whether requiring complicated work from all clients is the best solution, and it is suggested that only those using an address that has been misbehaving should be required to do the PoW. Banning Tor exit nodes completely may not be the best approach. Instead, throttling them using a PoW-based access control scheme could be more effective.The article also discusses the use of the same set of nodes for posting transactions using unrelated inputs limits the privacy improvement that can be gained from using unrelated inputs. An attacker may conclude that two distinct transactions originated from the same user if they come from the same set of 8 peers with unrelated Bitcoin addresses. It is suggested that only the same set of nodes should be used for posting a subsequent transaction when the input addresses are also the same, similar to how Tor uses different circuits for different hosts to connect to.Finally, Isidor proposes having both options available - allowing those who can solve the anti-DoS PoW to connect even if slower, while those who wish to run a weak client can choose to sacrifice privacy for connectivity and keep a cookie for connecting. The article concludes by suggesting that throttling misbehaving clients using a PoW-based access control scheme could be an effective solution to DoS attacks on Tor exit nodes for Bitcoin.
