Summary:

On the bitcoin-dev mailing list, Jonas Schnelli submitted two draft versions of Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs) regarding encryption and authentication of Bitcoin messages. The conversation revolved around the maximum message length that can be sent in an encrypted package/message in BIP. Lee suggested that the MAC length should be inferred from the cipher and authentication mode, rather than fixed. Jonas agreed with this and left the maximum message length up to the implementation. The BIPs involve p2p authentication for peers that would allow bloom filtering of transactions, encryption or any other node service that might lead to fingerprinting or resource attacks.Authentication would be required to selectively allow these services. Per message encryption is also discussed as a way to increase privacy and integrity, although there are concerns about smaller messages being vulnerable to statistical attacks. Responding peers must ignore - rather than ban - the requesting peer after five unsuccessfully authentication tries to avoid resource attacks. To ensure no message is dropped or blocked, communication must be hashed using SHA256, with both parties keeping the context of the encryption session.Other discussions on the mailing list include connecting SPV wallets to full nodes, bloom filtering in bitcoinj, and the need for basic encryption and authentication to be part of the protocol. Feedback and suggestions were given on the BIPs, including concerns about the implementation of a system that would ignore peers after 5 unsuccessful authentication tries, which could itself be open to resource attacks. Overall, the thread provides insights into the complexities of developing secure communication protocols for decentralized systems like Bitcoin.The conversation on the bitcoin-dev mailing list discusses proposed changes to the Bitcoin protocol, including encryption and authentication. The use of p2p code to connect a wallet to a node is deemed problematic due to privacy and security concerns, and an encrypted channel with a trusted full node is suggested as a solution for secure communication. The proposal for adding encryption involves negotiating encryption between two peers, and then encrypting all traffic between them. The negotiation process involves exchanging session public keys, verifying identities, and exchanging identity public keys with signatures of the session ID. Once the identities have been verified and communication is encrypted, there is protection against MITM attacks.The discussion also includes feedback on the encryption of messages in BIP, including the use of public keys for cold-storage key revocation, and the implementation of chacha20-poly1305 for AEAD. They also discuss the use of multiple keys to prevent implementation errors and handling failed authentication attempts. Finally, re-keying is discussed, with recommendations for resetting the message count and implementing bi-directional re-keying.Developers debated the use of an encrypted channel for secure Simplified Payment Verification (SPV) communication, with some arguing that a trusted full node and encrypted channel would provide a secure means of SPV communication. Suggestions were made to give encrypted messages their own port number, but this was met with reservations as it would require twice the bandwidth of the network. Overall, the conversation provides insights into the complexities of developing secure communication protocols for decentralized systems like Bitcoin. Feedback was welcome and the BIPs were still subject to change and heavy review before deployment.
