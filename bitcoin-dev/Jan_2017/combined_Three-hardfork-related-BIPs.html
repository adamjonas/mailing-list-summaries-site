Summary:

The Bitcoin community has been discussing various issues related to the network's scalability and security. One topic of discussion is the vulnerability of centralized nodes to follow anti-money laundering/know your customer (AML/KYC) rules, with concerns about the potential for a world of nodes in large data centers making it easy to force remaining Bitcoin nodes to comply with such regulations. However, some pointed out that countries like Iceland have specific laws to prevent government control over data and network traffic in data centers, making them more censorship-resistant.Another point of contention is the current block size limit of 1MB, with some arguing that it is too small and others arguing that it is too big. Luke Dashjr proposed a block size limit protocol change that would gradually increase the limit up to 31MB, but this proposal has faced criticism from some who argue that it could alienate miners and that the concept of spam transactions is not a real issue for them. Additionally, some members of the community have emphasized the importance of maintaining a peer-to-peer cash system rather than a settlement layer only available to the wealthiest and largest transactions.To address these issues, individuals have proposed various solutions, including blockchain pruning, limiting UTXO size, long latency UTXO commitment, and a fraud-proof system. Luke Dashjr has also put forth three hardfork-related BIPs to address the block size issue, although critics argue that his proposals may not be appropriate or effective. Ultimately, any solution will need to recognize that many people are not technically savvy and may be disinclined to use full nodes for Bitcoin transactions, and that running a full node requires significant resources.In relation to the maximum value for blocksize that the network can safely handle at any one time, researcher Andrew Johnson pointed out that the conclusion that the network can handle 4MB blocks today is a mischaracterisation of research conclusions. Johnson stated that the 4MB limit doesn't account for quadratic hashing for large transactions in large blocks. Additionally, in another discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, Luke Dashjr stated that Satoshi's vision of full nodes publishing proofs of invalid blocks for SPV nodes to verify is not possible. Dashjr believes that the only way for nodes to have full-node-security is to be a true full node, and having only full nodes in data centres is not realistic without centralising Bitcoin.Furthermore, Dashjr suggests that Zero-knowledge proofs are not helpful in the security of the system if applied naively. He explains that the missing data will mean part of the UTXO state can no longer be updated by other miners and thus they can't mine all transactions. This is why part of the economic pressure that users exert on miners is subverted by SPV/lite-clients.A discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list has also questioned why mining pools have not yet been required to implement AML/KYC regulations. Regulating mining is new and requires a good understanding of blockchains, and as Bitcoin grows regulated mining is likely to become a genuine risk.Bitcoin developer, Luke Dashjr has posted three hardfork-related BIPs on the bitcoin-dev mailing list; one of which is a block size limit protocol change that addresses criticisms of segwit. In an email exchange with Andrew Johnson, Dashjr argues that it is unlikely that the limit will drop all the way down to 300kb as it requires a hardfork consensus, software release, and activation by miners using BIP9. Dashjr suggests that such a drop would probably be good for the network in the long-term as it is necessary to maintain current first-time node synchronisation costs even with technological improvements.
