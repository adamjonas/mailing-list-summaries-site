Summary:

On April 24, 2015, Justus Ranvier proposed a new type of Bitcoin address called a "payment code" which positively identifies senders to recipients and provides for transaction refunds. It requires less blockchain data storage than stealth addresses, is SPV-friendly and can be associated with a real-life identity without compromising financial privacy.However, there are concerns around the "notification address" requirement that entails blockchain spam and address reuse. The proposal suggests using a one-to-one relationship between a payment code owner and their identity, which is a specific extended public/private key pair rather than a conventional understanding of identity. The sender will be freed from constantly monitoring an address known to be associated with their identity by providing alternate methods of notification such as Bitmessage notification.There were several email exchanges discussing the proposal, including concerns expressed by Luke Dashjr about the notification address requirement and its potential for address reuse and blockchain spam. He suggested a single zero-value OP_RETURN output with two pushes instead. However, Justus Ranvier acknowledged that one of the main goals is to be usable on as many mobile/light wallets as possible, and support for bloom filters or other querying methods is not as comprehensive as indexing by address.In a post dated October 22, 2015, Peter Todd announced that multi-push OP_RETURN outputs would become standard in version 0.12.0 of Bitcoin. He also expressed his desire to define a version 2 payment code that uses an improved notification system once the prerequisites for it are available on the network.The discussion revolves around ensuring privacy in SPV clients, with suggestions such as connecting exclusively to hidden services through Tor and broadcasting transactions through different peers than those used for checking balance. However, there are concerns about address reuse and blockchain spam.Luke Dashjr suggested using a single zero-value OP_RETURN output with two pushes instead of the notification transactions required by the payment code proposal. He also expressed his disagreement with the adoption of a BIP as it stands, arguing that the proposed changes were "mere software changes" and therefore indistinguishable from network-level changes which could affect users and wallet developers.Mike Hearn discussed the concept of identity in relation to payment codes and argued that mapping them to real-life identities should be limited to the application layer rather than the blockchain/network layer. Brian Deery expressed critiques and worries about payment codes, proposing ideas such as encoding the "x value" into the signature's R value to get rid of the op_return completely and using a common meeting point to trade off more work on the receiver's part to get some privacy.Overall, payment codes offer a promising alternative to existing Bitcoin addresses, but there are still concerns about privacy and the use of notifications transactions. The community is working towards finding solutions to these issues while also considering the limitations of current software and network capabilities.
