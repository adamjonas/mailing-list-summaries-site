Summary:

Bitcoin developers are discussing revisiting the replace-by-fee (RBF) policy outlined in BIP 125 due to difficulties with fee bumping when a transaction becomes pinned. A low-fee-rate sweep of one's payment created by a commercial service can make it infeasible to RBF the original transaction, even though the fee rate is low. Russell O'Connor proposes changing rules 3 and 4 of the BIP 125 policy to address this issue, but acknowledges that his proposal may be naive. Peter Todd suggests using the effective fee rate of the maximum feerate package that can be built from the set of transactions beginning with the candidate replacement instead of rule 3, but it may not be trivial to calculate. The discussion also mentions a restriction on spending unconfirmed outputs to solve the problem. Another issue discussed is the current rule requiring transactions being replaced to pay not only the relay fee but also the absolute fee, which can be cost-prohibitive. Todd suggests diffed tx replacement propagation as an alternative solution.The email thread also discusses relaxing some of the Denial of Service (DoS) prevention rules to open up more use cases and adoption. The weakening of DoS protections is not considered a huge problem, and it is proposed to try relaxing some of the rules in a release and see what happens. In addition, the discussion touched on monitoring, confirming, and rebroadcasting missing transactions in a way that is safe against reorgs. Peter Todd asked how many wallets had implemented CPFP fee bumps for incoming transactions. Furthermore, there was a debate about institutional wallets sweeping unconfirmed outputs, which is potentially problematic. Todd and O'Connor discussed the possibility of solving the problem of replacing transactions that have been offered a new transaction with a higher fee rate. Todd expressed his belief that any attempt to replace a transaction without paying the fees would be vulnerable to attack. However, O'Connor still believes that pursuing the proposed change in RBF policy is worthwhile as the current policy is problematic in practice for normal transactions.Developers also debated the issue of "pinned" transactions in relation to BIP 125, which requires not only the relay of the transactions being thrown away but also the absolute fee. Some suggested a diffed tx replacement propagation solution as a better alternative. In addition, Peter Todd expressed his opinion that it is not possible to solve the problem of unconfirmed payments, but acknowledged the benefits of relaxed restrictions. On February 14, 2018, there was a discussion about the computation of package-fee rates of mempool transactions. The idea was to reuse the calculation already being done for Child-Pays-for-Parent (CPFP) in order to compute the required value. Furthermore, developers debated the issue of "pinned" transactions in relation to BIP 125. It was suggested that a diffed tx replacement propagation solution is a better alternative to limit spends of unconfirmed outputs.Additionally, the discussion touched on miner incentives and developing a reasonable standard RBF policy that aligns with those incentives. O'Connor suggests changing rules three and four to address this issue. Rule three should instead pay a fee rate of at least the effective fee rate of any chain of transactions from the set of original transactions that begins with the root of the original transaction set, while rule four should also pay for replacing the original transactions at or above the rate set by the same fee setting. However, some have stated that O'Connor's proposal could be naive. Peter Todd also expressed concerns about relaxing anti-DoS protections. Nonetheless, there was a discussion about developing a reasonable standard RBF policy that aligns with miner incentives and is robust against possible DoS vectors.
