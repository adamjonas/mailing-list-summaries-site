The Bitcoin community has been discussing possible solutions to the ongoing issue of branding attacks by fork coins. One proposal is to amend the MIT license terms to disincentivize the use of the word "Bitcoin" by fork coins. However, some argue that social and marketing-based solutions would be more effective. There are also concerns about legal uncertainty and technical incompatibility with older versions of Bitcoin or existing independent blockchain implementations.Another suggestion has been made to limit cryptocurrencies with the same Proof of Work to ensure the stability and security of Bitcoin, but there is opposition due to potential branding attacks. Custom open-source licenses can create compliance issues for organizations trying to use software. The discussion highlights the challenges associated with balancing the need for brand protection with the principles of free and open-source software development.During the discussions, an unknown individual expressed disapproval towards those who are trying to change the fundamental principles of open source due to personal reasons and emphasizes the importance of maintaining the integrity of open source. Damian Williamson disagrees with any changes to the current MIT license, while Cory Fields suggests focusing on copyright litigation and trademark dilution concerns.Specifically, the Bitcoin-dev mailing list has been discussing a proposal to amend the MIT license of the Bitcoin software to prevent confusion among users and reduce harm caused by multiple hard forks. The proposed amendment suggests that no other projects using the name "Bitcoin" can include any part of the Bitcoin Core software unless it is fully compatible with the Bitcoin (Core) blockchain. However, some members of the community argue that social and marketing-based solutions should be used instead of legal ones. Others believe that trademarks would be a better solution, but raise concerns about legal uncertainty and how it would affect those forking older versions of Bitcoin or existing independent blockchain implementations.Furthermore, there is frustration over ongoing FUD and scammery going unanswered in any "official" capacity. Some suggest that a legal solution could keep some open source aspects of the code. Jameson Lopp argues that social and marketing-based efforts should be used to fight branding attacks on Bitcoin, rather than relying on courts or governments. Ultimately, the discussion highlights the challenges associated with balancing the need for brand protection with the principles of free and open-source software development.
