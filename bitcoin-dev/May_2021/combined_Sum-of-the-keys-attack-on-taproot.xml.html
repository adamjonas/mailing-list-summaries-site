Summary:

Alice's ownership of taproot address and public key "a*G" along with private key "a" raises concerns about potential attacks from malicious actors. Bob, who knows "a*G" by exploring the chain and looking for P2TR outputs, creates a taproot address "(b-a)*G" and sends a small amount to it, in an attempt to steal Alice's funds. To accomplish this, Bob creates a transaction with two inputs, taking coins from "a*G" and "(b-a)*G" addresses, requiring a signature matching the sum of the public keys used in taproot, which is "(a+b-a)*G", reduced to "b*G". One solution to protect against such attacks could be using multisignature setup or a timelock, where both Alice and Bob need to sign off on any transaction and wait for a certain period before allowing any transactions. Another option is using coin control features to ensure that her coins are not vulnerable to such attacks. In a discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, vjudeu suggested that producing a signature matching the sum of public keys used in taproot would be sufficient. However, Tim Ruffing corrected this statement, stating that taproot does not enable cross-input aggregation or spending multiple UTXOs with a single signature. Ruben added to the conversation by suggesting reading about MuSig, a key aggregation scheme using Schnorr signatures. The link provided leads to a blog post on Blockstream's website explaining the details of MuSig. It is crucial for users to be aware of these potential attacks and take proper precautions to safeguard their assets.
