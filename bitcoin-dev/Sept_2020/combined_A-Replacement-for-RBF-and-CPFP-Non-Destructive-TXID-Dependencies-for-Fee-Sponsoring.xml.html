Summary:

A draft proposal has been shared with Bitcoin developers to replace Child Pays for Parent (CPFP) and Replace-by-Fee (RBF) for fee bumping. The proposed mechanism involves defining a sponsor vector with specific rules and aims to provide a general specification for inter-transaction dependencies that restricts the mempool policy while ensuring a minimal mechanism that allows arbitrary unconnected third parties to attach fees to an arbitrary transaction. However, there are concerns about potential attacks on the proposed mechanism, such as garbage sponsors and concurrent states being pinned, and policies have been suggested to prevent them.The lack of a global mempool means that there may be unintended consequences for second-layer protocol developers, and further modifications may be needed to solve class b) of pinnings for multi-party time-sensitive protocols. Discussions suggest improving the current policy rules to make CPFP work better without a consensus change instead of introducing complex interaction with third parties. One suggestion made during the conversation is to have sponsor vectors point to input outpoints rather than transaction IDs, which would allow miners to include the sponsor transaction in any block that has a transaction with an input of the specified outpoint. There are also discussions about the limitations of using feerate-based package relay to solve RBF transaction pinning, and one suggestion to simplify package relay with sponsors is to do a sponsor-only package relay limited to two transactions: one sponsor and one sponsoree.The conversation also discusses potential attacks on the proposed mechanism, such as garbage sponsors and concurrent states being pinned, and suggests policies to prevent them. To prevent garbage sponsors, the proposal requires that the sponsor's feerate must be greater than the sponsored's ancestor fee rate. The serialization to support both input outpoints and txids can be done in a way that permits future extensibility. Overall, the proposed mechanism seeks to replace CPFP and RBF for increasing fees on transactions in the mempool and protect the network from denial of service attacks. However, there are concerns about potential attacks and unintended consequences, and further modifications may be needed to achieve the desired security and semantic for Lightning type of protocols.The conversation also discusses the use of sponsor transactions in off-chain protocols. The idea is that by sponsoring an outpoint, participants can increase the feerate of the package locally known without assuming about the concrete state. However, a malicious counterparty can still broadcast a heavy-weighted transaction and prevent the sponsoree from being timely confirmed. This is a function of package malleability allowed by the off-chain protocol. Jeremy expresses concern about a potential attack on Bitcoin's mempool pinning mechanism and suggests policies to prevent it. The proposal aims to solve class a) of pinnings by allowing fee-bumping with a new definition of dependencies. However, there are concerns that the Sponsor feerate is part of this commitment and cannot be unilaterally adjusted to actual mempool-congestion. The email conversation also discusses the proposal of a new type of transaction known as "Sponsors" that aims to allow third parties to attach themselves to the transaction graph of other parties and participate in fee bumping. The proposal is met with some skepticism as it breaks the principle that once a valid transaction is created, it should not become invalid later on unless the inputs are double-spent. However, the author argues that a consensus change may be required to achieve this mechanism because CPFP-based protocols are complex and suffer from chain bloat issues and composes poorly when used recursively.
