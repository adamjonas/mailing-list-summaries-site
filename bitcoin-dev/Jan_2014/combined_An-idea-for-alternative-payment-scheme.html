Summary:

The discussion revolves around various payment schemes using key derivation to ensure privacy while making payments. Nadav Ivgi suggested a scheme where the payer derives an address from the payee's master public key and then sends the receipt to the payee, who derives a private key with it and adds it to their wallet. This scheme has an advantage over plain payment protocol intended use in that they allow the first round of communication to be broadcast while preserving privacy. However, there are downsides to this scheme as well. The issue of address reuse can be avoided with this scheme, but there is a risk of losing access to funds if the receipt numbers are lost. Additionally, the scheme requires a better-defined channel than email to send the receipt to the payee and prevent data loss events that could result in the loss of money for the recipient.Adam Back points out that this scheme lacks Simple Payment Verification (SPV) compatibility. He suggests that the proposal has potential for Simple Payment Verification (SPV) compatibility and could offer better alternatives than other approaches, such as BIP38. These approaches force users to either accept higher bandwidth consumption, decrease anonymity set size, or lose payments. Adam further discusses proposals that allow for receiving unlimited unrelated payments with a single prefix query in conjunction with prefix filters. Another wild idea proposed by Gregory Maxwell involves the payee publishing two public keys, PP and PP2, and the payer picking a k value for signatures. The multiplier of PP2*k is paid to pubkey PP+n with r in the first signature or in an OP_RETURN additional output. The particular way specified breaks using deterministic DSA unless you use the OP_RETURN. The message also discusses the use of deterministic signatures, cautioning that the master root would need to be stored on the buyer's computer. The discussion concludes with Adam Back planning to publish a post discussing this proposal in more detail. He also plans to provide an overview of bloom filters vs. prefix filters, with the argument that bloom filters have poor scalability and should be depreciated.
