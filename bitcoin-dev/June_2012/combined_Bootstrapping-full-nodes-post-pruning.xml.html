Summary:

The discussion centers around concerns regarding the use of BerkeleyDB (BDB) in Bitcoin, as it lacks inter-version compatibility and space efficiency. Additionally, BDB files are not deterministic, which is problematic for read-only pruned indexes. To address this concern, a method of shipping pruned chains in a deterministic process to produce archival chains has been proposed. This method suggests having a client download a binary containing pre-indexed and pruned chain up to a certain block and then deleting data files before running with "-syncto=180000 -prune" and checking file hashes against what was downloaded. However, it is necessary to verify that the binaries match the source to avoid potential issues.In 2012, Mike Hearn expressed concerns about introducing unauditable single source material, as it would increase the attractiveness of compromising developers. He suggested having a deterministic process to produce archival chains and then introducing commitments to them in the blockchain. Archival nodes that never prune and encourage people to run them were also proposed. The discussion also considers the possibility of swapping out BDB for LevelDB, although it is uncertain how deterministic LevelDB is.The issue of bootstrapping newly installed nodes for Bitcoin is also discussed. Three options are suggested: having special archival nodes that never prune, shipping a post-pruning block chain and transaction index with client downloads, or using a combination of both approaches. While some individuals, like Greg, are not in favor of shipping pre-indexed files due to the absence of indexing overhead, it speeds up the startup process for new users. It is important to find a solution that balances the benefits and drawbacks of each approach to ensure that new nodes can be easily bootstrapped.
