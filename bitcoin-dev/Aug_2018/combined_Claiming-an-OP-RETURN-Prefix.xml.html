Summary:

The bitcoin-dev mailing list recently discussed the use of op_return prefixes for storing data on the blockchain. Christopher Allen cautioned against using an op_return prefix due to its potential for transaction censorship and instead suggested responsible ways to publish the hash, such as having it in the witness script data. Lautaro Dragan from Po.et reached out seeking clarification on claiming an OP_RETURN prefix, but Peter Todd recommended avoiding a prefix altogether for censorship resistance and anonymity reasons. The conversation also delved into the possibility of miners censoring transactions based on software and whether choosing not to mine transactions is censorship or not.The discussion also touched on the use of IPFS hashes in op_returns and the need for responsible ways to publish them to minimize the ability of a miner to identify the transaction. There have been suggestions for having the hash in the witness script data, but there has yet to be a solid proposal for best practices. Additionally, the thread discussed the possibility of miners identifying transactions from specific software and censoring them, with some arguing that this could be considered censorship politically if not economically.Lautaro Dragan also sought guidance on using colored coins to store data on the Bitcoin blockchain with a prefix, but there is no formal or standard process to claim it, and there is no existing BIP claiming an OP_RETURN prefix. Suggestions were made to collaborate with the authors of BIP 160 or write a new BIP if BIP 160 is insufficient, but BIPs need to specify an actual protocol, not just claim a prefix. Ultimately, it was recommended to avoid using a prefix altogether for technical reasons.The discussion thread also covered other topics such as pruning op_return outputs, timestamping with pay 2 contract, and compatibility issues between batching methods used by OpenTimestamps and IPFS directories. In response to a question raised by Jude Nelson on the bitcoin-dev mailing list about whether miners can identify transactions coming from specific software and censor them, it was noted that files are not uploaded to IPFS until transactions have been settled with several confirmations. This means that miners cannot identify transactions by their hashes until after they have already been confirmed and added to the blockchain. Luke Dashjr also pointed out that choosing not to mine transactions is not censorship.
