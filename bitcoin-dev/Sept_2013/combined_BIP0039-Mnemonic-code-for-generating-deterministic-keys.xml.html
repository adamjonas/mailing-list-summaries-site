Summary:

On September 10, 2013, slush sent an email to the Bitcoin-development mailing list announcing the finalization of the draft and reference implementation of BIP39. The goal was to standardize algorithms across various clients and fix some design problems of existing but not yet standardized Electrum mnemonic algorithm. BIP39 is seen as a complement to BIP32, which allows users to (paper) backup and share their wallet across multiple clients easily. In an email exchange, Matthew Mitchell suggested removing aggressive words from the python-mnemonic wordlist used in BIPs. Pavol Rusnak revisited the wordlist and replaced around 67 words that can be offensive in some context.A proposal for a superior system to BIP39 was suggested by Pieter a year ago. The scheme has built-in configurable difficulty and checksums, so the word set being used doesn't need to function for checking anymore. Any dictionary/language/text generator can be used and fed into the system, as the software on the other side doesn't need to use the same dictionary. However, it cannot encode arbitrary data, but it does have some theoretical advantages. Jorge Timón raised a concern regarding the use of mnemonics, which aims to easily remember information. He believes that removing offensive words from the list is counterproductive as these words have a greater emotional impact on human moral psyches. The developers of a cryptocurrency wallet are open to changes in their wordlist and will accept pull requests replacing potentially offensive words with more neutral ones that fit all other requirements. Despite concerns raised by one individual about the inclusion of words like "murder, black, people" in the wordlist, these words were also found in another widely-used wordlist without any complaints. In an email exchange, Matthew Mitchell expresses concern about potentially offensive or threatening words being generated by a tool that combines a mnemonic code generator with an NLP engine to output a short, humorous story as a passphrase. Gregory Maxwell suggests using mechanical turk to rate the words for potential offensiveness and recommends using neutral nouns rather than place names which may have political complications.The email thread also explores other proposals regarding mnemonic codes and NLP engines to create more memorable yet secure passphrases. Another individual expresses interest in combining an NLP engine with a mnemonic code generator to output a short story as a passphrase, with the key encoded within the story itself. The author questions the effectiveness of removing offensive words from mnemonics, arguing that these words are more memorable due to their emotional impact. They propose that using generally offensive words, especially when combined with each other, could optimize the "maximum unforgettableness" criterion. However, this proposal goes against the trend of removing such words and may not be well-received by users.The Bitcoin community has reflected on the BIP39 wordlist and changes have been made to improve it by removing offensive words, implementing an algorithm to detect words with similar characters, and resolving duplicities. Password protection of seed and seed hardening using Rijndael cipher has also been added. The blocksize can be 128, 192 or 256 bits, which fits the desired length of seeds.
