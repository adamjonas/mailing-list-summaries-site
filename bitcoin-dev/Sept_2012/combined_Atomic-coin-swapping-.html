Summary:

Jeff Garzik, the founder of exMULTI, Inc., discussed the potential use of colored coins in distributed bonds on Bitcointalk. He posed a problem where Alice transfers a 1-satoshi colored coin to Bob, who then transfers 100 BTC to Alice in an atomic unit with both parties approving the transfer through appropriate signatures. Garzik wondered if this was possible within the current bitcoin system and suggested that it may require a new SIGHASH_* type. The author of the post expressed interest in transitive/multi-hop transactions with colored coins, but did not understand why it was not possible. In another conversation, Jorge Timón raised a question regarding the impossibility of signing input scripts for transitive/multi-hop transactions with colored coins. Jeff Garzik responded, stating that a new SIGHASH_* type was needed to achieve what they wanted. Garzik also mentioned pybond as a solution to address the issue of atomic transfer of coins between two parties with approval from both parties.The default SIGHASH_ALL indicates that everything about the transaction is signed, except for the input scripts, which are always blanked out to avoid making it impossible to construct a transaction. Signing input scripts would make it impossible to construct a transaction, as signatures cannot sign themselves. On September 22, 2012, Mike Hearn confirmed this in a discussion with Jeff Garzik and stated that SIGHASH_ALL was the crucial piece he was missing. Garzik updated his post with a concrete example and detailed instructions for programmers to recreate. In an email conversation on the same day, Jorge Timón expressed his interest in transitive/multi-hop transactions using Ripple with colored coins and wondered why it was not possible. Jeff Garzik replied, saying that the crucial piece he was missing was SIGHASH_ALL.
