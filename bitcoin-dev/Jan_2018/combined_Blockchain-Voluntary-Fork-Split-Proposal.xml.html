Summary:

A solution to the scalability issue of blockchain is being discussed in the Bitcoin-dev mailing list. The proposal involves a voluntary split of the blockchain into two or more blockchains, which would double the capacity of the original blockchain. This would be achieved by hard forking the blockchain into two almost identical chains (e.g. blockchain A and B) with some identifiers to distinguish them. Coins on one blockchain cannot be sent to the other, but everyone gets double bitcoins, each with half the value of an original bitcoin. The proposal is considered backward compatible with old version transactions and creates a blocktree as new blocks are added to the leaves, providing even more capacity. When sending coins, the wallet should randomly select one blockchain and try to send through only one. The proposal will not cause dilution of anyone's bitcoin assets, and the total supply can remain the same. The discussion also delves into the question of what enforces that bitcoin A is worth the same as bitcoin B, and whether they are allowed to eventually diverge in price. According to Chaofan Li, if the two chains’ algorithms are the same except for some identifiers (e.g., btc.0 btc.1), they have no reason to have different value, and the market will adjust the value. Erik Aronesty via bitcoin-dev explains that all splits are voluntary, and without enforcement, liquidity will diverge. The inspiration behind this proposal comes from stock splits, where the price halves, but the market capitalization remains the same. This means there is no dilution of each shareholder's total assets. The larger network of money is more useful due to the cost of exchange between them, which can only be eliminated by one absorbing the network of the other. Thiers' law states that in the absence of currency controls, the more useful money will get used, and this does not mean they will become the same value. However, eventually, the better money will absorb the lesser. Although perpetual creation of new monies with exchange between them and the best money (largest network) could exist, layering proposes an approach that doesn’t require all merchants to perpetually be accepting different monies. Bitcoin's rising use implies rising fees, which in turn reduces usefulness (stability property). While the better money prices out certain scenarios, they remain viable in the lesser money. But eventually, the better money will absorb the lesser.
