Bitcoin developer Gregory Maxwell proposed Taproot, a new approach to Merkelized Abstract Syntax Trees (MAST) that would make smart contracts indistinguishable from regular payments. Taproot supports any number of participants and can be made indistinguishable from 1-of-1 using MuSig. There are concerns about paying directly to a public key instead of a public key hash, but the proposal is generally well-received. The email thread discusses the use of merkelized scripts on bitcoin-dev. The main benefits of MAST are efficiency and privacy. The author argues that any contract with a fixed, finite participant set can be represented as an OR between an N-of-N and a more complex contract. There are concerns about quantum attacks against ECC, but hashing provided for quantum resistance may not be as effective as previously thought. Direct SPK use can enable efficient ZKP over the UTXO set for private solvency proofs, although it isn't mandatory.Regarding the security of Bitcoin addresses in light of quantum computing advances, there are debates on whether to use public keys or pubkey hashes in the Taproot proposal. While hashing provides some level of quantum resistance, it may not be meaningful enough. Discussions on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list have proposed various solutions to combat the potential threat of quantum computing attacks on Bitcoin. One approach is to use quantum-safe zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP), although this would likely require a hardfork to apply to old transactions. Alternatively, the Fawkes signature scheme and the Taproot delegating CHECKSIG proposal by Gregory Maxwell have been suggested as sufficient for the foreseeable future. The bitcoin-dev mailing list has been discussing various proposals and concerns related to the interception and insertion of commitments in transactions in the blockchain. One proposal involves committing H(classic_pk, tx) and tx to the blockchain and waiting for confirmation before revealing classic_pk in the blockchain. Another proposed solution is Taproot, a delegating CHECKSIG that can make the special case of a top-level "threshold-signature OR arbitrary-conditions" indistinguishable from a normal one-party signature without any overhead at all. In addition, there are debates about whether paying directly to a public key instead of a public key hash in Bitcoin is a step backwards in terms of resistance to quantum attacks against ECC. The bitcoin community is currently discussing the adoption of Taproot, which has been proposed by Gregory Maxwell as a superior approach to a native-MAST template.
