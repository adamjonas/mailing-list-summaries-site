Summary:

There are ongoing discussions on the bitcoin-dev mailing list regarding issues with block space and transaction fees. One concern is that miners may include their own spam transactions in their blocks, which incurs a cost but gains them nothing. To discourage this practice, a proposal was made to reward the fees of the current block to the miner of the next block (or X blocks after the current one). However, some experts have pointed out that this proposal is not incentive compatible, as miners prefer alternative mechanisms for receiving fees.Another issue being debated is block space ownership. Eric Voskuil argued that miners have the right to sell or not sell block space since it belongs to them, while Gregory Maxwell countered that Bitcoin users collectively pay miners around $130k USD per block in the form of inflation for complying with the Bitcoin protocol. Maxwell further explained that reducing returns to miners would result in a reduction of hash power, rather than a decrease in transaction costs.A proposal has been made to discourage miners from flooding their own blocks with high fee transactions. This solution suggests rewarding the fees of the current block to the miner of the next block (or X blocks after the current one) and enforcing miners to set an anyone-can-spend output in the coinbase transaction of the block. However, there are concerns about the possible downside of miners not getting any rewards for mining blocks when the fork is activated.It was also noted that optimal sets of transactions can earn miners more fees, but miners can still leave out transactions without retaliation. There are flaws in the premise of fee recovery and side fees, and underfilled blocks are produced without any apparent consequences. In summary, while proposals have been made to address issues such as miners filling up blocks with high fee transactions to increase recommended fees, there are still concerns regarding incentive compatibility and alternative mechanisms for paying fees.
