Summary:

A recent discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list has raised concerns about the future of Bitcoin's difficulty level. Jefferson Carpenter suggested that Moore's Law could max out the difficulty within 400 years, potentially leading to losses for many. However, Peter Todd disputed this theory, arguing that if Moore's Law were to last that long, mining Bitcoin blocks would require unimaginable energy levels. In fact, Todd believes that once we reach such a point, the problem becomes the speed of light and we would run out of energy in our 10 minute light radius. As a result, Todd concluded that this matter belongs to science fiction writers rather than bitcoin-dev.Despite Todd's skepticism, some are still calling for a plan to be made in case Bitcoin does indeed reach the 400-year mark. This would involve migrating to a PoW that supports an even higher difficulty level. At present, it remains unclear if any such plans are in place. While there are potential concerns about Bitcoin's future difficulty level, the issue seems far off and somewhat speculative. It is suggested that a plan should be made today for when Bitcoin reaches the 400-year mark. This is because the lifetime of crypto algorithms, including consensus, involves trade-offs and it's the job of a dev team to examine those trade-offs and come up with an optimal solution. Additionally, cryptocurrencies and blockchains will require cryptography updates every decade or so to maintain security.One proposal to address the potential difficulty issue is to fork off a blockchain that accepts Bitcoin blocks with less than max difficulty, making it a soft fork. If the difficulty of a block is set to max, then the difficulty field is extended so that it represents a higher max difficulty under a different hashing function like SHA512, making it a hard fork. This blockchain will be identical to Bitcoin until the difficulty goes too high, at which point it diverges. However, transitioning from the current SHA256 to a higher-difficulty hashing function could be difficult. Bitcoin is currently the dominant cryptocurrency due to being the first mover and winning the game of inventing cryptocurrency, but it has a built-in end date that may result in a shift towards other cryptocurrencies. In order to mitigate this potential issue, planning for the future could be done today.
