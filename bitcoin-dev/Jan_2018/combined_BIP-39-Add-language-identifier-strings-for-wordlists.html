Summary:

The bitcoin-dev mailing list has been discussing the use of multilingual support in BIP39, which is the standard for mnemonic codes used to backup private keys. Some members have raised concerns about the lack of support for non-English languages and proposed a multilingual BIP39 wordlist, which has been implemented by Copay and its clones. However, this feature is not widely used and is not supported by major wallets. To address this issue, there is a proposal to enhance the BIP39 standard by adding canonical native language strings to identify each wordlist, short ASCII language codes, and creating a new standard where words are mapped to integers instead of literal strings.There is also a debate on the use of a wordlist versus a 32B hex string backup, with concerns raised about lock-in and the potential for users to lose access to their coins. The conversation emphasizes the importance of considering the needs of non-English speaking users when designing standards for cryptocurrency wallets. Some users have suggested mapping existing ISO/UTF language character standards to the seeds rather than limiting them to English.Furthermore, the discussion highlights the need to simplify the process of securing coins as some wallets have implemented non-standard or difficult-to-understand features. There is also confusion around the purpose of BIP32 and the use of multisig wallets. The context provides various resources related to privacy and security online, including tools for encrypting and protecting data, Zcash wallets, Bitcoin wallets, dynamic blocklists, passwords lists, anti-spies, and private torrents.In addition, the conversation touches on the potential loss of funds due to the lack of support across multiple wallet implementations, and the restriction of the standard to English only would exclude people who do not want to or cannot use Latin characters and words from the English language. This has led to a call for a survey to determine what people actually need and understand when it comes to securing their coins.Finally, the author proposes a new standard where words are mapped to integers, with a mapping from original language words to matching English words. They also suggest creating a better English dictionary for this new standard, with unique 4-character prefixes and no repeated words. The discussion highlights the importance of considering the needs of non-English speaking users when designing cryptocurrency wallet standards and suggests conducting a worldwide survey to determine these needs. Links to various GitHub pages relating to Bitcoin transactions, Zcash wallets, and Bitcoin wallets were also shared by the original poster.
