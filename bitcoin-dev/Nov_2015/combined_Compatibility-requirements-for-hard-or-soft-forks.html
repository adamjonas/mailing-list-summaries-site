Summary:

In November 2015, the bitcoin-dev mailing list was active with discussions about Bitcoin transaction version numbers and guidelines, as well as the threshold of reasonableness for OP_CAT scripts which require terabytes of RAM to validate. Gavin Andresen raised a question regarding the requirement of validating one-megabyte transactions under new rules, arguing that it could be an unacceptable confiscation of funds if expensive-to-validate transactions created and given a lockTime in the future were made invalid. Rusty suggested that a transaction that would never have been generated by any known software should be the minimum bar for "reasonable certainty". On the other hand, Justus Ranvier emphasized the importance of making it clear that non-standard transactions and scripts may become invalid in the future. The discussion also touched upon who should decide the standards for Bitcoin usage, with Luke-Jr clarifying that it is not the developers but rather the miners who make this decision.The discussion on Bitcoin-dev mailing list suggests the importance of creating an informational BIP (Bitcoin Improvement Proposal) to ensure safe use of Bitcoin. According to Tier Nolan's message, guidelines for this proposal include increasing transaction version numbers when possible, considering transactions with unknown/large version numbers as unsafe to use with locktime, and providing reasonable notice before implementing changes. Non-opt-in changes should only be made to protect the network's integrity. Transactions that can be validated without overburdening the network are considered safe to use, even if they are non-standard.In a similar thread, Rusty Russell suggests that surpassing the minimum benchmark of "reasonable certainty" would involve adding redundancies such as OP_CHECKSIG and clear, widespread notification of change.Bitcoin developers are discussing the definition of "locked transactions", which may refer to P2SH outputs, but this is an unusual use of terminology given that outputs and transactions are distinct concepts. Gavin Andresen supported the guidelines provided for Bitcoin but pointed out that there could be arguments over what constitutes "reasonable notice" and what fits under "protects the integrity of the network". If every consensus rule change had been accompanied by a version number bump, it would have been possible to phase out old versions without invalidating signed-but-unbroadcast transactions.Finally, discussions took place around the issue of short-term changes to the Bitcoin protocol as a moderation rule. The pro argument highlights the possibility of expensive-to-validate transactions with a lockTime in the future that may have no other way of spending the funds. On the other hand, the con argument suggests it is unlikely such large, timelocked transactions exist due to the Core code's policy. It is proposed that the requirement should be relaxed to only valid 100,000-byte transactions under old consensus rules.
