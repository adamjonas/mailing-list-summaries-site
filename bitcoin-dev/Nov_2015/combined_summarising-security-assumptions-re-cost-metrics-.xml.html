Summary:

Bitcoin's security model has been extensively discussed on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list. The role of economically dependent full nodes, known as validators, in enforcing consensus rules and ensuring network security was emphasized. It was noted that there is a trade-off between weak miner decentralization and good validator decentralization, and it is crucial to maintain a balance between the two. Best practices include keeping things simple and avoiding complex optimization problems. The use of APIs in Bitcoin was also debated, with some arguing that they contribute to reducing network security for everyone over time, while others believe they are beneficial for decentralization. However, it was agreed that power users and businesses using APIs instead of running a full node must be clear about the API and what security they are delegating to a third party. It was also noted that the presence of dozens of blockexplorer APIs makes them interchangeable, contributing to decentralization.In addition, the concept of key-holder decentralization was discussed, with clarification sought on its definition and meaning. It refers to both the absolute number of key-holders and the number of transactions they make. The Lightning Network was also considered as a means to handle excess transaction demand.It was observed that small miners should not be significantly disadvantaged for Bitcoin's security, and a trade-off exists between weak miner decentralization and good validator decentralization. An incremental approach may be useful in simplifying and arriving at a consensus more quickly. To maintain a balance, there should be no significant collusion advantages that create policy centralization as a side-effect.Overall, it is important to ensure the security of the Bitcoin network by considering all aspects of its design and requirements. While consensus rule enforced is the most secure defense, economic alignment, overt attacks, and meta-incentives can also serve as defenses. Best practices include simplicity and an incremental approach to deployment.
