Summary:

The context discusses the Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs) and their purpose of providing a stable system environment for programmers to work towards an accepted standard and serving as an equalizer for smaller groups. The author also mentions the challenges of rule creep and bureaucracy that can arise from too many small rules. The discussion revolves around BIP_0014 which specifies software stacks rather than end applications, and the potential violation of it by Gavin's recent commit that uses the same version string for the GUI interface and the daemon mode. There is ambiguity in the interpretation of BIP language regarding what is permitted versus encouraged versus required, and the author recommends using the RFC2119 keywords where appropriate to avoid such confusion.Luke-Jr and Amir Taaki were discussing the acceptance of bitcoin URI in the standard. Amir had previously contacted IANA and had to read five large documents as a prerequisite. He did not end up writing the RFC. Luke also contacted IANA but never heard back. In December 2011, Amir Taaki suggested using a hierarchical method to map browser user agents in order to make it easier to scan all browsers using WebKit. He believes that developers want to follow standards but are held back by inflexibility or ambiguity. The proposed method allows every client to supply all the information they have without needing to decide what to leave out. Dr. Andy Parkins provided his email address and signature at the end of the message.The author suggests splitting bitcoin-qt into two parts and explains how following a similar approach as Mozilla Firefox's application suite can be useful.
