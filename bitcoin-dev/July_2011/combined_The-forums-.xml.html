Summary:

In July 2011, discussions were held regarding the Bitcoin forums and their usefulness. John Smith proposed that the link to the forums be removed from bitcoin.org and replaced with a new forum focused solely on helping users, merchants, and developers with Bitcoin-related issues without engaging in political or economic speculation. However, there were concerns about the lack of manpower to moderate such a forum.Jeff Garzik agreed with Smith's concerns and suggested that the forum should ideally be used as a way for developers to communicate with actual users and solicit feedback on user interface features. There were also talks about killing the forum.bitcoin.org DNS entry altogether due to illegal content being posted on the forums. Douglas Huff suggested killing the DNS entry for forum.bitcoin.org, and Jeff Garzik hoped that Sirius would handle moving forum.bitcoin.org to forum.bitcointalk.org. However, he was the only one with keys to the bitcoin.org DNS, creating a potential obstacle in making the change. There were suggestions about renaming the development project to a new URL, but some opposed it due to the name "Bitcoin" being well-known, even though many people misunderstand its meaning.Additionally, discussions took place about maintaining forums and the possibility of moving them to another domain due to their growth into a monstrosity. Vladimir launched a Bitcoin community portal at https://bitcoin.org.uk, featuring advanced forum functionality, a blogging platform, a community calendar, and chat capabilities. It is professionally hosted on high-end dedicated servers running FreeBSD in a data center located in Amsterdam, NL. The site is already functional and open for registration, and Vladimir welcomes any suggestions and feedback. Some members of the community were annoyed by the existing forums and suggested that they be removed from Bitcoin.org and replaced with a new forum focused solely on helping users, merchants, and developers with Bitcoin-related issues without engaging in political or economic speculation. The writer of the context advocated for the Bitcoin Stack-Exchange to be the main user/merchant support site due to its defined acceptable and unacceptable topics and good user moderation/reputation system. However, as of writing, the site still needed 64 more people to commit to using it before it could launch, with 48 of those needing to be established Stack Exchange users.In a related conversation, Matt Corallo expressed his negative opinion about online forums, calling them a "cesspool." There were also concerns about the impact of active members on the Bitcoin forums on the project's direction, leading to perceptions among developers that they were viewed as political extremists. However, it was suggested that the forums should ideally be used to communicate with actual users, such as soliciting feedback on UI features. A simpler forum structure was proposed that would be easier to moderate if enough people had moderation power. Two categories could be created: Technical Support and Development, and if there was a large enough group in a certain country willing to maintain order, these categories could be replicated per language.Ultimately, the solution to the capacity issue involved removing the link to the current forums and coming up with alternative media to address user issues, such as a moderated mailing list. In an email, the writer requested the removal of a forum link associated with illegal activity, including someone offering to sell a bitcoin mining virus. The issue is considered a new level of bad PR as comments on the forum show support for the illegal activity. Rob suggested that the development project should move to a new URL instead, while Matt Corallo believes that removing the link and coming up with an alternative medium to address user issues, such as a moderated mailing list, would solve the capacity issue.
