Summary:

In May 2013, several proposals related to committed transactions in Bitcoin were discussed by developers on the Bitcoin-development mailing list. One proposal was made by Adam Back, who suggested using fixed size committed coin spends with blind-sender, auth-tag, and encrypted-tx-commit for better cryptography. The proposal aimed to prevent dishonest mining and fix issues related to arbitrary policies being imposed by a quorum of more than 50% miners.Another writer proposed that committed transactions could potentially help Bitcoin scale since they are more compact than regular transactions. However, there were anti-DOS complications which could be avoided by mixing blinded and unblinded inputs. Gregory Maxwell also proposed an interesting idea for a coin spending system that involved creating txouts that pay P2SH addresses with an OP_PUSH nonce in them. The recipient of the coins would be provided with the nonce out of band, and when they spend the coins, they provide the script but not the nonce. This allows the recipient to know which coins they are spending, but not the public. However, the size of the proof grows endlessly as more spent coins cannot be proven to have been spent because the proofs are passed out-of-band. Maxwell suggested that instead of providing privacy, this implementation would make identities public but only once they're buried a bit, bounding the growth of the proofs.The email conversation between Gavin and Gregory Maxwell in May 2013 also discussed a proposal by Adam Back to hide coins being spent on the Bitcoin network. This would involve using blinded payments, which could not be used for transaction fees unless they were unblinded. The concept was similar to the Guy Fawkes protocol, but with the commitment being used to hide the coin being spent rather than the preimage proving authority to spend a coin.Moreover, Todd raised concerns about the lack of chargebacks in Bitcoin, which could create issues as criminals learn to use it for effective kidnap and ransom cases. He predicts that after the first $10mn kidnapping-for-bitcoin heist, governments will be forced to decide how they view the system, which could lead to them arresting/questioning anyone identified holding tainted coins or blocking Bitcoin altogether as Iran does TOR.Lastly, Adam Back proposed a blind commitment proposal that could fix the issue of protocol voting attacks and make it hard for even a 99% quorum to impose policies. Gregory Maxwell also discussed the vulnerability of protocols in the Bitcoin network and the need for committed transactions to help Bitcoin scale. Caleb James DeLisle wrote about the use of bit-commitments stating that it is up to individual users how long the maximum chain they accept is.
