Summary:

The email conversation between Adam Back and Jeff Garzik in May 2013 covers a range of topics related to Bitcoin. One such topic is merge-mining, which involves combining the mining process for Bitcoin and hashcash to create an anti-spam measure for emails. The discussion also revolves around creating anonymous identities using a decentralized approach called SIN. This identity system has a creation cost attached to it to prevent the creation of too many tokens.One idea proposed by Gregory Maxwell involves using a Merkle Sum tree to split one sacrifice among an arbitrarily large set of users. This idea allows for splitting an on-chain sacrifice into enough parts to make it useful even when transaction fees become large. Destroying Bitcoin through merged-mining, destroy-via-miner-fee, or anyone-can-spend is also discussed.Garzik explains that the miner's reward for processing transactions is the sum of all per-transaction fees and the initial currency distribution of 25 BTC. However, the protocol prevents zero-output, give-it-all-to-the-miner transactions. He suggests proof of directly mining a block with a coinbase that immediately makes payment to future miners and believes that ideally, one needs to simultaneously mine and give a reward to future block miners.The context introduces Rivest's PayWord as a potential mechanism for low-value micro-payments. Additionally, PowPay is presented as a method of making transactions that can provide greater scalability than Bitcoin. DoS attacks and PoW withholding are also discussed.Finally, the email exchanges talk about the proposal of creating an anonymous identity using a decentralized approach and integrating it with common software to prevent spam and abuse. The evaluation of these concepts largely depends on the needs of the service in question. It is suggested that the output sum > 0BTC limitation could be changed to >= to allow for closer approximation of give-to-miner transactions.
