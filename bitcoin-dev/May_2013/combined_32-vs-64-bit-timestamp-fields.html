Summary:

The Bitcoin protocol specification includes both 32-bit and 64-bit timestamp fields, which record the time at which a block was mined. The 32-bit field was originally used, but in 2011 it was increased to 64 bits to allow for longer timestamps up to the year 584,942,417. While the 64-bit field is now the recommended option, the 32-bit field remains in the protocol for backward compatibility. The reason for having both fields is likely due to legacy reasons or decisions made in the past.Interpreting timestamps on blockchain blocks for timestamping purposes is more complicated than it appears at first. In an email exchange between John Dillon and Peter Todd, Todd suggests changing the meaning of the header timestamp to be relative time passed since the last block. While absolute time is necessary in some cases, simple relative ordering is sufficient for many things.In an email thread dated May 8, 2013, Addy Yeow asked about the presence of both 32-bit and 64-bit timestamp fields in the Bitcoin protocol specification. Jeff Garzik replied with "hysterical raisins," implying that the reason was due to legacy issues. Satoshi Nakamoto's deliberate use of 32-bit timestamps in Bitcoin's code is speculated by Peter Todd in another message dated May 8, 2013. He suggests a hard fork as an opportunity to change the timestamps, as one would likely be necessary at some point for other reasons. Nodes have different acceptance criteria for blocks based on time and previous blocks, and miners have an incentive to set the timestamp accurately to prevent difficulty from driving up. Two types of timestamps possible are proofs that data existed before a time and proofs that data existed after. A random beacon can be used as a secure way of timestamping, but two assumptions must be made: 50% of the hashing power is controlled by honest miners, and those honest miners have accurate clocks. Another way to handle the problem of block header time is to have a flag day where blocks after a certain height are considered to have a different epoch from the standard 1970 one when computing their time. Pieter Wuille disagreed with Garzik, stating that he believed it was highly unlikely that they would break the block header format and invalidate all mining hardware. Overall, the email conversations and discussions highlight the technical complexities involved in designing and implementing blockchain technology.
