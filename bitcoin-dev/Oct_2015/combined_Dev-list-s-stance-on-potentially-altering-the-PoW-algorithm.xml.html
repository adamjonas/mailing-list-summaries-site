Summary:

The bitcoin community has been discussing the potential for changing the proof-of-work (PoW) function and whether it should be altered. One suggestion is to introduce new hash algorithms through ongoing competitions, which would help create an ASIC-resistant PoW. However, this requires human intervention and cannot be programmed algorithmically. Furthermore, debates on changing the PoW function are unlikely to occur unless there are issues with the current hash-power composition.In 2015, discussions were held on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list regarding algorithmic protection from ASIC/FPGA optimization. Jorge Timón argued that it would be impossible to guarantee such protection since anything that can be done with software can also be done with hardware, and specialized hardware is always more efficient energy-wise. However, BIP99 allows for "anti-miner hardforks" to restart the ASIC and mining market in case mining becomes too centralized. The term "ASIC-resistance" was criticized as an absurd term coined to promote some altcoins. An email exchange discussing the feasibility of ASIC-resistance in cryptocurrencies pointed out that the term was coined to promote certain altcoins. However, they noted that making it arbitrarily hard in practice is possible. They further added that simple-to-implement-in-hardware pow algorithms are preferable to memory-hard ones.The email conversation between Daniele Pinna and Adam Back discusses the proposal of an asymmetric memory-hard PoW through a recently published paper. The paper proposes an algorithm that guarantees protection from ASIC/FPGA optimization. The email thread also mentions the limitations of the Cuckoo cycle algorithm, which has been improved upon by the proposed algorithm. However, there are papers demonstrating that protection from ASIC/FPGA optimization is impossible. The email discussion raises questions about the stance of core developers on potentially altering the bitcoin PoW protocol. It is also noted that a novel optimization-resistant algorithm could greatly improve decentralization in the bitcoin network due to a resurgence of desktop/cellphone mining. In response to concerns about the centralised hoarding of mining ASICs, restarting mining with a new algorithm may be an acceptable solution. Such hoarding-miners do not participate in the economy and are not meaningful contributors by accepting payments from other bitcoin users. However, there is no basis for these claims, and such questions should be directed towards economists rather than developers.The fallacy of "all-or-nothing" thinking has also been highlighted, with criticism of the term "ASIC-resistance" coined to promote certain altcoins. It has been suggested that the arrogance of the Core camp should be toned down since it is possible to make an algorithm arbitrary hard to crack. Additionally, BIP99 suggests "anti-miner hardforks" to prevent mining centralization, but not for promoting ASIC-resistance altcoins.Overall, the discussions highlight the challenges associated with protecting against ASIC/FPGA optimization and the potential benefits of introducing a novel optimization-resistant algorithm to improve decentralization in the bitcoin network.
