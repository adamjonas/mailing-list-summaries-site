In 2015, the Bitcoin-dev mailing list discussed the network's available bandwidth for increased block sizes. One participant noted that in its current state, only 37% of nodes would be able to upload a 20MB block to a single peer in under 20 seconds. If the suitability bar is set at taking only 1% of the block time (6 seconds) to upload one block to one peer, then 69% of the network would fail for 20MB blocks. The discussion also focused on measuring the upload capacity of peers by downloading from them and ensuring consistency and accuracy in blockchain data.However, several discussions on the same mailing list in 2015 revolved around Bitcoin's network bandwidth and whether it could support larger block sizes. One test showed that only 16% of nodes could upload blocks faster than 10MB/s, while 37% failed to upload blocks faster than 1MB/s. This indicates that the current state of the network does not support increased block sizes. Despite this, the study found that the Bitcoin network can support larger blocks than the current 1MB limit, with an average maximum throughput of 27 transactions per second, corresponding to a block size of 2.1MB. Even nodes with slower connection speeds were able to handle blocks up to 4MB without issues. The study highlights the potential for future improvements in transaction processing, but any changes to the block size limit must be carefully evaluated and implemented to maintain the integrity of the Bitcoin network. The discussion on the bitcoin-dev forum serves as a reminder of the challenges involved in working with blockchain technology, and the importance of careful attention to detail in maintaining the accuracy and consistency of blockchain data. Concerns were raised regarding the potential for different selections to be made by each peer when measuring the upload capacity of peers by downloading from them, thus leading to discrepancies or errors. Additionally, due to bandwidth being generally asymmetric, the findings may be optimistic as only download capacity was measured. Furthermore, multiple peers at once need to be sent blocks for reliability, further reducing upload capacity. The study also notes that the network needs significant additional margin to resist attack as performance is consensus-critical. Therefore, any changes to the block size limit must be carefully evaluated and implemented to maintain the integrity of the Bitcoin network, taking into account factors such as decentralization, security, and economic incentives.
