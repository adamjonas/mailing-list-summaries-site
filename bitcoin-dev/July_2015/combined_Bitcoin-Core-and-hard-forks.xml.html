Summary:

The Bitcoin development community is currently engaged in discussions related to the security, operation, and sustainability of the network. Pieter Wuille believes that it is the responsibility of Bitcoin Core to create software that guarantees the security and operation of the network, including DoS protection mechanisms. He emphasizes the importance of a fair fee market and growing the block size limit with technology rather than due to a fear of change. The community is still grappling with finding a balance between increasing block sizes, maintaining a fair fee market, and ensuring the security and decentralization of the network.There are differing opinions on how to address the increasing demand for block space, such as increasing computational resources or increasing fees. Eric Lombrozo suggests direct party-to-party contract negotiation will enable mainstream usage of cryptocurrency, while the blockchain will serve primarily as a dispute resolution mechanism. The idea of implementing Quality-of-Service (QoS) guarantees in transactions has been proposed, with suggestions for miners to set different values for transaction fees and adding better QoS tools to create a healthy fee market.The debate over larger block sizes has taken a non-technical and counterproductive turn, with name-calling and drama on social media platforms proving to be counterproductive. While some argue that increasing block size is necessary to support mainstream adoption, others stress the importance of maintaining decentralization and balancing simplicity, fairness, and practicality for both miners and users.The Bitcoin mailing list has seen discussions about the authority of Bitcoin Core developers and their ability to set rules for the Bitcoin economy. There are concerns about forcing a view and giving up consensus, which could result in market disruption. The discussion also touches upon the possibility of implementing QoS guarantees and changing consensus arguments in the codebase while maintaining the security and operation of the Bitcoin network.Bitcoin developers are engaged in discussions about block size increases and fees, with some advocating for a hard fork to increase the current 1MB limit. Quality of service guarantees are suggested as a way to promote a healthy fee market, with third-party services bidding on behalf of miners or wallet owners. The importance of measuring risk against known metrics and approaching sensitive conversations with empathy is also highlighted.The community is divided on whether to increase block size limits or work on scalability solutions like Lightning Network. While some advocate for working together to address the problems and scale it up, others argue that block size increases do not solve the issue of scaling the network and only postpone dealing with transaction fees. There are concerns about centralized block size limits and the potential risks to the network's security model if block size is increased.Wallets should be assumed to have low computational resources and intermittent internet connections for the foreseeable future if it is to become a practical payment system. QoS guarantees can be obtained by using third parties separate from individual miners that do bidding on behalf of wallet owners. Negotiating directly with miners via smart contracts seems difficult at best due to mining being a random process. The blockchain primarily acts as a dispute resolution mechanism for direct party-to-party contract negotiations, while the block size is about supply and demand of finite resources.Jean-Paul Kogelman suggests using implicit QoS as a simpler alternative that requires fewer parties and aligns better with Bitcoin's original idea as a peer-to-peer digital cash system. In this approach, miners set their fees based on tiers for different levels of service, and users choose the level of service they want without considering block size. Miners then adjust their fees based on demand for each tier, increasing costs for popular tiers and lowering them for less popular ones. This incentivizes miners to adapt their pricing to user demand, creating a market-based approach to transaction optimization.Overall, there are different opinions on the best course of action to take, and it is crucial to weigh the pros and cons of each option before making a decision. The priority should be on finding better scaling solutions that avoid the need for global consensus for the vast majority of transactions, rather than increasing block size. The ongoing debate centers around the ability of users to decide what their consensus parameters should be at runtime and how best to sustain the Bitcoin system through fees.
