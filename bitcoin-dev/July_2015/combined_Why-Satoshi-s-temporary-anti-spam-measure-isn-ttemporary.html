In 2015, the bitcoin-dev mailing list was host to several email threads discussing various aspects of Bitcoin's future. One such thread featured a debate about whether smaller or larger block sizes were preferable for the cryptocurrency. Gavin Andresen argued that while smaller blocks had utility, larger blocks would lead to more innovation and utility without artificial limits. Jorge Timón argued that even if the Lightning Network was never developed and block size remained at 1 MB forever, fees would still be competitive with non-decentralized payment systems that have proportional fees. The discussion also touched on the importance of security and decentralization in maintaining Bitcoin's value. Adam Back argued that a proof-of-work blockchain is difficult to replicate due to its high level of security and lack of expertise required to maintain it.Another thread discussed scalability and speed in light of Bitcoin's payment network ("Layer 1"). Members agreed that Bitcoin should scale subject to the axiom of decentralization, and suggested that the addition of "layer 2" protocols (such as Lightning and other sidechains) will allow fast, low-fee bitcoin transactions within two years, which will simplify the scaling debate. The email thread also talked about how a slow or lack of increase to maximum transaction rate will cause pressure on fees. Losing decentralization would mean losing the whole thing. Additionally, there were debates about the risks and benefits associated with larger block sizes. Rusty, an advocate of IBLT, published tests which seemed to indicate that the risks of larger blocks were greater than initially thought. Pieter Wuille disagreed with those advocating for controversial changes to the network, arguing that the risks of such changes outweighed any potential benefits. Instead, he suggested that block sizes should be scaled gradually over time according to technological growth.Other discussions on the bitcoin-dev mailing list touched on the future of Bitcoin regarding its low availability of transaction space and high costs. While some argued that any decentralized system will have high transaction costs and scarcity, others pointed out that physical limitations would force decentralized systems to look certain ways, which could explain why there were "$10 fees" in the original scenario. The discussion also touched on internet architecture and the need for better architecture.One email thread centered around the potential benefits of decentralized technology following a similar path to multi-core CPUs. While Thomas Zander mistakenly used the term "distributed" instead of "decentralized," he noted that libraries and approaches for distributed systems can also be useful for decentralized systems.Overall, the discussions highlighted the importance of balancing technical considerations such as security and decentralization with economic factors such as fees and market share in determining the future of Bitcoin and other cryptocurrencies. The use of PGP encryption provided cryptographic privacy and authentication for data communication, and links to Abis.io and Keybase.io/odinn were provided for further information on Bitcoin development.
