Summary:

The bitcoin-dev mailing list saw a discussion between Thomas Zander and Adam Back regarding trust in the Bitcoin network. While Zander argued that decentralization and distribution of trust has historical precedent, Back clarified that the goal of Bitcoin is not to completely eliminate trust but rather to reduce the need for it in certain situations.The conversation revolves around the topic of trust in the Bitcoin network, with arguments made for both sides. Some argue that off-chain use of Bitcoin is a constructive way to improve scale and interoperability, while others believe it weakens Bitcoin's security. Lightning-like protocols may provide security at scale, but there are concerns about non-cooperative hubs flooding the network and making it unusable.Lightning assumes explicit trust and ID, which is not going to work, given that users of Bitcoin trust nodes doing the verification because they know it is in their best interest to be honest. The Lightning Network proposal was another topic of debate in the thread as the issue of identifying users online arose. One participant stated that Lightning assumes explicit trust and ID, which would not work since having explicit counter-parties is different from Bitcoin. Neither Sidechains nor LT preserve this important property, and there are no good proposals to make Bitcoin scale. However, another participant argued that Lightning does not require explicit trust, and worst case scenario, coins can end up blocked until next in-chain broadcast.In addition, Joseph Poon suggests that every transaction should have signed proof of payment to ensure that the recipient received the funds. Trust takes many forms and is not a binary function. Multisig escrow transactions are one way to conduct secure transactions without relying on trusted third parties.The importance of security and providing more security than people want is stressed in the discussion. Lightning-like protocols may provide security at scale, but off-chain use of Bitcoin should not weaken Bitcoin's security. There is also a mention of individuals within the Bitcoin sphere who need to be trusted to manage important tasks such as the Wiki and Github repository, but the concept of trust in the Bitcoin world is not binary and takes many different forms.Overall, the discussion highlights the complexity of trust in Bitcoin and the various tradeoffs involved in achieving decentralization and scalability. The history of money is a good way to study trust problems, but insights into these issues are rare to find in Bitcoin development. Finally, the assertion that one doesn't need to trust anyone to use Bitcoin is disputed because users rely on trusting the mining system and Bitcoin developers to a certain extent. There are different levels of trust required, and terms like "trustless" and "decentralized" simply indicate an acceptable level of trust in the system.
