Summary:

The discussion revolves around fee-bumping techniques for Layer 2 networks, particularly in Bitcoin's Lightning Network and Revault. Two main approaches, Child-Pay-For-Parent (CPFP) and input-based, are compared, with the latter being less studied but improved with the deployment of the SIGHASH_ANYPREVOUT softfork proposal. The post proposes a new class of solutions called "tx mutation schemes," which can increase the fee by lowering a particular output after the transaction is signed without invalidating the signature.The trade-offs associated with fee-bumping reserves are also discussed, along with the potential benefits of future soft forks. The post concludes by evaluating the Fee-Bumping Batching mechanism for both approaches and highlights future package relay bandwidth efficiency considerations. Additionally, Antoine and Lloyd exchange emails discussing different fee-bumping techniques, while Antoine also discusses the potential benefits of future soft forks that would allow for significant onchain footprint savings. Finally, the conversation covers various technical approaches to secure transactions and prevent malicious attacks in multi-party contracts and channels, as well as tradeoffs associated with fee-bumping reserves and the use of keys. A proposed solution involves using smarter tx-relay techniques such as "attach-on-contract-utxo-root" CPFP or blinded CPFP. The proposed solution for securing transactions and preventing malicious attacks in multi-party contracts and channels involves using MuSig key for all contract participants with SIGHASH_ANYPREVOUT | SIGHASH_IOMAP for signature commitment. There is also a discussion around the use of fee-bumping UTXO pools for vaults and Lightning Network channels. It is noted that node operators may not keep enough fee-bumping reserves, which could create a tradeoff between liquidity and security. The proposed solution involves creating an entire UTXO pool in advance by broadcasting a single fan-out transaction. Smarter tx-relay techniques such as "attach-on-contract-utxo-root" CPFP or blinded CPFP might help solve this issue. Lastly, the conversation discusses the proposed new SIGHASH_GROUP flag that commits to each output in a transaction and could allow for the combination of x-inputs and y-outputs safely. The post compares input-based and Child-Pay-For-Parent (CPFP) approaches for fee-bumping and anti-fee sniping protection. Input-based bumping can be done via SIGHASH_ANYONECANPAY/SIGHASH_SINGLE malleability flags but doesn't work for first stage transactions with multiple outputs. To overcome this limitation, the post suggests deploying the SIGHASH_ANYPREVOUT softfork proposal. Overall, the post and email thread provide in-depth discussions on various fee-bumping strategies for second-layer protocols and their trade-offs in terms of security, efficiency, and scalability. Relevant resources and proposals are also provided for further exploration and consideration.
