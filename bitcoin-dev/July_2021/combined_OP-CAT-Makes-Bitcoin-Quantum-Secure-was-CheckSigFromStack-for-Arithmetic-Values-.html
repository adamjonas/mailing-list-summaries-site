Summary:

A proposal has been made to make Bitcoin quantum-proof using various methods, including Lamport signatures, Winternitz OTS, and OP_CAT. While Lamport signatures are resistant to quantum computing, they are also large in size. Winternitz OTS may be used to compress them, but it requires a limited-repeat construct that is not available in SCRIPT. OP_CAT can sign up to 20 bytes, making it "quantum safe," but it cannot be directly soft-forked to Segwit v0 because it modifies the stack, so a new opcode would be required.Taproot provides another possible option for making Bitcoin quantum-proof, as the commitment scheme can be securely opened to m even with a quantum computer. Additionally, Schnorr signatures have a stronger non-malleability property than ECDSA, making them binding to the approved transaction, and once Lamport signed, even a quantum computer could not steal the funds.Jeremy Rubin proposed using OP_CAT or similar operations to sign an EC signature, making Bitcoin "quantum safe." ZmnSCPxj responded by suggesting the use of Winternitz OTS instead. Although Lamport signatures are quantum secure, they require a couple of kilobytes, which is too large for the current block size limit. However, Rubin's proposal does fit within the program space and stack size limits for Bitcoin scripts. The commitment scheme in Taproot can also nest inside a Tapscript path, adding more security to the system.In brief, the proposals aim to make Bitcoin quantum-proof using various methods. While some methods require a limited-repeat construct that is not available in SCRIPT, others involve OP_CAT or similar operations to sign an EC signature. The commitment scheme in Taproot can be securely opened to m even with a quantum computer, and Schnorr signatures have a stronger non-malleability property than ECDSA, making them binding to the approved transaction. It remains to be seen which method will be implemented and how effective it will be against quantum computers.
