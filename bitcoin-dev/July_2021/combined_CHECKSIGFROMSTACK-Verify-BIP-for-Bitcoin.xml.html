Summary:

The Bitcoin community has been discussing the implementation of the OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACK opcode and the various options available to split R/S values or variable-length messages. There have been four options considered, including making a new 64-byte PK standard, splitting (R,S), using different opcodes, and CAT. The Elements project is working on updating the implementation of OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACK for tapscript on elements, which would use BIP-0340 schnorr signatures and support variable-length messages, making it suitable for Bitcoin.In addition to this, there has been discussion around the adaptation of the CheckDataSig specification for a BIP in Bitcoin using 2 OP_SUCCESSX opcodes. The proposed specifications aim to improve the efficiency and flexibility of the Bitcoin Cash network. Modifications were suggested, including whether CSFS should require some sort of tagged hash, split the signature's R & S value stack items, or support a cheap way to reference the taproot internal or external key.Jeremy Rubin, a member of the Bitcoin community, has shared his thoughts on sophisticated covenants and expressed his interest in the development of CSFS as an independent non-bundle. He believes that validation costs can be kept in check and individuals are free to burn their coins if they so choose but wants to ensure that when covenants are enabled, people do not have to jump through too many hoops. He plans on spearheading the development of CSFS and has provided a link to his current thinking on how to proceed with deploying and developing covenant systems for Bitcoin.There have also been inquiries about the design process for the BIP, specific applications for !=32 arguments, and the safety of using 0-520 byte arguments. References were provided to several important specifications, including OP_CHECKSIG, Strict DER Encoding, Low-S and Nullfail Specification, Bitcoin ABC implementation, Andrew Stone’s OP_DATASIGVERIFY, Andrew Stone's article on Scripting, and Peer Review of Andrew Stone's Proposal.
