Summary:

The article discusses various proposals and ideas put forward by Bitcoin developers to achieve atomicity and stealth payments without adding extra space to the blockchain. One proposal involves a lightweight client identifying transactions via the payment protocol, while another suggests extracting randomness from one of the scriptSigs through an algorithm. However, there are still issues with these proposals, such as the possibility of the sender withholding the signed transaction or the receiver claiming to have never received it.Pieter Wuille suggests improving atomicity in the payment protocol instead of adding stealth functionality as a last resort. He argues that communication between sender and receiver has its benefits, such as negotiation of transaction details, messages associated with the transaction, no need to scan the blockchain for incoming transactions, and the ability to cancel if either party doesn't agree. However, improving atomicity may not be practical due to edge cases and IsStandard() rules. Peter Todd proposed a payment system that would eliminate edge cases and allow for proof of payment, involving signed PaymentACK messages and scriptSig-less Bitcoin transactions. Meanwhile, Jeff Garzik argues that putting the communications medium in the blockchain itself is wrong and goes against the purpose of the blockchain. The speaker suggests that atomicity in transactions should be utilized more for secure payments that don't take up extra space in the blockchain, while also acknowledging the benefits of communication between the sender and receiver.The context also discusses the rejected idea of using a communication backchannel for retrieving funds due to non-atomicity issues. Instead, payment messages are sent directly to the merchant who takes responsibility for broadcast, ensuring atomicity. Extending BIP70 with ECDH would make sense in order to enable other useful features such as messages and refund addresses. In a conversation between Peter Todd and Pieter Wuille, Wuille proposed an idea for making payments more atomic and avoiding edge cases. The idea involves having PaymentACK messages signed with the same key as the payment request, or using some delegation mechanism.Chris Pacia and another individual discussed the potential of using the payment protocol for stealth, but they realized that doing so would require frequent wallet backups. Although wallet backups may become more valuable over time as they contain more essential data not found on the blockchain, the ability to access funds with just 12 words and a date is a desirable safety feature. Overall, there is a need for discrete key multisig, extending BIP70 with ECDH, and letting the free market for fees do its job without dictating trade-offs based on top-down edict.
