Summary:

In the Bitcoin developer community, there have been proposals to change the current fee structure to reduce pending transaction time and individual transaction fees while increasing total transaction fees. One proposal suggests using marginal pricing with no block size limit, allowing miners to choose the market sat/byte rate with the cheapest-fee transaction included in their block. This dynamic block size limit is regulated by profit motive and aims to maximize transaction fees for every block while ensuring all fees are fair. Low-fee transactions will eventually confirm, giving them a liveness property not previously enjoyed.Another proposal suggests redesigning the fee market of Bitcoin by removing the block size limit and allowing miners to implicitly choose the market sat/byte rate. Excess transaction fees are refunded to the inputs, resulting in a dynamic block size limit regulated by profit motive. The goal is to maximize total transaction fees, reduce pending transaction time, and individual transaction fees. The current fee system used in Bitcoin, Generalized first-price auction, may not be maximizing miners' fees. A two-part pricing scheme involving a fixed fee per transaction plus variable fee per byte could work better by deterring spamming of the mempool and not losing revenue from people with higher willingness to pay.There are concerns about Out of Band (OOB) fees in Bitcoin, but participants make the best decisions for their own interests, resulting in multiple choices. Competent wallet software can automatically draft two transactions, one paying OOB and one paying min fee, at equivalent expected rates. Miners would construct blocks that locally maximized their revenue. Super-rational behavior might be for a majority of hashpower to collude to permit high fee-rate transactions every other block and only permit low feerate in the others, handicapping the capacity limiting argument.The article discusses the concept of Out of Band (OOB) fees in Bitcoin, which have been prevalent throughout its existence. The argument made is that it is impossible for both miners and users to prefer OOB fees as the system disincentivizes them. However, this analysis is flawed as it assumes a single decision-maker, which is not true in most economic circumstances.Long-term, tx fees must support hash power by themselves. The economic approach to this is to remove the block size limit and allow miners to implicitly choose the market sat/byte rate with the cheapest-fee transaction included in their block. Excess transaction fees are refunded to the inputs. This will result in a dynamic block size limit regulated by profit motive.In an email thread discussing auction theory and its potential application to Bitcoin, Chenxi Cai explains that Bitcoin currently uses a Generalized first-price auction. Despite the potential benefits of alternative approaches such as the Generalized second-price auction and the Vickrey-Clarke-Groves auction, the choice of fee design will not impact miners' fees unless the outcomes of the auction change. Changing the bidding system to the marginal price would allow for a superseding of the block size limit, which changes the outcome of the auction as different transactions are included.Lastly, the hash power of individual miners is not significant compared to the entire network. The proposals aim to improve the efficiency and fairness of the fee system in Bitcoin.
