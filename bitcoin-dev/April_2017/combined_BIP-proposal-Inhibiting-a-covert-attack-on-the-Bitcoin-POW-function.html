Bitcoin Core developer Gregory Maxwell has proposed a solution to the potential security problem in the proof of work function that could save miners up to 30% of energy costs. The vulnerability was found in a major manufacturer's mining ASICs, and its exploitation could interfere with useful improvements like segregated witness. A new BIP draft has been proposed to tackle the issue of ASICBOOST mining, which some miners use to gain an unfair advantage. Critics argue that it is damaging to Bitcoin's ecosystem, price, and ability to upgrade while holding back significant improvements. The ongoing conflict between Bitmain and Bitcoin developers over ASICBOOST is being compared to last year's DAO hack. Maxwell has proposed inhibiting the covert attack on the Bitcoin POW function caused by ASICBOOST, but concerns have been raised about the impact on miner revenue and the difficulty of implementation. A soft fork proposal has also been made to lower technical complexity and risk associated with the issue. Additionally, there are proposals to prevent the covert exploitation of the vulnerability caused by the design oversight in the PoW function.The controversy surrounding ASICBOOST continues, with accusations that certain miners secretly use it to gain an unfair advantage. Bitmain's chips have confirmed support for ASICBOOST, adding fuel to the debate. The ethical implications of using ASICBOOST mining are a topic of debate within the Bitcoin community. Some believe miners should keep their optimizations to themselves, while others argue that such optimizations discourage growth and improvement of the protocol.The Bitcoin mining technique known as ASICBOOST is causing controversy among miners, with some using it to save up to 30% on energy costs. Gregory Maxwell, a Bitcoin Core developer, has proposed a technical solution to prevent covert ASICBOOST mining, which he claims is preventing improvements to the Bitcoin protocol. However, there are practical concerns regarding the implementation of such a plan, as miners are likely to resist this change. Peter Todd, another Bitcoin developer, has argued that disabling ASICBOOST would only affect Bitmain and not other miners. Todd emphasizes that ASICBOOST is only relevant to miners like Bitmain who have access to it while other miners don't. The patent for ASICBOOST is held by Timo Hanke and Sergio Demian Lerner but has not been licensed for free and open use by the public. The situation has been compared to the DAO fork, with developers proposing to modify the code in order to negate gains made by Bitmain. While some believe this is justified, others argue that rule changes should not be used to disempower and impoverish particular entities without very good reason. Overall, the debate around the ethical implications of ASICBOOST continues, with some arguing for blocking only covert usage while others argue that there is every reason to eliminate both non-covert and covert usage. It is important to realize how difficult it will be to pull off such a change, as it involves invalidating hardware. Proposals that are not simple and least contentious are unlikely to succeed.
