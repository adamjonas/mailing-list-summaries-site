Summary:

On the bitcoin-dev mailing list, there have been discussions about preventing miner centralization and protecting the network from malicious actions by hashpower majorities. One proposed solution is the implementation of Malicious Miner Reactive Proof of Work Additions (MR POWA) through a hard fork. This involves introducing multiple new proofs of work, including Scrypt, Ethash, and Equihash, which would diversify hardware and reduce the impact of subsidised or inexpensive electricity in certain regions.The proposed MR POWA hard fork would serve as a deterrent against malicious actors who have significant influence over SHA256 hardware and would ideally never activate. However, if consensus were to form around a hard fork in the future, nodes would be able to upgrade and MR POWA would automatically activate on non-upgraded nodes, but it would be of no economic significance. The hard fork could also introduce a block size increase and activate SegWit.Another solution proposed on April 16, 2017, by Erik Aronesty was to use eight or more secure hashes that can be implemented on GPU/CPU and rotate through them per block round robin. This method is less expensive to maintain than ASIC since the write time for configuring FPGA with a fresh bitstream is measured in tens of milliseconds. However, centralized manufacturing leads to a monopoly, and a rotating POW seems to make ASIC manufacture impractical compared to generalized, commercially available hardware.Despite the proposed solutions, there are concerns about changing the economic incentives behind bitcoin mining. Andrew Johnson expressed his worry about how much the proposed solutions could change the economic incentives behind bitcoin mining since people might not be able to invest in hardware reliably without knowing their profitability.The concept of chain work in Bitcoin is being reevaluated due to the differing difficulties of hash functions, which could significantly alter the economic incentives behind bitcoin mining and create new attack vectors if large amounts of hardware are taken offline. Erik Aronesty suggested using a variant of Keccak where the sponge size increases with additional zero bits required to prevent specialized hardware, but SHA3 would have to be compromised for this to work.Ultimately, consensus among nodes and miners remains crucial for the success and security of Bitcoin. The writer supports having multiple Proof-of-Work (PoW) forks to choose from to prevent attackers from secretly making ASICs ahead of time in the event of an emergency PoW fork. However, they believe that it is problematic to have one chain running a rotation of algorithms and suggest having just one algorithm without rotation once it is chosen.
