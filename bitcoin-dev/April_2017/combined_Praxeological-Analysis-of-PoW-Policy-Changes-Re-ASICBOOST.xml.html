Summary:

This article discusses the ongoing controversy surrounding the use of ASICBOOST technology in Bitcoin mining and the proposed policy changes to counter it. The potential profits from covertly using ASICBOOST are estimated to be around $100 million USD per year. However, miners and money owners have different motivations, with miners seeking short-term ROI and money owners wanting a decentralized PoW algorithm.Changing the PoW algorithm in a way that renders existing ASIC miner capital worthless is not ideal as it would create opportunities for first-to-market optimizations to centralize mining. Gregory Maxwell's proposal does not make existing mining capital worthless; it simply removes the advantage of using the patent-encumbered optimization. The article recommends making it a policy to counter/prevent patent-encumbered PoW optimizations if possible while minimizing disruption on the utility and availability of optimized mining capital equipment.Efficient mining hardware development is desirable for users, as it makes transaction ordering more future-proof. Any policy changes to PoW should be made carefully to minimize hampering effects on research and development of such hardware. The current BIP proposed by Gregory Maxwell blocks the patented covert optimization of ASICBOOST, but further measures may need to be taken to disable the evident version of ASICBOOST if it becomes an issue in the future.Overall, owners of Bitcoin should support and activate the proposed PoW policy change by Gregory Maxwell as soon as possible to counter the ASICBOOST patent encumbrance. Future ASICs and mining equipment should be made with the option to run without optimizations that make assumptions about policy subject to change in a future soft fork.
