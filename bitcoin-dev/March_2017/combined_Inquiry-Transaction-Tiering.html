Summary:

The Bitcoin community is engaged in discussions about transaction tiering and the possibility of users choosing where they transact. The idea is to have miners listen to the network rather than soliciting transactions directly from users, which can contravene a critical value-add of the peer-to-peer network. Two proposed ways of providing this service include creating segregated mempools managed by an authenticated third-party, or creating transactions where the mining fee is an output to an address owned by preferred miner(s). However, both methods have potential pitfalls that need attention before they become urgent.The discussion also delves into the idea of a block space authority, which could govern a particular block and include a user's transaction. This leads to a discussion on the legitimate authority of nodes in the network and how it can be mapped to terminology in politics, such as proposing, approving, or rejecting changes. Participants argue that miners do not have any real authority unless it is due to unfounded/insecure reasons such as divine inspiration or respect. Martin Stolze proposes transaction tiering as a means for users to choose which miners confirm their transactions, while also suggesting the use of "block producer" or "block generator" as more accurate terminology. AJ West has created a proof-of-concept project called Preferred Miner to explore the idea of miners as service providers. However, there are concerns around creating segregated mempools and extra fees for users. The conversation also touches on the authority of transaction processors or block generators and their economic incentives versus political motivations. The recent language used in opinions by the ECB and drafts by the EU suggests that commercial interest must have a way to transact on somewhat regulated space. There is also discussion around the use of clear and accurate terminology to better understand the complexities of Bitcoin and its evolution.Martin Stolze has identified three options along the lines of Lawrence Lessig's concept of Cyberspace: Law, Architecture, and Market. Law requires direct engagement between users and block space authorities (BSAs), which could involve brokers and increase transaction costs significantly. Architecture suggests the removal of transaction fees, but this could lead to spam/empty blocks and require a complete redesign of Bitcoin. Lastly, Market proposes direct communication between users and BSAs, through individual mempools. Different BSAs can receive different transaction fees for the same transaction in their respective mempool to reflect the user's preference.The email discusses the desire to have the ability to choose where to transact in Bitcoin. The writer argues that the physical location of the miner does not have any legal implications for the relationship between sender and receiver of a payment. They explain that German law would apply to a contract between two parties in Germany, even if the payment was made in Icelandic kronur via PayPal, which went through Luxembourg, Australia, and the US. The writer also claims that almost nobody cares who will mine a transaction because it makes no technical difference. Overall, the discussions revolve around transaction tiering, miners as service providers, block space authority, legitimate authority of nodes, and the importance of clear and accurate terminology in understanding the complexities of Bitcoin. The ongoing evolution of Bitcoin is highlighted, with various proposals and potential pitfalls being discussed.
