Summary:

The implementation of Segregated Witness (SegWit) for Bitcoin Core's 0.13.1 release has been a contentious issue due to its long wait time and uncertainty around activation. Despite the objections, most underlying libraries have already adapted or are in the process of being adapted for SegWit, and companies are planning to update their services accordingly. The release of SegWit is set to include segregated witness for Bitcoin mainnet, and SPV wallets will continue to function as normal without upgrade.However, end users may want to upgrade to benefit from lower transaction fees when sending transactions. Wallets may not want to invest resources adding support for a feature that may never be activated, and some are taking a "wait and see" approach. Tom Zander has voiced concern over the deployment of SegWit and suggests using BIP9 to shorten the grace period is risky as it needs to be implemented in an extra-safe way due to its controversial nature. Once deployed, the feature cannot be rolled back, and all transactions made during this time will turn into everyone-can-spend transactions.Despite the objections, most wallets will probably work just fine even if they don't support SegWit, and developers should use testnet or regtest to iron out any issues before hitting mainnet. Flexible Transactions is considered as a safer alternative to SegWit, but it will not be known which option will be activated until it is locked in.In a recent discussion on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list, Tom Zander suggested a minimum of two months as the fallow period for upgrading to a new release. However, Gavin Andresen replied that he had asked many businesses and individuals how long it would take them to upgrade to a new release over the last year or two, and nobody said it would take them more than two weeks.Bitcoin Core's 0.13.1 release will include segregated witness for Bitcoin mainnet, which has been tested on testnet and other software. However, objections have been raised to the way it has been implemented, making some wallets take a "wait and see" approach. While most underlying libraries have already adapted or are in the process of being adapted for SegWit, most libraries cannot release their SegWit supported versions until SegWit is released as final in 0.13.1. Companies are already planning to update their services for SegWit despite this.Pieter Wuille proposed setting BIP 141's start time to November 15, 2016, at 0:00 UTC (unix time 1479168000), following the BIP9 recommendation to set the versionbits start time a month in the future. However, there are concerns about the safety of the upgrade, with some members calling for an extended "fallow period" to ensure safety.The discussion on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list revolves around the safety measures for deploying SegWit, a controversial soft-fork. Tom Zander highlights that proper miner support is crucial before activation as all transactions made during this period would become everyone-can-spent transactions if any issues occur. He suggests waiting for at least a week to observe any signs of false signaling and cautions against using SegWit extensively at the beginning.Zander argues that longer grace periods for upgrades would make the network safer, but Gavin Andresen believes that if someone is running their own validation code, they should be sophisticated enough to mitigate any risks associated with SegWit activation on their own. Businesses and individuals surveyed by Andresen believe it would take them no more than two weeks to upgrade to a new release.
