Summary:

The context discusses various proposals and discussions related to Bitcoin's inflation formula, volatility, stability, and the feasibility of stablecoins in a secure and P2P system. The proposed Bitcoin Improvement Proposal suggests changing the inflation formula, which some believe would violate the trust of those holding the currency who bought it hoping its value in relation to other currencies would increase, not just because of its fixed money supply.Bitcoin's volatility is attributed to low liquidity, future acceptance based on volatile sources such as politicians' opinions and government policies, and lack of proof-of-work from society's end. However, laws, regulations, and policies will add stability over time.A proposal by Ryan Carboni suggesting hard-coding or letting miners choose exchanges for adjusting block rewards is deemed unfeasible due to potential vulnerabilities and the need for consensus between miners. The lack of price stability in Bitcoin generates menu costs and incentivizes speculation, hindering its viability as a currency in a closed economic ecosystem.Finally, discussions about stablecoins and their feasibility in a secure and P2P system are also mentioned, with one participant deeming them impossible to implement. The proposal to add price stability to Bitcoin without pegging it to any one currency suggests that miners keep track of the exchanges. They would compare the percentage change in exchange price over the previous 432 blocks, calculate the standard deviation of the percentage change and exclude outliers. The minimum block reward would be one Bitcoin, reducing variability in relation to a basket of currencies and discouraging views that Bitcoin is speculative.However, the idea of adding reliance on data from third parties outside the Bitcoin network is not well-received and may be rejected due to potential vulnerabilities. The proposal suggests that exchanges should be hard-coded into Bitcoin or miners should choose, but there must be a consensus between miners to avoid rejections from peers. Many people bought Bitcoin hoping for its value to increase compared to other currencies, not just because of its fixed money supply.In a conversation between Gavin Andresen and Ryan Carboni, Andresen pointed out that the proposal to hard code exchanges into Bitcoin or allow miners to choose them ignores the difficult problem that needs to be solved for it to work. He compared it to saying "We need a way to travel around the world quickly. There will be an anti-gravity technology; how this works is not something I'm personally focused on." Ryan Carboni stated that the lack of a Central Bank in Bitcoin is a feature rather than a bug and that political debates should be directed elsewhere. The email exchange between Gavin Andresen and Ryan Carboni took place on the Bitcoin-development mailing list sponsored by Intel XDK. Carboni suggested hard-coding exchanges into the system or leaving them up to miners to choose, but Andresen pushed back, comparing it to saying we need a way to travel around the world quickly without focusing on the difficult problem that needs to be solved for it to work.
