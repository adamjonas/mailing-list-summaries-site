Summary:

The Bitcoin development community has been discussing several proposals related to dust protection measures and the minimum fee rate required for relay. Aaradhya Chauhan suggests that the minimum fee rate of 1 sat/vB is too high and should be lowered, even if not immediately, with the support of full node operators as it is not a consensus rule. She proposes that this change can be made when prices double and remain constant at that point. Peter Todd suggests removing the fixed dust limit entirely and relying solely on the mempool size limit to determine what is or is not dust. He proposes that nodes could change their limits without requesting permission or making changes in the source code. In addition, he states that if some node was a miner, then it could be enforced, but free transactions were still useful for communication.There is also a discussion about the feasibility of low-fee transactions. Some nodes may accept zero to one satoshi per virtual byte transactions while some miners may choose to accept only 10 sats/vB or higher. However, the de-facto standard is one satoshi per virtual byte. Fees are not part of the consensus, so they can be freely adjusted by each node. Peter Todd suggests a hashcash-based alternative broadcast scheme as a way to rate-limit access to a broadcast network used by a small minority of nodes. This would involve attaching work to low fee transactions as compensation and/or spam proof, but it suffers from ASICs.Another proposal suggested miners experiment with lower fee rates to increase revenue by using them on odd dates (even dates remain default) for a month. However, Todd points out that without a way for lower-fee-rate transactions to get to those miners, experiments like that are pointless. He proposes that if someone wants to suggest things like this, they should propose a way to get non-standard transactions to miners, like a hashcash-based alternative broadcast scheme.In recent discussions on the Bitcoin-dev mailing list, David A. Harding clarified that the minimum output amount policy (dust limit) should not be confused with the minimum transaction relay feerate policy (min tx relay fee). Lowering the minimum relay feerate was seriously proposed four years ago in a patch to Bitcoin Core, but it faced challenges such as preventing denial/degradation-of-service attacks and dealing with a fragmented userbase. The economic benefit of reducing the feerates for the bottom of the mempool seems small compared to the complexity of lowering the minimum feerate.The email conversation between Alice and Peter Todd discusses whether lowering the minrelaytxfee for Bitcoin transactions would reduce profits for miners. Peter presents a complex argument about block publication incentives for miners and states that, to a first approximation, failing to mine a known transaction at any fee above zero is leaving money on the table. Overall, there are ongoing discussions among the Bitcoin development community regarding low-fee transactions and dust limit policies. While there is no hard consensus rule on fees, each node can adjust its own fees, and the de-facto standard is one satoshi per virtual byte. Developers have tried to decrease the default minimum relay tx fee rate, but concerns about DoS attacks, impact on compact blocks and erlay efficiency, transaction pinning, and increased IBD time have made it challenging. Some have suggested experimenting with lower fee rates to analyze their impact on revenue, but without a way for these transactions to reach miners, such experiments are pointless.
