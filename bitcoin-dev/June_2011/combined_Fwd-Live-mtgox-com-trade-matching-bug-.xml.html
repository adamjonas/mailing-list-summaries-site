Summary:

In 2011, an email thread was started discussing whether or not Mt Gox related topics should be discussed on the Bitcoin development forum. While some argued that it was relevant as other Bitcoin software, others pointed out that because it was closed source, there was nothing that could be done about it by forum members. Additionally, there was concern about disclosing security vulnerabilities publicly and the need to give developers enough time to fix them before going public. The email ended with a digital signature attachment for added security.Mike Hearn requested that discussions related to Mt Gox be kept off the bitcoin-development forum, arguing that it wasn't directly related to core software. However, someone else believed that Mt Gox's code was just as relevant as BitcoinJ or any other Bitcoin software.Then, on June 27, 2011, Doug Huff sent an email to the Full Disclosure mailing list, Mt.Gox and Bitcoin Dev Development groups regarding a bug in mtgox.com's trade matching system. The bug could be exploited by traders to drain their accounts. Huff suggested that the site should be taken down until the issue was resolved or publicly demonstrate how this order would never execute. He also warned that if the bug was only a display bug, it could still be used to influence market conditions. The email contained two attachments, smime.p7s and PGP.sig, which could not be opened without additional information.Gavin Andresen confirmed that the Bitcoin-development mailing list is not for the discussion of Mt Gox or other sites or software that uses bitcoin. In response, Mike Hearn requested that Mt Gox-related content be kept off the development list as it was unrelated to core Bitcoin software.Doug Huff then forwarded an email detailing a live mtgox.com trading bug, which could be used to influence market conditions if it is only a display bug. He suggested either taking the site down until corrected, or publicly showing how the order would not execute.
