In a recent discussion, Antoine Riard addressed the concern of front-running in a peer-to-peer marketplace. Front-running refers to entering into a trade with advance knowledge of a transaction that will impact the price of the underlying security. In the context of Bitcoin and CivKit, front-running could occur when a bulletin board operator discovers a batch of market offers and takes advantage of the opportunity before publishing the offer on their board.To understand the dynamics of this scenario, Antoine outlined a hypothetical situation involving Mary the Maker, Terry the Taker, and Bob the bulletin board operator. Mary publishes a limit order to buy a derivative, and Bob, as the bulletin board operator, has the option to execute the order. If Bob chooses not to execute, he relays the order to Terry and other subscribers to his market. Terry then has the option to execute the trade against Mary's limit order. If Terry also decides not to execute, Mary's order remains on the bulletin board.Antoine suggests that this model resembles more of a brokerage rather than an exchange market structure. He proposes that Terry should open his own brokerage and compete with Bob by quoting prices. By comparing metrics like execution quality, such as price improvement, execution speed, and effective spread, Bob and Terry can attract more market activity by providing better prices.While front-running is considered a concern, Antoine believes it indicates active participation and liquidity in the market. He emphasizes the importance of finding products people are interested in trading and creating a good user experience. Antoine mentions the possibility of having tiered services, where one market bulletin board specializes in processing large-scale data and another focuses on content aggregation. This differentiation allows users to choose the trading approach that suits their preferences.Antoine also discusses potential approaches to mitigate front-running risks in a peer-to-peer marketplace. One approach involves duplicating offers to multiple concurrent boards operated by independent identities and monitoring latency anomalies. Another approach suggests running bulletin boards as a federation, using consensus mechanisms to ensure fair publication of offers. Each approach has its own advantages and considerations.In conclusion, Antoine highlights that the CivKit framework provides flexibility for peer-to-peer marketplaces, allowing adjustments to front-running resistance parameters based on specific trade types and market participants' tolerance. The design aims to enable censorship-resistant and permissionless global trading while ensuring user privacy and incentivizing efficient service operators. By addressing concerns like front-running and focusing on user experience, the marketplace can attract active participants and liquidity.The paper discussed in the provided context delves into the architecture of a system and expresses gratitude to the peer reviewers who contributed to its development. The authors mention their plans to gradually release code and modules, utilizing the Lightning Dev Kit and Nostr libraries.
