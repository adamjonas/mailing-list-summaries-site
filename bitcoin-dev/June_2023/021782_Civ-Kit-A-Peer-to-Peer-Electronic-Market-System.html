The conversation discussed the design assumptions and concerns surrounding front-running in a peer-to-peer marketplace. Mary, Terry, and Bob were mentioned as participants in this discussion. Mary published a limit order to buy a derivative, and Bob had the option to execute the order or relay it to Terry. The concern raised was that Bob, as the bulletin board operator, could collect outsized profits, potentially causing Terry to move to other markets. It was suggested that Terry should open his own brokerage and compete with Bob on quoting prices.One solution proposed to mitigate front-running was to have multiple bulletin boards operated by independent identities. By monitoring the latency of these boards, anomalies could be identified and addressed. Another approach discussed was running the bulletin boards as a federation, using techniques like Honey Badger BFT, where consensus among federation members would be required before publishing offers.The importance of execution quality and providing better prices was emphasized, as it attracted more market activity. The concept of front-running resistance in different types of trades was explored, with some goods being considered less risky due to factors like production cycles. However, for fiat currencies or bitcoin financial contracts, front-running by the bulletin board remained a qualified concern.Different security paradigms were discussed, such as duplicating the announcement of offers to multiple boards and monitoring latency, or running the boards as a federation with consensus rules. The configuration of these parameters depended on real-world deployments and the types of trades involved.The conversation concluded by mentioning the CivKit framework, which aims to provide a flexible framework for peer-to-peer marketplaces.
