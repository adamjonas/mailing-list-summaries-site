Summary:

Bitcoin developers have been discussing various aspects of Bitcoin development, including checkpoints, hard forks, and the maximum depth of a reorg allowed by non-machine consensus. One proposal suggests eliminating checkpoints altogether, while another suggests hardcoding a blockhash that would exempt a blockchain from following BIP65 rule up until that blockhash, while any other blockchain without that blockhash must respect BIP65 for all blocks in the chain. Despite concerns raised regarding the performance optimization and the process followed for this change, the developer responsible has agreed to modify the BIP draft to de-emphasize the performance aspect. Eric Voskuil expressed his disagreement with the recent simplification of consensus rules for BIPs 34, 66, and 65, calling it a "horrible precedent" due to its hardcoding of rule changes based on large forks indicating catastrophic failure, poor process in regards to already shipping, and the lack of material performance optimization. Pieter Wuille proposed getting rid of checkpoints on the blockchain to prevent developers from controlling the "true chain". He believes that if review is good enough to catch unintended consensus changes, it should also be able to catch the introduction of an invalid checkpoint.In a discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, Tier Nolan argued against the addition of a checkpoint in relation to a potential 50k re-org. Nolan suggested that a hard fork with a slim chance of practical effect is preferable to adding a checkpoint. The conversation also touches on buried softforks, which modify the validity of a theoretically construable chain from invalid to valid, rather than being a fork at all. There is disagreement on the use of buried softforks as a performance optimization.The BIP process pertains to changes made in the Bitcoin protocol, and this specific BIP has been accepted. However, Eric Voskuil via bitcoin-dev expressed his disapproval of the change, stating that it sets a bad precedent, as well as poor processes. In November 2016, Bitcoin Core merged a simplification to the consensus rules surrounding deployment of BIPs 34, 66, and 65. This resulted in a discussion among developers, with Eric Voskuil initially opposing the change. During the discussion, BIP30, which resolved a catastrophic protocol flaw, was brought up as an example of a previous change that had been given similar treatment after a reasonable amount of time had passed.Overall, the goal is to simplify the code while ensuring the security and validity of the blockchain. The ongoing discussions and proposals regarding the use of checkpoints and simplification of consensus rules for BIPs 34, 66, and 65 in Bitcoin's blockchain will continue to shape the future of Bitcoin development.
