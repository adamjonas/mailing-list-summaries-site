Summary:

In March 2013, a conversation between Luke-Jr and Randy Willis centered around the idea of introducing super-nodes with thousands of connected peers to improve UDP and Bitcoin protocol capabilities. However, they emphasized that any UDP Bitcoin protocol should not try to emulate a connection as UDP is connectionless. Jeff Garzik of exMULTI, Inc. proposed a UDP P2P protocol extension for Bitcoin that uses UDP bound to the same port as TCP P2P. He suggested that it could improve "inv" relay speed and block relay speed. Jeff Garzik also mentioned Bittorrent's evolution of a full transfer protocol over UDP to bypass firewalls and similar obstacles. The discussion then moved towards the usage of UDP and signaling acceptance of messages. The concern raised was regarding TCP/UDP port forwarding for transferring bulk data. UDT (UDP-based Data Transfer Protocol) was suggested as a solution to break the data transfer bottleneck, while SCTP (Stream Control Transmission Protocol) was considered for its advanced features such as multi-homing, message-oriented data transfer, and multi-streaming capabilities. In a conversation between Gregory Maxwell and Jeff Garzik, Gregory expressed that SCTP is an optimal transport for Bitcoin-like messaging, but it may face restrictions due to firewall practices. Jeff Garzik's rough draft implementation of a UDP P2P protocol extension for Bitcoin required an active simultaneous TCP P2P connection and allowed multiple P2P messages per UDP packet permitted. The project aimed to open up new design avenues for P2P patterns more suited to UDP than TCP in general. Jay F suggested the use of tunneling and SCTP for Bitcoin-like messaging in a conversation with an unknown recipient. The issue of TCP/UDP port forwarding was discussed. Various aspects of using newer user-mode transports that run over UDP were discussed in conversations between Gregory Maxwell, Jay F, Randy Willis, and others. This included their effectiveness in being tunneled and firewalls' restrictions on new protocols like SCTP. The feasibility of introducing super-nodes with thousands of connected peers was also discussed to enhance the capabilities of UDP. The conversation introduced more modern alternatives to UDP like UDT and SCTP. UDT uses UDP to transfer bulk data, while multiple UDT flows can share a single UDP port, making it firewall-friendly. On the other hand, SCTP is known for its advanced features such as multi-homing, message-oriented data transfer, and multi-streaming capabilities. The author of these opinions was not directly involved in the project but was trying to prevent someone from reinventing the wheel. It is worth noting that using these transport mechanisms may result in a data stream that looks like WEBRTC traffic. The discussion primarily focused on the use of newer user-mode transports that can run over UDP and be hole-punched and port-forwarded like TCP/UDP. However, some of these transports cannot be tunneled effectively, though most of them can.
