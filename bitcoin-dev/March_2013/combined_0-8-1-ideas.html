Summary:

The context discusses various proposals and discussions on the need for hard forks in the Bitcoin protocol. One proposal suggests patching pre-0.8 branches to support an increased lock count to avoid the problem from recurring, while another proposes a hard fork from block 262144 to block 393216. There are concerns about technical complications and risks involved in implementing these proposals. The issue of defining a hard fork in relation to the Bitcoin protocol is also discussed, with Mark Friedenbach and the writer of an email exchange arguing that a hard fork is a rule difference that can cause irreconcilable failure in consensus. Other topics discussed include the behavior of the Bitcoin protocol, the block size limit, and the need for client detection and warning about not-trivial forking. Despite the various proposals and discussions, it is unclear which solutions were ultimately implemented in the Bitcoin protocol.In another discussion, members of the Bitcoin-development mailing list discussed a proposal to handle a crisis without resorting to a hard fork. The proposal includes patching pre-0.8 branches to support an increased lock count and providing a non-standard patch to mining pool operators rejecting blocks that trigger the lock count limit. Mark Friedenbach and Luke-Jr discussed whether or not to add-to/modify the bitcoin protocol rules which all verifying implementations must adhere to. Another concern raised was the difficulties in getting people to update their systems away from version 0.7 and earlier. The development community responded explaining that critical fixes are backported to make updating instead of upgrading possible, but this still requires integration and testing, which can be time-consuming.In a discussion on March 13, 2013, Cameron Garnham expressed that many people who signed up for Bitcoin believed there was a 1MB block limit and were surprised by the lock limits. He argued that these limits were unexpected and should be removed as they were not described in the source code and any influence from external libraries on the consensus mechanism is unacceptable. However, it's important to note that the lock limits were explicitly set in the bitcoind source code.Finally, there are discussions about changing the definition of "6 confirmations" to be "six confirmations clear of any other chain" to address the failure to assure users that their transactions were confirmed. Additionally, there were concerns about client detection and warning in the case of not-trivial forking. Despite the various proposals and discussions, it is unclear which solutions were ultimately implemented in the Bitcoin protocol. Updating or upgrading Bitcoin software requires careful consideration of the risks and benefits, and critical fixes are backported to maintain compatibility with older versions.
