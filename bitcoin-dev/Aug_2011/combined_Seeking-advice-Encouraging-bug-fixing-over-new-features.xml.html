Summary:

The Bitcoin community is discussing how to encourage more bug-fixing and testing of the existing features instead of continuously adding new ones. One suggestion is to create a list of bugs and offer BTC bounties for fixing them based on their severity. Another idea is to set up a bug tracker unrelated to Github, and insist that patches won't be accepted without some new tests. Additionally, it was suggested to seed the test suite with a small number of simple tests for each part to encourage people to add more.The developers discussed offering grants to active developers and separating the program from the blockchain, which can help decrease the "people are making money off my back" feeling that stands in the way of new developers. Unit tests are critical for complex projects like Bitcoin, and insisting on patches without unit tests will be a good start to get people to add more. The discussion also revolved around the problem of "drop and run" coding where developers add new features and disappear without addressing bug reports or other feedback regarding their contribution.The Bitcoin development team discussed various strategies for improving the software and attracting new developers, including offering bounties for fixing bugs and finding security holes. Concerns were raised about the potentially unlimited amount of money demanded by GitHub's terms of service and the potential for competitive dynamics to emerge among contributors. The team also acknowledged that technical discussions alone cannot address larger questions of project direction and leadership.There were suggestions to sponsor and provide grants to active developers, separate the program from the blockchain, and accept patches for merged mining and alt chain stuff which doesn't directly benefit Bitcoin. The group believes that while competition for bounties could be problematic, it is important to find ways to attract developers and encourage them to contribute to the project. It was suggested to seed the test suite with simple tests for different parts of the codebase and insist that anyone who touches a piece of code introduces tests before a patch is accepted to pay down the bug debt and improve the overall quality of the software.Gavin Andresen, a developer of Bitcoin, asked for advice on how to encourage more bug-fixing and testing instead of adding features. He plans on leading by example by tackling the huge backlog of reported bugs himself. Suggestions include offering bounties for fixing bugs, making a list of bugs that need fixing, and maintaining a changelog file that credits bugfixes to contributors for the next few releases. It was noted that early adopters still have large stashes of BTC, making it hard for others to justify volunteering work on a codebase that will directly benefit them. Some propose sponsorship of developers through grants for active developers to bring them together, while others suggest policies like "that spiffy new feature you want won't be considered until you've helped close some open bugs".The Bitcoin community discussed ways to encourage bug-fixing and testing of existing functionality, including creating a list of bugs and offering BTC bounties for fixing them based on their severity. There were concerns about whether this approach would justify agreeing to GitHub's steep demand for potentially unlimited money in their terms of service. It was also suggested to offer BTC bounties for finding new security holes and serious bugs. The idea of publishing a roadmap is also mentioned. Another suggestion is to put a bounty on the person to lead the effort, rather than the bugs themselves. Additionally, there were discussions about hiring someone full-time to work on the software, creating a bug tracker unrelated to GitHub and developing a support network where companies pay for support. Seeding the test suite with simple tests for each part may be a good way to encourage people to add more tests. It was suggested that insisting on anyone who touches a piece of code introduces tests before a patch is accepted could help to pay down the bug debt. Finally, the development of unit testing infrastructure to assist complex projects is recommended.The discussion on hiring a full-time software developer for Bitcoin and offering bug bounties raised concerns about the feasibility of offering bounties for bugs and how to determine the amount and recipient of the bounty. There's also a suggestion to focus on squashing bugs before adding new features, which could be done through branching for bugfixes. A member suggested creating a page on bitcoin.org to attract developers to work on Bitcoin and proposing that they put a bounty on the test framework.Lastly, there is a list of C++ unit testing frameworks available on Wikipedia, which can be accessed at http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_unit_testing_frameworks#C.2B.2B. The user mentions that they already have the Boost testing framework implemented, but it does not have many tests.
