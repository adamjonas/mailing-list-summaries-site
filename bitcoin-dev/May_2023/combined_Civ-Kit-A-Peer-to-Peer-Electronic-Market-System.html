Summary:

In a recent discussion about the CivKit architecture for a peer-to-peer marketplace, front-running was identified as a concern. Front-running refers to taking advantage of advance knowledge of a transaction that will affect the price of a security. The discussion presented a scenario involving Mary, Terry, and Bob, where Bob, as a bulletin board operator, has the option to execute a trade before publishing it on his board. If Bob chooses not to execute, he relays the order to Terry, who can then execute the trade. However, if Terry also decides not to execute, Mary's order remains on the bulletin board. The concern raised was whether this model resembled a brokerage rather than an exchange market structure. It was suggested that Terry should open his own brokerage and compete with Bob by offering better prices.To address front-running resistance in a peer-to-peer marketplace, the discussion proposed two approaches. The first is to duplicate the announcement of offers to multiple concurrent boards operated by independent identities and monitor latency. Latency anomalies could indicate misbehaving boards, which can then be disqualified from future announcements. The second approach involves running the bulletin boards as a federation using consensus mechanisms to ensure fair publication of offers.The CivKit architecture aims to provide a flexible framework for a peer-to-peer marketplace, allowing customization of parameters such as propagation latency monitoring and federation set and rules based on the types of trades and market participants involved. The goal is to create a system that is resistant to front-running and other information asymmetries while providing a good user experience.In terms of reputation and incentivization, the discussion mentioned the possibility of using a Web-of-Stakes framework, which assigns economic weight to market entities based on their reputation. Additionally, privacy-preserving credentials backed by Bitcoin payments could be used to incentivize service operators to strive for efficiency.Antoine Riard, Nicholas Gregory, and Ray Youssef are developing a peer-to-peer market system that enables global trading without censorship or permission requirements. Their design combines the Nostr architecture with Lightning onion routing for confidentiality. The market boards serve as Nostr relays with a Lightning gateway and operate autonomously and in competition. Trades are escrowed under Bitcoin Script contracts, relying on moderation and know your peer oracles for adjudication.To address front-running resistance in peer-to-peer marketplaces, two security paradigms are proposed. The first involves duplicating offers to multiple concurrent boards and monitoring latency for anomalies. Misbehaving boards can be disqualified from future announcements if anomalies are detected. The second paradigm suggests running the bulletin boards as a federation under Honey Badger BFT, where incoming board offers become consensus items announced to all federation members.The scoring of trades and service operators is facilitated through the Web-of-Stakes framework, which assigns economic weight based on the Bitcoin UTXO set. This reputation paradigm can be combined with decentralized identifiers using Web-of-Trust techniques. An incentive framework for service operators is also proposed, utilizing privacy-preserving credentials backed by Bitcoin payments.Antoine, Nicholas, and Ray plan to release code and modules gradually, building on top of the Lightning Dev Kit and Nostr libraries. They aim to collaborate with the community to standardize libraries and ensure interoperability between clients. The whitepaper provides a detailed exploration of the system's architecture, and feedback on the project is welcomed.Overall, Antoine, Nicholas, and Ray are developing a flexible framework for peer-to-peer marketplaces that addresses front-running resistance and focuses on confidentiality, security, reputation, and incentives. Their goal is to create a system that supports various types of trading and encourages collaboration within the Bitcoin community.
