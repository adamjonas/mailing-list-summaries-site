Summary:

In a recent Bitcoin-dev email thread, the topic of raising the limit on OP_RETURN was initiated by Christopher Allen. It was concluded that there are no reasons to impose limits as people will find workarounds, and fees will regulate this. However, concerns about freeriders impacting the whole network and increasing decentralization among miners were also discussed.The discussion then shifted towards concerns about inscriptions being inserted between two specific flags, OP_FALSE and OP_IF. Moth proposed implementing a validation check to reject witness scripts that have arbitrary data between these two flags, preventing overloading of the network with transactions solely for ordinals and brc-20 tokens while still allowing all the benefits of taproot. Peter Todd argued that blocking such use cases is hopeless, noting that there are endless ways to embed arbitrary data in Bitcoin transactions. Additionally, the current flood of BRC-20 inscriptions does not require any data to be embedded in the chain at all.Regarding whether it was an oversight to lift the size limit for witness scripts as part of Taproot, there is no clear-cut answer. There is a concern that banning the storage of arbitrary/non-functional data could lead to people abusing useful data, causing more harm than good. It might be wise not to incentivize people to do something worse by attempting to censor what they're currently doing, given that it could be even worse.Finally, Christopher Allen expressed frustration with the free-rider problem caused by inscriptions combined with DOS attacks on Tor, making it expensive for him to host and maintain his free privacy service. He suggests finding a solution to this problem, but is unsure what it might be. Relevant threads on raising the limit on OP_RETURN were shared in the message, but it is unclear whether this would be a viable solution.
