Summary:

The topic of fee-bumping in Bitcoin transactions is discussed extensively in various posts, emails, and articles. The issue is critical for the security of many protocols built on Bitcoin, including Revault, which is designed to manage funds in a multi-party setup using off-chain transactions with a time lock that can be enforced on-chain if needed. One of the challenges with this protocol is fee-bumping, where miners increase feerates by triggering desperation-driven fee-bumping procedures, which could negatively impact users' experience and economic competitiveness.Proposed solutions include dropping the requirement on increasing absolute fees if the mempool is "full enough," introducing new consensus rules, or using a fee-bumping "shared cache." The post discusses strategies for choosing reserve feerate, including using percentiles, maximum historical feerate, or a maximum historical feerate adjusted in dollars. To manage the UTxO pool across time and merging consolidation with fanout, two distributions per vault are introduced: the "reserve distribution" and the "bonus distribution."Effective bumping and rebumping are necessary to ensure timely inclusion of transactions in the chain. The article suggests using `estimatesmartfee` to base estimates and re-bumping after each block connection. The paper presents their study and observations regarding different metrics such as time at risk. The email thread debates over various aspects of Revault and bitcoin network, analyzing the application of spending policies by watchtowers that can enforce certain rules such as preventing Unvault outside of business hours. The potential privacy issues associated with watchtowers are also mentioned.The discussion moves on to fractional fee-bumping reserves being more realistic to expect in the LN network and higher levels of reserve aren't worth the opportunity cost. Gloria Zhao, Darosior, and Ariard discuss the importance of fee-bumping for contract security, specifically focusing on the watchtower model, which applies spending policies through network monitors. They talk about improving lower-level RBF rules instead of requiring applications to write workarounds and generate extra transactions. They also discuss when to fee-bump and consider questions such as whether a fee-bumping strategy should be dependent upon the rate of incoming transactions and how close you are to your timelock expiry.The email thread discusses the Real Revault approach and its use-case, primarily how to claim back coins from a contract after a deadline before taking part in it. The article proposes a reserve feerate to limit the maximum feerate above which you cannot enforce all your contracts on-chain at the same time. The authors suggest a dynamic fee estimation model that takes into account transaction dependencies and aims to minimize the cost of enforcement. They also suggest strategies for choosing the reserve feerate and detail the problem of predicting the broadcast-time feerate market.Overall, the discussions emphasize the need for careful consideration of various factors in designing fee-bumping strategies for off-chain contracts and the importance of an insurance market to mitigate risks involved in enforcing such contracts on-chain. The post and email thread highlight the challenges associated with fee bumping in Bitcoin transactions and propose various strategies and solutions to address these issues, including dynamic fee estimation models, reserve feerates, and insurance markets.
