Summary:

CoinSwap is a privacy-enhancing technology that allows users to swap one cryptocurrency for another without revealing their transaction details. The protocol can be built into existing wallets, and offers better privacy than current equal-output CoinJoin apps while being cheaper for the same level of privacy. CoinSwap prevents transaction amounts from being linked by using hash-time-locked contracts, routing, multi-transactions, and private key handover.To improve the protocol further, it can be combined with Succinct Atomic Swaps (SAS), Pay-to-Endpoint (P2EP), liquidity market, routed CoinSwaps, breaking change output heuristics, fidelity bonds, multi-transaction CoinSwaps, federated message boards, and PayJoin with CoinSwap. Private key handover and SAS are suggested as methods to enhance privacy and anonymity in Bitcoin transactions. The use of fidelity bonds protects against sybil attacks and solves the "who goes first" problem in CoinSwap.In addition to discussions about improving the CoinSwap protocol, there were also conversations about scalability and privacy concerns in Bitcoin transactions. The email exchange between Lee Chiffre and Bitcoin developer, Chris Belcher, discussed the need for trustless coinswaps and how such a system could be developed without scalability issues. The discussion also highlighted two interesting concepts to improve privacy in Bitcoin transactions: atomic swaps and Pay-to-Endpoint (P2EP). Furthermore, the email conversation emphasized the importance of caution and patience when dealing with cryptocurrency transactions to ensure maximum security.Bitcoin is being used more as day-to-day money, an alternative approach called swap-on-receive+swap-on-change has been proposed by ZmnSCPxj in the email conversation. This method involves maintaining two internal wallets - a pre-mix wallet and a post-mix wallet - to amortize the timing of CoinSwaps so that sending payments is as fast as non-CoinSwap onchain wallets. The discussion also explores using PayJoin-with-CoinSwap for improved privacy, caution and patience when dealing with cryptocurrency transactions for maximum security, and improvements to the CoinSwap protocol to avoid theft and griefing attacks. The email conversations between various Bitcoin developers and experts discuss the privacy and scalability concerns of Bitcoin transactions. One proposed solution is the CoinSwap protocol, which allows users to swap their coins with others in a non-custodial way, enhancing user privacy and security by breaking the transaction graph heuristic. The protocol uses hash-time-locked contracts, routing, multi-transactions, and private key handover to prevent transaction amounts being linked and to swap coins without revealing identities or transaction amounts.CoinSwap can be combined with CoinJoin, and to defend against attacks, Bob maintains a list of "decoy UTXOs". In order to communicate, JoinMarket uses public IRC networks for communication. It is proposed that there be a small number of volunteer-operated HTTP servers run on Tor hidden services. Their URLs are included in the CoinSwap software by default. They can be called message board servers. Makers are also servers run on hidden services, and to advertise themselves they connect to these message board servers to post the makers' own .onion address.CoinSwap is a privacy protocol that can improve Bitcoin's fungibility and create a private system for sending transactions. The protocol involves decoy UTXOs to protect against leaks and a communication network using federated message boards protected from spam with fidelity bonds. CoinSwaps can be adopted unilaterally and offer much more privacy than the existing equal-output CoinJoin apps, breaking the common-input-ownership heuristic by involving inputs from different people. PayJoin-on-CoinSwap transactions can help individuals recover degraded privacy, particularly if they are spending from a reused or otherwise linked address. However, there are downsides to PayJoin-with-CoinSwap, including the possibility of spying and the need for makers to reveal additional UTXOs during the swap protocol.The proposal suggests a solution for enhancing privacy in Bitcoin transactions through Coinjoin. Similar to Monero's ring signatures, the use of decoys is employed to conceal the source of the transaction. However, this approach results in larger transaction sizes, which could lead to scalability issues. The protocol can be extended to include multiple parties, and transactions must be at least 12 times larger to achieve the desired level of privacy. It should be noted that while the protocol lacks nLockTime, it can still be utilized before the Spilman timeout with some loss of privacy due to nonstandard scripts. Ultimately, the proposal offers a potential solution to Bitcoin's current lack of privacy but raises concerns regarding scalability.
