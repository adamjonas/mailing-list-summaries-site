Summary:

The article discusses the potential impact of halving on the Bitcoin mining industry. It highlights the concerns raised by Alex Mizrahi about the catastrophic loss of hashpower and security after the reward halving if miner's income margins are less than 50%. However, the equilibrium break-even situation could prevent a hashpower drop of over 50%.The article also explores the estimation of miner's income margin (MIM) and concludes that if f > 1, MIM is less than 0.5. The discussion suggests that the next halving event could be different from the previous one due to the maturity of the market resulting in lower margins. Additionally, with the rise of profit-motivated mining and multipools like NiceHash and Multipool.us, there is an increased risk of double-spending attacks.The conversation highlights the need to consider all potential effects of halving the block reward and the importance of understanding the basics of Bitcoin mining. Different mining devices have different efficiencies, and newer and more efficient hardware may help to push the hashrate back up again within a reasonable timeframe. The article emphasizes the importance of game theory for business people in the evolving hashpower market towards financial instruments where the owner of the hashpower may not receive income. Market players also create agreements with miners, receiving negative value if hashpower is simply shut down.In the world of cryptocurrency, "Flag day" herd behavior is unlikely for well-informed and prepared market participants. It is considered rational to turn off mining devices until difficulty adjusts. The recent halving event didn't have a significant impact on the market, but it might be different next time.The Bitcoin subsidy halving is an event which is known to market participants in advance, and thus it shouldn't result in significant changes of the Bitcoin price. However, if miner's income margins are less than 50%, we might experience a catastrophic loss of hashpower, and more importantly, a catastrophic loss of security after reward halving.According to the simple model, if for a certain miner MIM (miner's income margin) is less than 0.5 before subsidy halving and bitcoin and electricity prices stay the same, then mining is no longer profitable after the halving. In this case, if the majority of miners have MIM less than 0.5, Bitcoin is going to experience a significant loss of hashing power.Different mining devices have different efficiency. After the reward halving, mining on some of these devices becomes unprofitable, thus they will drop out, which will result in a drop of mining difficulty. But, if before-halving MIM is close to zero and mining devices, as well as the cost of electricity in different places, are nearly identical, approximately half of all hashpower will drop out. However, there is still hope that it won't be critical since in the equilibrium break-even situation, hashpower drop is less than 50%.
