Summary:

The email exchanges among members of the Bitcoin community from 2014 discuss various vulnerabilities and potential solutions for the cryptocurrency network. One issue discussed is the creation of an arbitrary fee, which could incentivize miners to collect huge fees and even form temporary coalitions to claim the fee. Another topic discussed is the rational self-interest of miners and how it can lead to network fragmentation. The DECOR+ protocol is suggested as a solution that promotes cooperation among miners. Other tools such as locktime on normal transactions and block commitments in transactions are also explored. A potential attack called Chained Kickback Double-spend Attack (CHAKIDO) is discussed, which involves using Optimum Rational Best-chain Selection (ORBS) patch to perform a double-spend against a target like an exchange. Overall, the discussions highlight the importance of considering long-term stability over short-term gains and the need for protections against attacks.In an email conversation, Sergio Lerner described a vulnerability in the Bitcoin protocol known as "The Freeze on Transaction Problem," which occurs when someone publishes a transaction with fees exceeding the block subsidy. To address this problem, some tools were discussed, including the use of locktime on normal transactions, block commitments in transactions, and fee forwarding. Another proposal was to formalize the proportion of "rational" vs "honest"/"altruistic" participants in the network and analyze income tradeoffs for different levels of altruism. Another idea from TierNolan suggested that miners who own 1/N of the total hashrate must choose between two strategies if the last known block claimed a large tx fee of L. The first strategy is mining on top of that block and winning the usual reward R with probability 1/N, while the second strategy is mining on top of the previous block, trying to make two blocks in a row, which might get a reward of L with probability 1/N^2.The problem of creating an arbitrary fee can arise due to a bug or accident in defective wallet software, and not necessarily as an attack. Miners could be incentivized to collect these huge fees, which might provide a higher incentive than the block subsidy on the trunk. Addressing the known bug of the signature hash would have positive effects for resource-limited hardware wallets.In an email exchange between Tom Harding and Sergio Lerner, the topic of rational self-interest in Bitcoin mining was discussed. Lerner argued that if all miners acted rationally, then competing for high-fee transactions would be the most profitable strategy, even if it led to network fragmentation. The solution to this problem is the DECOR+ protocol, which shares block-rewards by including "uncles" and splitting the reward between miners at the same height until coinbase maturity is over.In an email exchange on October 7th, 2014, Gregory Maxwell discussed the potential issue of anonymous double-spends in Bitcoin. He proposed a method where the output of a fraudulent spend would be locked one block higher, and then that output would be spent again in the same manner, with each step paying fees. While this idea could potentially prevent double-spends, Maxwell noted that it was not automatic and relied on miners choosing to take the kickback payments.
