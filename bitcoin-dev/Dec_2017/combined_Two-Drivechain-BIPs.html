Summary:

The security model for drivechains and sidechains is a hotly debated topic among Bitcoin developers. In an email exchange, Paul Sztorc discusses the potential solution to the collective action problem inherent in fraudulent withdrawals from sidechains. The proposed solution involves using smart contracts to ensure collective action by recruiting accomplices and paying them cuts of the theft. The vulnerability in fraudulent withdrawals is addressed, with a proposal to restrict withdrawals to simple P2PKH or P2WPKH only. Additionally, the potential for miner centralization and collusion is debated, with arguments for and against such behavior.Furthermore, multiple discussions have taken place regarding the Drivechain proposal and its implications on Bitcoin's ecosystem. Matt Corallo expressed concerns about the lack of evidence regarding the "Risk of centralisation of mining" drawback in section 4.3 of the sidechains paper. Reddit users presented an analysis on blind merged mining (BMM) and drivechains, which highlighted the disconnect between voting and "blind" merge mining and outlined eight different strategies miners can use with BMM. The Drivechain project has released its code on GitHub, which means it is no longer just a concept. The article also discusses the potential outcomes of implementing a strategy to undercut competitors by upvoting only your own withdrawals and downvoting those of your competitors. The author proposes that P2PKH can be used to create a Theft Contract and an Accomplice Contract. On January 12th, Paul Sztorc announced that Drivechain has created a new source code and is no longer in the vaporware stage, with the documentation/BIP-Drafts ready for formal BIP number assignment and code review. Matt expressed concerns about the security model proposed in BIP 2, stating that it could further reduce hashpower-secured sidechains' security level.In this context, the author discusses a collective action problem that arises in the context of bankruptcy reorganization cases. The author explains that, in such cases, creditors may face a dilemma between accepting a settlement with the debtor or holding out for a better deal. This creates a collective action problem where creditors must coordinate their actions to achieve the best outcome. The author distinguishes between two types of bankruptcy cases: "reorg" cases and "will" cases. According to the author, the collective action problem is more difficult in "will" cases than in "reorg" cases. This is because in "will" cases, there is no ongoing business to preserve, and creditors are simply trying to get as much money as possible from the debtor's assets. This means that there is less incentive for creditors to coordinate their actions and more opportunity for free-riding. Overall, the author's analysis suggests that bankruptcy reorganization cases present complex challenges for creditors seeking to maximize their recoveries. While coordination can be difficult, it is crucial for creditors to work together to achieve the best possible outcome.
