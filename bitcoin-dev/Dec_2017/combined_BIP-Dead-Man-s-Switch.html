Summary:

The issue of lost Bitcoins due to human error such as losing private keys is a significant problem for the Bitcoin Improvement Proposal (BIP) system. It is estimated that out of the 16.4 million Bitcoins ever mined, about 4 million are lost forever due to unknown private keys of certain Bitcoin addresses. This leaves only 14.4 million Bitcoins in circulation, and with a cap of 21 million Bitcoins that can be mined, the total number of Bitcoins in circulation will eventually reach zero.To address this issue, the BIP proposal suggests implementing a "dead man's switch" causing dormant Bitcoin addresses to automatically expire after a fixed length of time, such as ten years. The aim is to combat the issue of lost Bitcoins. However, there are concerns over potential errors in moving funds around to avoid nullification, with cold storage being a safer option.Luke Dashjr has also proposed extending the range of relative lock-times to allow for several years, which could help address the issue of lost Bitcoins. However, increasing the divisibility of Bitcoins can only be done on 2nd layer solutions such as the Lightning Network, while on the base layer, it would require a hard fork that would undermine the invariants of Bitcoin.Implementing a dead man's switch could create terrible incentives for miners, causing them to censor transactions that move funds close to their expiration time to increase their own future rewards. Nick Pudar, a developer, has expressed concern about the potential negative consequences of such a system. He believes it could force people to move funds around, introducing multiple opportunities for errors, and would nullify the purpose of cold storage.Ultimately, discussions about these proposals have taken place among Bitcoin developers, but there is no way to eliminate the gradual loss of Bitcoins in transactions except for implementing a Dead Man's Switch or increasing the divisibility of Bitcoins. However, both solutions have their own limitations and drawbacks.
