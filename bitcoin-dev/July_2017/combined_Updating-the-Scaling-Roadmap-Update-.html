Summary:

Bitcoin's scalability issue is a challenge that the cryptocurrency community is grappling with. To address this, various technologies are being explored, including Segregated Witness, Lightning Network, Schnorr signature aggregation, MimbleWimble's shrinking block history, and drivechains. However, there is uncertainty regarding the effectiveness of these technologies, and a hard fork to increase block size may be necessary.Paul Sztorc, a Bitcoin developer, has proposed updating the Core Scalability Roadmap created in December 2015. The new roadmap aims to increase Bitcoin's maximum tps rate or make it easier to process a higher volume of transactions. The technical community has completed several items on the Dec 2015 roadmap, such as VersionBits (BIP 9), Compact Blocks (BIP 152), and Check Sequence Verify (BIP 112). Segregated Witness (BIP 141) and Lightning Network are complete and await activation. Transaction Compression, Schnorr Signature Aggregation, and Drivechain, which allows bitcoins to be temporarily offloaded to 'alternative' blockchain networks, are currently under peer review and may be usable by the end of 2017.If the outlined capacity improvements prove insufficient, a hard fork to increase block size by a moderate amount may be necessary. The draft of the updated roadmap has been uploaded to GitHub for comments and expressions of interest. However, without interest from the maintainers of bitcoincore.org, the document may not gain traction.Alex Morcos and Peter Todd urge caution when adding new, untested ideas to the Bitcoin roadmap due to potential risks involved. Proper security peer review and testing are necessary before implementing new ideas. Todd cites his own Treechains work as an example of an idea generating excitement but proving more challenging to implement than anticipated. He argues that Drivechains is currently in a similar situation and emphasizes the importance of taking time to ensure proper security peer review.An email exchange between David A. Harding and Paul Sztorc discussed the possibility of a document gaining traction on BitcoinCore.org. Paul expresses concern that without interest from the maintainers of bitcoincore.org, the document may be unable to gain traction. David suggests that if Paul wants his document to appear on the website, he should open a PR. He also indicates that while the community of Core Contributors ultimately decides in a philosophical sense, it is up to the maintainers of BitcoinCore.org in a practical sense to decide whether a standard has been met.
