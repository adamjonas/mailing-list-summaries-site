Summary:

The Bitcoin-dev mailing list has been a forum for discussions on Segregated Witness (SegWit) support. Some users have expressed their desire to run Bitcoin software without SegWit, while others have explored the potential ramifications of disabling enforcement of SegWit rules. While there are legitimate reasons for not using SegWit, such as limited bandwidth and resources, those who disable it entirely risk accepting blocks that nobody else in the network will accept, leaving them open to higher risks of double spends.Dan Libby notes that even if one does not upgrade to SegWit, there is no avoiding receiving UTXOs that were previously encumbered by a SegWit output. However, old nodes make the transaction appear always spendable for them, regardless of whether it really was spendable or not, in order to maintain backward compatibility. Gregory Maxwell responds to someone questioning why they would want to opt out of SegWit compliance, noting that it is not simple to disable SegWit correctly and that it isn't a supported configuration.Hampus Sjöberg explains that old nodes do not understand the witness program encoded in a new format, so a number greater than zero is put on the stack, which will be evaluated to true and counted as a valid spend. However, this creates a risk for those running legacy clients as they can be tricked with "zero confirm" transactions. To be certain, the only way to avoid receiving any other UTXOs is to run a node that has never published an address to a third party. Another option could be modifying their own node to reject such transactions, but this would hardfork the network. It may be possible without modifying any rules to construct a "certain balance" and an "uncertain balance".The discussion also revolves around disabling SegWit activation in Bitcoin Core releases prior to 0.13.1, with some users requesting a way to avoid SegWit activation until they personally feel comfortable with it. However, this is not a supported configuration and may cause unintended consequences. Some users have suggested keeping the cost of their node lower by running a non-SegWit node, but this approach may not be practical in the long run. One user even considered modifying their own node to reject SegWit transactions, but this would defeat the purpose of remaining with status-quo rules.Ultimately, the conversation highlights the importance of understanding the new witness program and consensus rules associated with SegWit to ensure the security and validity of bitcoin transactions. It is important for users to fully understand its implications before making a decision. The discussion acknowledges the potential drawbacks of SegWit compliance such as increased bandwidth requirements and the need for up-to-date nodes, but also emphasizes the importance of SegWit compliance for full verification of ownership in Bitcoin transactions.
