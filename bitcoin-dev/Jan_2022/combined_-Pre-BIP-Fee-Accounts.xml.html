Summary:

Bitcoin developer Jeremy Rubin has proposed an "extension block" account system as an alternative to the current transaction fee model in bitcoin. The system defines a special output type called a "fee account" that could store all deposits in a separate UTXO database for fee accounts and sign only two kinds of transactions: either a fee amount and a TXID (or Outpoint) or a withdraw amount, a fee, and an address. Rubin believes this design would improve the privacy of bitcoin transactions by allowing fee account paying to be unlinked from the transaction being paid for.The proposal aims to simplify the API, fix dust leakage for eltoo-like protocols, and enable support for real-world use cases such as Fee Bumping stuck transactions, DoS-resistant Payment Channels, and other long-lived Smart Contracts that cannot predict future fee rates. The Lightning Network is used with PTLCs to allow another individual to sponsor a transaction on your behalf only if they reveal a valid signature from their fee-paying account.In addition, Rubin proposes modifying the design to improve privacy by implementing something like Tornado.cash or allowing trustless mixing to be done by miners automatically. He also suggests adding trustless mixing operations that allow miners to automatically split groups of accounts with similar values into a common denominator and change, adding better privacy. However, the author notes that this type of design could be done as a federated network that bribes miners, potentially interfering with normal mining.The conversation also revolves around a new type of pinning attack called "necromancing," which is distinct from other types of pinning attacks. The speakers suggest that Bitcoin standards should avoid making unwanted necromancy a cheap and easy attack. They discuss various proposals, such as using a Tapscript version for fee-account-authorization which would allow feebumping without requiring an explicit output on the initial transaction, saving blockspace.Additionally, the conversations touch on topics such as DoS-resistant payment channels, multiparty RBF, and the potential use of a verifiable stealth address-like protocol that would allow for fee balances to be traced. The Lightning Network can also be used in conjunction with PTLCs to allow for another individual to sponsor a transaction on your behalf. The conversation also discusses the potential for a personal preference versus technical requirement when it comes to doing one transaction with all pending commitments at a particular time rather than mining two transactions for a specific set of commitments.The email conversations between Rubin, Peter Todd, and other individuals discuss various proposals for improving the fee-paying semantics in Bitcoin. The conversation suggests implementing fee accounts or similar mechanisms to prevent necromancy and pinning attacks. They also discuss using Child Pays for Parent (CPFP) to solve a complicated issue with relative locktimes in Bitcoin Vaults. To CPFP, a separate output just for CPFPing is needed or some sort of RBF-able addable input/output or sponsors. Two existing forms in Bitcoin include Replace-by-Fee and Child Pays for Parent. Overall, Rubin's proposal for an extension block account system could potentially improve the fee-paying semantics in Bitcoin and offer better privacy and scalability. However, there are concerns about potential attacks on transactions and the centralizing effects of implementing the proposal as a federated network that bribes miners. The discussions also cover various topics related to blockchain technology, including the definition of "necromancing," a pinning attack where an out-of-date version of a transaction is mined, and potential attacks on Bitcoin transactions.
