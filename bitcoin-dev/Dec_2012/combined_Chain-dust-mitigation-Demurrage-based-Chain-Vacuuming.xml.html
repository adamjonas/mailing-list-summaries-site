Summary:

The article discusses various email conversations related to Bitcoin, covering topics such as the issue of "dust" in the blockchain, the importance of privacy in Bitcoin, and the proposed feature that would enable users to switch between different coin selection policies. Michael Gronager proposed a way to address the issue of "dust" in the blockchain, which accounted for a significant portion of unspent transaction outputs (UTXOs). Specifically, 11% of UTXOs were worth only 1 Satoshi (0.00000001BTC), with 32% being less than 0.0001BTC and 60% being less than 0.001BTC. The main source of these payouts was Satoshi Dice, and while there was nothing inherently wrong with this, it could potentially lead to too many small payments clogging up the blockchain. To mitigate this problem, Gronager suggested introducing demurrage, not in its traditional sense as a percentage over time, but as a means of recycling small amounts of bitcoin over time.A proposed feature would allow users to switch between different coin selection policies, such as minimizing fees, maximizing privacy, defragmentation, or a general "I don't care" option. This feature could greatly enhance user experience by allowing them to tailor their coin selection process to their specific needs and preferences. However, it remains to be seen how well it would work in practice and whether it would introduce new issues or complications.The discussion also included proposals to improve Bitcoin's anonymity by cleaning up dust from any address by default, incentivizing reductions in the UTXO set size, and incorporating a "destroy wallet" button for people with wallets that only have dust remaining. The importance of separating "anonymity" from "privacy" in Bitcoin was also emphasized, as privacy is a feature demanded by users while anonymity may not always be necessary or useful.Additionally, Michael Gronager proposed introducing demurrage as a way to recycle small amounts of bitcoin over time, but this would require imposing some lifetimes on colored coins. In an email conversation, there was a suggestion to improve anonymity in the bitcoin network by cleaning up dust from any address by default while still allowing users to disable that feature. It's important to separate anonymity and privacy in the network to avoid confusion, and privacy is supposed to be a core feature of the network. Additionally, the messaging system of the payment protocol spec has a solution to avoid cluttering the blockchain with unnecessary outputs.It's noted that Bitcoin is not a democracy, and every full node independently validates the rules of the system without influence from others. A change in the system requires near-universal consensus that it is essential. There are concerns about problem transactions and unowned bitcoins, but potential solutions include making client software aggressive about sweeping up dust inputs, changing the default relay and mining rules to penalize transactions with very small outputs, incentivizing reductions in the UTXO set size, and incorporating a "destroy wallet" button for people with wallets that only have dust remaining.
