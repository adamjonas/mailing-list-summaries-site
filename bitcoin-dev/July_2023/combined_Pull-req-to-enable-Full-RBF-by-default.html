Summary:

The implementation of full Replace-by-Fee (RBF) in Bitcoin transactions has raised concerns about its impact on merchants and users who rely on zero-confirmation transactions (0-conf). To address this issue, a "first seen safe" rule is suggested, allowing transactions to be replaced but requiring the inclusion of the original outputs. GAP600, a service provider for BTC transactions, has observed a significant usage of their platform with approximately 350k unique transaction hashes per month over the past three months. Notable clients of GAP600 include Coinpayments, Coinspaid, and Changelly. Despite increased fees during high transaction volume periods, GAP600 maintains that their current system functions without major issues.GAP600 claims that they have not observed any impact on double spend rates for their transactions due to the full RBF implementation, contradicting the widely reported figure of 40% adoption of RBF by miners. They anticipate that once full RBF becomes the default setting, the adoption of their service may significantly change. It is important to note that GAP600 does not function as a payment processor but rather serves payment processors, merchants, and non-custodial liquidity providers catering to non-custodial wallets. Accuracy in predicting double spends is crucial for GAP600, as they reimburse their clients in case of any errors.The author of the context has submitted a pull request to Bitcoin Core to enable full-replace-by-fee (full-rbf) by default. Currently, around 40% of the total Bitcoin hash power is mining full-rbf replacements across 8 different pools. Several block explorers, including blockstream.info and mempool.space, have enabled full-rbf on all their nodes. Additionally, nodes installed via BTCPay Server and MyNode also enable full-rbf by default. Measurements indicate that a significant percentage of nodes have manually enabled full-rbf, and there is a well-connected set of nodes running the author's full-rbf peering patch.Successful full-rbf replacements are fairly common, as shown on https://mempool.space/rbf#fullrbf. However, the fact that only a minority of nodes relay full-rbf replacements poses a barrier to taking advantage of them in multi-party transactions. The author believes that enabling full-rbf by default will address this issue.During previous discussions on full-rbf, the only opposition came from Bitrefill, who claimed to have an actual use for unconfirmed transactions. However, the author has found no evidence that Bitrefill still accepts unconfirmed transactions as payment, despite their payment page claiming otherwise. All test transactions conducted by the author required confirmation.On-chain wallets have been moving towards removing the ability to set non-BIP125-rbf transactions. For example, Electrum removed the ability to turn off BIP125 last year, and other wallets like Phoenix, Green, Nunchuck, and Zeus also do not provide a way to turn off BIP125. These wallets consider "non-replaceable" transactions to be a support headache.The author argues that the dream of "on-chain coffee payments" is dead and that there is no value in maintaining the BIP125 distinction when a significant portion of hashing power ignores it. Enabling full-rbf by default in Bitcoin Core's GitHub master, to be released in v26.0, is proposed. Subsequently, the author suggests deprecating and eventually removing all BIP125 code and associated complexity in future releases.Overall, the implementation of a first seen safe rule and considering the specific use cases of services like GAP600 could help avoid the potential negative impact of full RBF on merchants and users who rely on 0-conf transactions. References provided in the context offer further information on block explorers enabling full-rbf, nodes running full-rbf, multi-party transactions with full-rbf, opposition to full-rbf from Bitrefill, removal of non-BIP125-rbf transactions in on-chain wallets, and the value of enabling full-rbf by default.
