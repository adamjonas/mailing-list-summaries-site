Summary:

In a recent discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, participants explored various aspects of blind signing protocols and their implementation. One topic of discussion was the vulnerability of blind MuSig protocols, particularly MuSig1, to attacks such as Wagner's attack. The importance of nonce commitment and determining the message before sending the nonce to avoid vulnerabilities was emphasized. Alternative schemes involving commitment, zero knowledge proof, and signature exchange were suggested, but further research is needed in this area.Another focus of the discussion was the incorporation of two-factor authentication (2FA) into blind signing. While the protocol proposed using 2FA as authorization for blind signing, concerns were raised regarding potential vulnerabilities, including attacks on K values and the absence of a blinding step. These vulnerabilities could potentially allow an attacker to extract the server's key or gain access to the message being signed. Participants shared their insights and observations to refine the protocol and address these security concerns.The implementation of blinded statechain protocols using Schnorr MuSig2 was also discussed. The goal was to have a fully blinded server that does not learn sensitive information such as the aggregate public key, aggregate signature, or the message being signed. The security of the system relies on trusting the statechain server to report the number of partial signatures it has generated, which are then verified client-side. Concerns were raised about the vulnerability of the scheme to certain attacks due to the lack of multiple 'R' points per signatory. References were provided for alternative approaches and insights into blind Schnorr signatures.The discussion also touched upon the need for proof of secret key (PoSK) when transmitting public keys and the potential use of blind signing schemes. However, it was acknowledged that PoSKs are not a panacea and can introduce other issues, such as the modification of the final key by the last person contributing a key.Furthermore, there was a discussion on the security of blinded statechains and the verification process for statecoin transfer. Measures were suggested to ensure that the recipient retrieves current public keys, verifies the address of the statecoin, and ensures that the sender has the private key used to generate X2. This ensures that the address was generated with the cooperation of the server and that no previous owner holds the full key.Overall, the discussions analyzed the proposed protocols, identified potential vulnerabilities, and shared insights to refine the protocols and address security concerns. The participants highlighted the challenges and complexities involved in implementing certain features and emphasized the need for further research in these areas. The ongoing efforts reflect a commitment to enhancing the privacy and security features of the Bitcoin protocol.
