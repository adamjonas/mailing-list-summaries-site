Summary:

Alfie John, a member of the bitcoin-dev group, expressed concerns regarding the potential for a middleman to suppress the initial 'encinit' message during negotiation in BIP 151, leading to both peers falling back to plaintext. The reduction to plaintext is not an interesting attack vector for active attackers, as they can simply impersonate the remote side. However, authentication is addressed through a separate specification that builds on this one, which provides protection against passive attackers. Gregory Maxwell suggested negotiating a secure channel from the outset or backing out entirely with no option of falling back, and indicating this loudly by the daemon listening on an entirely new port.In a discussion thread dated June 9, 2016, Gregory Maxwell discussed an attack vector where plaintext reduction is not interesting for active attackers as they can impersonate the remote side. Authentication is addressed through a separate specification that builds on this one. Alfie John asked if there are any links to discussions on how authentication may be done. Jonas Schnelli suggested reviewing his current work on Auth-BIP and provided a link to his work on Github and the most recent MITM/auth discussion.In a bitcoin-dev mailing list, Jonas Schnelli shared that he is working on an Auth-BIP but it is not ready for review yet. However, he provided the link to his current work on Github for anyone who wants to take a look. Alfie John thanked Jonas for providing the links. The author of the message believes that BIP 151 is a good idea but raises a concern about the initial 'encinit' message being suppressed during negotiation, causing a fallback to plaintext. In conclusion, the discussion highlights the importance of negotiating a secure channel from the outset, indicating it loudly with the daemon listening on an entirely new port, and the need for authentication to protect against passive attackers. Jonas Schnelli's work on Auth-BIP is recommended for those interested in the topic.
