Summary:

The bitcoin-dev mailing list has seen numerous discussions on various topics related to Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs), including the use of Protobuf vs JSON in the Bitcoin P2P protocol, the need for subscriptions to be built into the protocol, and the inclusion of anti-money laundering (AML) and know-your-customer (KYC) tools. There is a desire for more flexibility and options in the Bitcoin P2P protocol and for subscriptions to be included in the protocol to facilitate Bitcoin's use as a real payment system.Erik Aronesty suggests using JSON messages instead of protocol buffers for improved ease of use and extensibility, but notes that both options have their flaws. He also proposes alternate schemes for merchant identification and recommends BIP0047 payment codes over wallet addresses for subscriptions. The discussion touches on identity methods other than x509, with the suggestion that users should be able to identify themselves with any selection of identity systems. There is a debate on the participation in Anti-Money Laundering (AML)/Know Your Customer (KYC) compliance, with some arguing against it due to its negative impact on fungibility and privacy. However, Justin W. Newton defends AML/KYC compliance as one of the use cases that BIP 75 and certificates can support, based on open source and open standards. Peter Todd argues against the participation and suggests removing BIP75 from the bips repository and boycotting wallets that implement it. Additionally, the practicality of maintaining infrastructure for payment protocols is discussed, with Erik Aronesty noting that it is impractical for end-users. There are proposals to extend the Bitcoin URI scheme for authenticated requests without requiring an https server, while also addressing issues such as multiple destination payment addresses and allowing merchants to refuse payments. The importance of subscriptions for Bitcoin to be used as a real payment system has been recognized, with a proposed model for standardized and secure delivery of invoices, receipts, and payment requests.The Bitcoin developer community is against implementing AML/KYC tools in Bitcoin due to difficulties in implementing global consensus. BIP75 was designed to allow voluntary identity exchange at the application level, protecting user privacy and fungibility, while still allowing those who want or need to exchange identity info the option to do so directly between peers. JSON and protocol buffers are being compared for efficiency, with protobuf favored over JSON despite its imperfections. The push for client supplied identification is not desirable as it negatively impacts fungibility and privacy.In regards to editorial control, some degree of editorial control is inevitable but should be restricted to objective processes rather than personal opinions. Peter Todd suggests removing BIP75 from the bips repository and boycotting wallets that implement it. Aaron Voisine defends BIP75 by stating that it gives parties transacting complete control over who they choose to share their identity information with. Companies or individuals who require AML/KYC can find ways and do it at their side isolated from the entire network.Overall, the discussions on bitcoin-dev highlight the need for continued improvements and updates to Bitcoin protocols to ensure ease of use, privacy, and security for users. The debates center around balancing privacy concerns and security measures with the practicality and usability of the protocol. There is a desire for more flexibility and options in the Bitcoin P2P protocol and for subscriptions to be included to facilitate Bitcoin's use as a real payment system, while ensuring the protection of user privacy and fungibility.
