Summary:

Bitcoin developer Russell O'Connor proposed the addition of two new Script operations, OP_CAT and OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACKVERIFY, which would enable several applications such as oracle signature verification, secure multiparty computations, transaction introspection, weak covenants, and more. He believes that these new opcodes are more useful than the SIGHASH_ANYPREVOUT and OP_CHECKOUTPUTHASHVERIFY proposals, which have limited use cases and complicate Bitcoin operations.There is a debate about whether to add OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACKVERIFY or rely on shortcuts like SIGHASH_ANYPREVOUT and OP_CHECKOUTPUTSHASHVERIFY. ZmnSCPxj suggests identifying common patterns of usage for OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACKVERIFY and proposing them as specific "optimized" opcodes could be a better approach. Anthony Towns suggested implementing a pure functional language that could be compiled down to SCRIPT and leveraged with OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACKVERIFY. The discussion also revolves around the use of OP_COSHV and its potential amendments, including replacing 'peeking at surrounding data' with a pushdata-like opcode, adding transaction reflection operations, and splitting number-of-input-verification and outputhash-verification operations into different opcodes.Meanwhile, there is a preference for OP_COSHV due to its simplicity and lower per-block bandwidth usage. The possibility of implementing it as a multi-byte pushdata opcode or with template optimization is also mentioned. The safety and efficiency of using OP_LEFT over OP_CAT is also noted.The proposal for OP_CAT and OP_CHECKSIGFROMSTACKVERIFY is seen as a way to enable generic building blocks for Bitcoin Script and embodies the idea of "programmable money." A lack of tools rather than a lack of use may be responsible for the infrequent use of certain features in Bitcoin Script. The discussion highlights the need for general-purpose tools that can be used for a variety of purposes, and the potential to optimize these operations for popular constructions that may arise in the future.
