Summary:

In 2012, Gregory Maxwell raised concerns about the lack of proper security discussion on the Electrum website. He pointed out that claims such as "You do not have to trust the server with your money" were factually incorrect and suggested that a detailed security discussion would help users better understand the software's security measures. After contacting Thomas, he was informed that Electrum had undergone significant changes and was now a simplified-payment-validation node with generally the same security properties as other SPV nodes. The new UI no longer hides confirmation status of payments. However, there are still areas for improvement in both the client and security communication to users.Mike Hearn also commented on the difficulty of communicating transaction confidence to users and the need for an open UI design to address this issue. He proposed adding one-liners that link to a page explaining different security models (full, SPV, superlight). However, finding someone trustworthy to work on the bitcoin.org website is difficult due to possible politicization of the matter. Overall, it was recommended that technical people communicate among themselves to adjust development focus to mitigate risks and offer cautions where necessary.The discussion primarily revolved around the security implications of thin clients, particularly Electrum. Thin clients must trust their servers and are susceptible to various attacks such as lying servers, monitoring, selective denial of service, and deanonymization. These risks are not widely known among general users and even some sophisticated Bitcoin participants. Moreover, Electrum's default user interface does not indicate when payments are unconfirmed, potentially leading to theft. The protocol between clients and servers is also unauthenticated cleartext JSON in TCP, making it vulnerable to passive attackers.The author proposes that users will transition to stronger client models due to better security awareness and caution from the community, rather than just improvements in the clients themselves. However, there is concern that bad press from thefts and insufficient explanations may hinder the adoption of thin clients and damage Bitcoin's reputation.
