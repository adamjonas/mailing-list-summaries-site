Summary:

In the email exchange between Ryan and Ruben, Ryan thanks Ruben for writing a proposal and mentions that the ideas discussed in the proposal are not as novel as he might think. The concept being discussed is known as "fraud proofs" which aims to reveal erroneous steps in computational processes. However, fraud proofs do not address the issue of data availability. The problem arises when someone claims that the data is unavailable, and the only way to verify this claim is by downloading the data. Malicious peers can cause others to download all the data, nullifying any bandwidth savings. Fraud proofs could still help reduce computation needs but do not fully solve the scalability issue.Ruben provides historical context by mentioning that fraud proofs were considered for inclusion in segwit but were abandoned due to the data availability issue. He also mentions a possible solution called PoW fraud proofs/softchains, which he believes is better suited for low-bandwidth mainchain nodes. Ruben suggests that data availability sampling through erasure codes is another option but acknowledges its complexity and fragility.In another part of the email, Ryan brings up the topic of implementing a two-way sidechain peg within the Bitcoin protocol. He proposes a solution that does not involve multisig federations or require miners and full nodes to possess knowledge about the sidechain or run a sidechain node. The proposed method involves conducting sidechain withdrawals directly through the miners. To prevent theft, a three-month period is mandated for peg-outs, during which all miners vote on the peg-out. This allows the community to respond in case of incorrect peg-outs or theft. Criticisms aside, Ryan finds this proposal viable and likely effective.Ryan then introduces the concept of Sentinel chains as a potential improvement to the process. Sidechain nodes would function as Sentinels and notify full nodes of thefts via a secondary network. These Sentinel nodes monitor the current state of Bitcoin blocks and mempool transactions, actively searching for invalid transactions that contravene sidechain consensus. They transmit these invalid transactions or blocks to public Nostr servers. Bitcoin full nodes can run a small daemon alongside Bitcoin Core to receive these notifications and ignore the invalid transactions. Full nodes can choose their own decentralized web of trust to receive updates from Nostr. This approach reverses the conventional model of two-way pegged sidechains by having sidechains monitor nodes instead of the other way around.Ryan concludes by summarizing the differences between drivechains and Sentinel chains. Drivechains require an initial consensus soft fork, involve withdrawals in three-month periods, and exclude Bitcoin full nodes from participation in sidechain consensus. Sentinel chains, on the other hand, do not require an initial soft fork, allow instantaneous withdrawals, enable individual withdrawals, and allow Bitcoin full nodes to engage in consensus. Ryan believes that Sentinel chains offer advantages in terms of speed, efficiency, and alignment with Bitcoin's principles.Overall, the email discusses the concepts of fraud proofs, data availability, and the proposal of implementing a two-way sidechain peg. It also introduces the idea of Sentinel chains as a potential solution.
