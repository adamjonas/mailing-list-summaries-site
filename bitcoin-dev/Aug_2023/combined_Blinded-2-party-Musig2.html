Summary:

A recent discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list explored the concept of "proof of secret key" (POSK) to enhance security when transmitting public keys. However, there are potential issues with using POSKs in MuSig, as it could allow the last participant to modify the final key without detection. Logistic difficulties also arise in producing POSKs. Instead, the MuSig and MuSig2 papers provide solutions for security concerns without the need for POSKs.In the discussion, the security of blind MuSig protocols is being examined, specifically in preventing attacks like Wagner's attack and ensuring signing process integrity. One suggestion is to include commitments to the nonce R in a third round of MuSig1. The use of challenge c in constructing s1 is also questioned, but using c1*a1 instead of c1*a1*x1 is proposed as a potential solution. This proposal takes advantage of the blockchain's ability to calculate 'c' and the aggregate key, making it difficult to correlate the transaction with just the quantity 'c1*a1' seen by the server. Additional guarantees, such as proof that the signing request is well-formed, are acknowledged as necessary for a blind signing scheme.The discussion also touches on integrating a proof of secret key into low-level protocols and mentions a modified protocol for key generation, signing, and statecoin transfer. The implementation involves fully blinding the server in a 2-of-2 Schnorr MuSig2 for statechains. Concerns are raised about the vulnerability of this modified scheme to specific attacks due to the presence of only one 'R' per party.Overall, the discussion revolves around improving the privacy and security features of the bitcoin protocol, particularly in blind signing schemes.
