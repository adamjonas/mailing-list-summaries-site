Summary:

In a recent Bitcoin Protocol Discussion, the topic of inscriptions in transactions was raised. The discussion focused on whether it is better to detect and standardize these types of transactions or to create new forms of spam transactions. It was noted that even if rules were implemented to prevent inscriptions from reaching miners, users could still send non-standard transactions or change the script used for inscriptions. This puts anti-inscription advocates at a disadvantage, as they would need a majority of the network to upgrade their relay policy, while pro-inscription users only need to convince a small number of enthusiasts and businesses to update.One approach suggested by a user named rot13maxi is to make the cost of blockspace more expensive than what inscribers are willing to pay, either through high fees or lack of market demand for inscriptions. They dismissed appeals to Satoshi's writings on spam as not compelling arguments.In response, another user named LÃ©o Haf questioned the usefulness of standardization rules if they can be bypassed by miners. They argued that the cat and mouse game with spammers can be won through detection and standardization. They also mentioned that integrating the anti-inscription patch into Bitcoin Core by default would increase its effectiveness as nodes update. They stressed the need to solve the Initial Block Download (IBD) problem and the growing UTXO set size, as blocking ordinals alone may not effectively discourage unwanted behavior.The discussion further delved into the challenges of preventing inscription spam, including the possibility of creating new nodes without synchronizing non-consensus data. Some participants expressed concerns about the effectiveness of adding a standardization option without a soft fork and the adaptability of spammers to any limitations imposed. There were also discussions on the implications of inscriptions on the Bitcoin network and the different perspectives on how to address them.In another discussion, the increasing use of inscriptions as a spam attack on the Bitcoin network was raised as a concern. Unlike other methods, inscriptions have no restrictions and can embed themselves in the witness with a fee reduction. To address this issue, a proposal was made to add a standardization option that would allow the community to decide whether to activate it or not.Some participants argued that maintaining the status quo would be the default approach if there were no good solutions to the problem. They emphasized the need to address the growing initial blockchain download time and UTXO set size. The discussion highlighted that blocking inscriptions in the mempool of a node would lead to an endless chase, as new types of inscriptions could always be created. It was noted that the use of inscriptions is primarily associated with selling NFTs or Tokens, concepts that the Bitcoin community has traditionally rejected.Overall, the discussions shed light on the challenges and considerations surrounding inscription spam on the Bitcoin network. While there are differing opinions on the effectiveness of blocking inscriptions and the potential unintended consequences, finding a solution is crucial for maintaining the integrity and efficiency of the network.
