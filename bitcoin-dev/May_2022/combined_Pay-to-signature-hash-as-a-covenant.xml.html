Summary:

The context explores the potential use of covenants in a pay-to-public-key (P2PK) scenario without necessitating any consensus changes. The author suggests experimenting with both P2PK and legacy signatures, but believes that Schnorr signatures will be more flexible and allow for a wider range of use cases. They propose a "pay to signature" script using OP_SWAP and OP_CHECKSIG, though they acknowledge that it is insecure and should be handled with care. Instead, they suggest replacing it with a signature hash, which would remain untouched, and discuss the flexibility of signatures over public keys.The author also suggests that using the right signature with the correct sighashes could potentially disable key recovery and require a specific public key, thus restricting the destination address. They are open to ideas on how to solve this issue and propose combining it with OP_CHECKSIGADD or wrapping it in multisig. The email also delves into a technical discussion on the circular reference problem for covenants and explains why pubkey recovery is impossible in BIP340 schnorr.In order to implement these covenants, the author proposes the use of P2WSH with a redeemScript containing an OP_CHECKSIG, which would pre-commit to a particular transaction based on the SIGHASH flags of the fixedSignature. The post-COVID congestion control application proposed for OP_CTV could work even in the absence of OP_CTV. The author also discusses a purported OP_CHECKSIGHASHVERIFY, which accepts a SIGHASH flag and a hash and checks whether the sighash of the transaction is equal to the hash. This technique should work with Tapscripts inside Taproot, but the fixedPubKey cannot be the same as the internal Taproot key.Overall, the context presents a detailed exploration of the use of covenants in P2PK scripts and the potential implementation challenges and solutions.
