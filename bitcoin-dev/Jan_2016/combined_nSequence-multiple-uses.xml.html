Summary:

Andrew is looking for clarification on the multiple uses of nSequence in Bitcoin transactions. He mentions that it can be used for locktime and opt-in Replace-by-Fee (RBF) signaling, but there is no way to differentiate between these uses, which could lead to unintended behavior. Andrew suggests using version bits to solve this issue. During a recent Bitcoin-development discussion, Andrew notes that spending a time-locked output requires setting nSequence to less than MAX_INT, but opting into RBF also requires setting nSequence to less than MAX_INT. Dave responds by explaining that opting into RBF requires setting nSequence to less than MAX-1 and provides a link to BIP125 for further information. An nSequence of exactly MAX-1 enables locktime enforcement without opting into RBF. Overall, Andrew seeks clarification on the different uses of nSequence in Bitcoin transactions and proposes using version bits to distinguish between them. During the discussion, Dave corrects Andrew's understanding of nSequence and explains that an nSequence of less than MAX-1 is required for opting into RBF.
