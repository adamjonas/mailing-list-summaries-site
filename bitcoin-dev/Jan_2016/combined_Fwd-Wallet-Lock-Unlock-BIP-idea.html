Summary:

The article discusses various aspects of wallet encryption and the proposed BIP by Scott Morgan to lock and unlock wallets in Bitcoin. The writer mentions that encryption support is available through RPC calls such as 'encryptwallet', 'walletpassphrase', and 'walletlock'. However, adding a noticeable flag to indicate locking or unlocking the wallet may not align with good privacy ethics. The writer suggests obfuscating the information through some one-way encryption for checking if a wallet is locked without being able to obtain a list of locked or unlocking wallets easily.Scott Morgan proposed a BIP to lock and unlock wallets in Bitcoin which could help reduce theft by adding a method to lock and unlock wallets. Users could 'lock' their wallet with an unlock period, which would get added to the blocks and confirmed like other transactions. During transaction creation and mining, the top blocks would be checked to see if the wallet is locked, and locked wallet transactions would not be confirmed. The proposal is different from bip-0065, which adds a lock time to a transaction that the recipient user must wait before they can spend the coins. Locking a wallet might incur a fee, and it could also slow transaction creation and mining a fair amount. Anyone could check the transactions to locked wallets to see how many BTC are being held or are being unlocked soon, which could affect the price of BTC in fiat as supply would change similar to the way mining changes it. The idea of locking entire wallet addresses indefinitely has huge implications for the entire Bitcoin ecosystem and probably needs a start date at least a year in the future after it was developed. The writer notes that Scott Morgan does not plan to work on the main bitcoin core as he is a Privacy and Java evangelist. He has been doing a little mining to attempt to help fund his company's open source Java projects Tests4j and Fabricate and hopefully in the future Taxi, Sanctum and Intelligence4j.
