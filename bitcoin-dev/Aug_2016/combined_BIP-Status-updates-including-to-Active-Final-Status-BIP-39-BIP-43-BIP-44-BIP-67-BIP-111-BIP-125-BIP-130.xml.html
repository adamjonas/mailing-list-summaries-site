Summary:

The discussion in this context revolves around the Bitcoin Improvement Proposals (BIPs) and whether they should be treated as final or not. Luke Dashjr, a Bitcoin Core developer, has proposed updating several BIPs to Final Status, including BIP 39, BIP 44, BIP 67, BIP 125, and BIP 130. BIP 39 is used by many wallets and hundreds of thousands of users, while BIP 44 appears to be implemented by multiple wallets. However, there are concerns raised about the broken state of Simplified Payment Verification (SPV) wallets that don't have bloom filtering detection. SPV wallets must update to respect NODE_BLOOM as bloom filtering is CPU and disk intense, and some node operators have disabled it.The email chain between Luke and Jonas Schnelli on the bitcoin-dev mailing list discusses the development paradigm of "maybe detect funds" and whether it should be encouraged for Bitcoin. There seems to be some confusion over the use of the word 'not' in one of the statements made by Jonas Schnelli. Additionally, there are concerns raised about the broken state of SPV wallets that don't have bloom filtering detection. This has caused panic among less technical users who end up rescanning the blockchain and losing their transaction history.Luke Dashjr, a Bitcoin Core developer, believes that BIP39 and BIP44 are poorly designed. The two proposals are used by numerous cryptocurrency wallets and hundreds of thousands of users, but there is limited support for them in Electrum. Dashjr has stated that he will not use BIP39/BIP44 for default Electrum wallets for several reasons. During a discussion on the bitcoin-dev mailing list, Luke Dashjr shared his views on BIP39 and BIP44. He mentioned that BIP 39 is a mnemonic code used to generate deterministic keys, which is used by many wallets and hundreds of thousands of users. BIP 44 is Multi-Account Hierarchy for Deterministic Wallets, which appears to be implemented by several wallets.A user posed a hypothetical scenario on the bitcoin-dev mailing list regarding what would happen if they generated 21 addresses and address0 received funds 180 days after generation while address21 immediately received funds. This would trigger a resize+20 of the address-lookup-window, but the wallet would need to go back 180 days to detect any transactions for address21 in a new lookup-window. This scenario is not unrealistic as a merchant might generate addresses beyond their gap limit and orders get filled at a later date. The lack of consideration for these cases is another issue with BIP44.Overall, the discussions reflect concern for potential issues and limitations in the current design of Bitcoin wallets. There are concerns around the implementation of BIP 111, or the NODE_BLOOM service bit, which has been added to Bitcoin Core and derivatives but is unclear whether clients are using it yet. The developers of such clients are requested to comment regarding whether their software supports this BIP, and if not, whether they intend to support it in the future.
