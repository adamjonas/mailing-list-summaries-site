Summary:

In the world of cryptocurrency mining, discussions are ongoing about ways to make Bitcoin mining "unASICable" and to create an ASIC-proof proof of work algorithm. One suggestion is to change the reward structure to allocate a portion of the reward to runner-ups, even if their block is orphaned. However, this could create downsides such as having to keep the orphaned blocks around in the future. Another idea is to use an IO-bound algorithm, rather than CPU-bound, to protect against ASIC mining. Dr. Andy Parkins suggests that by making the proof-of-work hash for a block hash of [NEW_BLOCK] [ALL_PREVIOUS_BLOCKS] [NEW_BLOCK], every byte of the blockchain must be fed through the hashing engine, making it IO-bound. A hashing algorithm called ROMix is also discussed, which requires a different 32 MB of blockchain for each hash, uniformly distributed throughout the blockchain.The conversation then shifts to the production of competitive mining equipment, with one user noting that if POW is most efficient on general-purpose CPUs, only entities like Intel, AMD, and IBM would be able to produce such equipment. In response to the idea that it is impossible to have an ASIC-proof proof of work algorithm, Dr. Andy Parkins suggests that while it may be true that no algorithm can exist that cannot be implemented in an ASIC, it is only true for algorithms that are CPU bound. To protect against ASIC mining, the algorithm could instead be IO-bound, rather than CPU-bound. By making the proof-of-work hash for a block hash of [NEW_BLOCK] [ALL_PREVIOUS_BLOCKS] [NEW_BLOCK], in which [ALL_PREVIOUS_BLOCKS] is 20GB and growing, every byte of the blockchain must be fed through the hashing engine, making it IO-bound.There are also discussions of making mining IO-hungry instead of memory hungry by using ROMix, which would require miners to read 20 GB of data on every hash even if only a small fraction of the data is used for each hash. Additionally, an idea proposed in 2014 to create an ASIC-proof proof-of-work algorithm was deemed not workable due to several reasons, though ways to make it work with fewer tradeoffs have been suggested. However, specialized high-efficiency hardware for mining may still exist, potentially creating an advantage for earlier/larger participants.The importance of keeping algorithms simple and ASIC-easy is reiterated as it promotes diversity in the marketplace and prevents the ASIC advantage from being concentrated into the hands of fewer people. Discussions around the limitations and potential issues of these ideas are important for learning and advancing the technology. The overall theme of the discussion is the ongoing pursuit of efficiency and fairness in cryptocurrency mining, with different perspectives and ideas being shared on how to achieve these goals.
