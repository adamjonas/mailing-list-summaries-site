Summary:

Gem, a company that offers wallets for various cryptocurrencies, is proposing a new hierarchical deterministic (HD) wallet path structure that separates the coin_type field into two fields - network and asset_type. This change would enable validation of address formats, appropriate daemons selection for scanning tokenized assets, and choosing multiple blockchain data sources relevant to an HDNode in the wallet using only information in the HDNode's path. The company is considering publishing this information in a blog post or requesting a BIP number and publishing an informational BIP specifying the new purpose.The discussion also revolves around the implementation of a new token layer on an existing blockchain, where the use of HD wallets can generate new token addresses. However, maintaining it could be challenging. Another member suggested using m/44'/9'/a'/c/i instead of having an internal mapping from 9'->0' to find out which blockchain to query.In an email exchange between two people named Matt, the use of a proposed path structure in HD wallets was discussed. The author expresses concerns over HD chain standards like Bip43/44 and suggests creating deep level chains like m/a/b/c/d/e/f/g/h may require more CPU power to derive private key with a given seed or wordlist. They question whether standards of HD chain structures lead users and developers in the right direction, citing examples of wallets that do not follow the Bip39 standard or support Bip44.Matt Smith from Gem discusses the need to store paths in the wallet database and proposes a path structure that separates the concept of network and coin type. He points out that there's no way to infer the path of an address inside an HD wallet from the address alone. Therefore, HD wallets need to store either the paths, addresses, or both that have been previously derived/used to monitor the blockchain. He expects multisig HD wallet imports to require master keys and a list of account paths rather than a list of addresses. He concludes that this use case might be very specific to their model, but the reason they should request a BIP number for this is that to start using it, they need to pick a number for the purpose field and not want to do it arbitrarily.The author raises concerns about HD chain standards like Bip43/44, saying wallet re-importing or recreating can be risky and lead to missing out on spent or spendable coins. Creating deep level chains requires more CPU power to derive private keys, and users of hardware wallets may feel the required time when signing/deriving. The author questions whether HD chain structures lead users and developers in the right direction and cites examples of wallets like Electrum and Breadwallet that do not follow Bip39 or support Bip44. Links to Bitcointalk.org and Github.com are provided for further information.
