Summary:

The Bitcoin community is actively seeking ways to improve the efficiency and security of the network by addressing the limitations and concerns around Replace-by-Fee (RBF) policies. Some proposed solutions include rate-limiting mechanisms, such as transaction relay rate limiting with a feerate-based priority queue and staggered broadcast of replacement transactions.However, there are concerns about enabling attackers to censor others' transactions or recycle fees without adding more fees to the transaction. Suggestions also include user-elected descendant limits on transactions and a "mining score" calculator to prioritize relay based on feerate.There have also been discussions on improving RBF policy, including requiring the ancestor score and feerate of replacement transactions to be higher than those of original transactions, enabling fair feerate bids for Lightning Network channels, and rate-limiting replacements per prevout and transaction validation per peer. However, there are concerns around introducing new pinning vectors and ensuring incentive compatibility and DoS protection.The addition of rate-limiting in transaction relay to prevent resource exhaustion and spam, as well as RBF DoS protection, was recently discussed on the bitcoin-dev mailing list. There were concerns raised about possible impacts on propagation and recycling of fees. DOS concerns were deemed overblown, and the goal was to design a system where spam is manageable. The discussion also revolved around implementing outbound transaction relay size-based rate-limiting and prioritizing transaction relay by feerate.Regarding wallets, it was suggested that it may be better to have the mempool acceptance rule only consider economic incentives, while spam prevention only concerns whether to inform peers about the transaction. In terms of keeping high-feerate evicted transactions around for a while to improve compact block relay, there is concern that the 100 tx LRU cache may not be sufficient. It is suggested that a rate limit applies only to replacement transactions.The post discusses various issues related to DoS protection, iterative/additive batching, and the use of verifiable delay function in rate-limiting transaction replacement. The first point suggests limiting the amount of work required for replacing a transaction rather than focusing on the number of times a transaction can be replaced or the volume of transactions that can be evicted during a replacement.The second point highlights the benefits of iterative batching, where an organization puts a transaction in the mempool that pays to N users and updates it to N+2, N+2...N+M payouts as they see additional requests for payouts. The third point suggests using a verifiable delay function (VDF) over each of the N COutpoints individually to rate-limit transaction replacement, eliminating the VDF period depending on the feerate increase.Overall, various potential solutions are being explored to make Bitcoin more efficient and secure, with feedback being solicited on these proposals.
