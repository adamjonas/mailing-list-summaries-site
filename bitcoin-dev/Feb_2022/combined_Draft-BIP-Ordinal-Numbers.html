Summary:

The bitcoin-dev mailing list discussions cover various topics related to Bitcoin, including the use of ordinal numbers in transactions. Casey Rodarmor proposed a scheme called Ordinal Numbers for numbering and tracking satoshis across Bitcoin transactions. The proposal suggests splitting dust into unit satoshis and using them for NFTs or colored coins. Despite positive feedback, concerns arise about increasing payment precision by going sub-satoshi on chain and the potential issue of stolen or blacklisted coins.AdamISZ corrects the notion that increasing payment precision would inherently remove unconditional soundness from the system. The practicality of numbering each satoshi created is also questioned due to the large number of ordinal numbers that would be assigned. There is consideration of using zero satoshi as a substitute for hidden values in a Monero-like system to make it backward-compatible. However, there are concerns about connecting any future protocol based on that with ordinal numbers since ordinal numbers are inherently public and do not work with private or obfuscated transactions.Despite this, ordinal numbers provide an efficient way of tracking satoshis across transactions without explicit creation and have various potential applications such as NFTs, reputation systems, and Lightning Network-compatible stablecoins. It is unlikely for Bitcoin to adopt a feature that would hide coin amounts as it would remove unconditional soundness from the system, though backward compatibility with old style addresses could be maintained.When satoshis are spent in a transaction, the input satoshi ordinal numbers are assigned to output satoshis using a first-in-first-out algorithm. The conversation discusses how Bitcoin assigns ordinal numbers to each Satoshi created and how these numbers are determined when BTC is transferred to another UTXO. The use of ordinal numbers in Bitcoin allows the output of an ordinal without a transaction ID. While a full index can be expensive, it only needs to track contiguous ordinal ranges.There is consideration of increasing payment precision by going sub-satoshi on chain, which could be extended to ordinals through the use of fraction ordinals like 1.1 or 4.85. The adoption of ordinal numbers will increase the demand for transactions and drive up fees, which is a strong positive of the proposal. There is also discussion about introducing a Monero-like system that hides coin amounts, but it is noted that such a change would make it impossible to audit the blockchain and verify that there hasn't been inflation.In the world of cryptocurrency, a zero satoshi input or output holds no value and can be ignored when using an ordinal index. These inputs or outputs carry no ordinals, making them insignificant in calculations involving cryptocurrencies like Bitcoin. The BIP defines the ordinal ranges for transactions with duplicate IDs, following the behavior of Bitcoin Core. The need for this rule is being explained in more detail in the BIP. Overall, the discussions highlight the challenges and considerations involved in implementing changes to the Bitcoin system, especially in terms of maintaining backwards compatibility and ensuring soundness and security.
