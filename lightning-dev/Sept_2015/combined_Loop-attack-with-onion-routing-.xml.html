The context discusses various aspects of Bitcoin transactions, including the impact of blockchain fees and the challenges associated with onion routing. Rusty Russell proposes peeling the onion in a scenario where Alice sends a payment to Emma via Bob, Carol, and Dave. The Lightning Network is also discussed, with potential attacks identified, such as a DoS attack that forces nodes in the path to lock up N bitcoins and routing payments back to another channel owned by the attacker.Rusty suggests using a scheme where a valid unilateral close transaction is required, with the threat of closing channels as a disincentive. The conversation also covers potential collusion between parties involved in a Lightning Network transaction. Finally, there are discussions about the risk model for Lightning Network's payment channel system, per-hop contracts, and resolving hashed time-locked contracts (HTLCs).The conversation among Lightning Network participants covers several topics, including per-hop contracts and fines for delayed R values. Non-trusted neighbors could pre-pay a fine fund before routing payments towards them to prevent issues when misbehaving nodes do not pay their fines. Onion routing and source routing are discussed in relation to privacy in payments. Independent onion routing from both sides of a payment transaction is proposed, requiring end-to-end communication in real-time.Concerns about a potential attack on the Lightning Network are raised, involving routing a payment back to another channel owned by the attacker and then refusing to disclose R. This could lead to graph centralization. The primary issue with "onion" routing is that some parts of the graph may be faster with disclosure of R. Possible solutions are proposed, including establishing blame via information disclosure and sending funds to each participant with multiple signatures for different times of disclosure of R. However, no clear solution is finalized at the time.Overall, the context provides a detailed exploration of the complexities involved in Bitcoin transactions and the challenges faced by users and intermediaries alike. Suggestions are made to ensure enforcement of fine payment, and potential attacks on the network are identified, with ways to mitigate such risks proposed. The trade-offs of having both options available create emergent cartelization incentives.
