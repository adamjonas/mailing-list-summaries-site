Summary:

The introduction of dual funded transactions in Lightning has presented challenges in protecting against liquidity griefing attacks. These attacks occur when a peer locks their UTXOs, preventing their reuse if the peer goes silent. To address this issue, it has been proposed that UTXOs should never be locked in dual funded transactions, allowing them to be automatically reused if needed. However, this solution creates problems when using 0-conf as accidental double-spending can lead to fund loss.To mitigate these concerns, alternative solutions have been suggested. One suggestion is to lock UTXOs only if the node is the sole contributor. This approach would prevent accidental double-spending in 0-conf channels. Another concern is the isolation of UTXOs used for 0-conf and non 0-conf funding attempts. To address this, a "soft lock" could be used when selecting UTXOs for non 0-conf funding attempts.The idea of adding 0-conf support to Lightning was also discussed. While some developers appreciate the benefits of 0-conf, others have expressed security concerns about its implementation. The discussion on this topic is ongoing.In addition, the concept of swap-in-potentiam addresses was introduced as a way to ensure the safety of dual-funded 0-conf transactions. By using these addresses with the acceptor, liquidity griefing can be prevented from being cost-free. The expected use-case for these transactions is when on-chain operations are slow and multiple confirmations are required. Trust issues between the initiator and the acceptor were also addressed, suggesting possible solutions like the LSPS3 Promise or trusting the acceptor in the dual-funding flow.Overall, the discussion emphasized the need for safeguards against liquidity griefing in dual funded transactions, particularly when using 0-conf. Various proposals and solutions were put forward to address these concerns, including the use of soft locks, isolation of UTXOs, and implementing 0-conf support in Lightning. The email thread sought to gather ideas and suggestions for improving the situation and offering better protections against liquidity griefing for nodes offering 0-conf services.
