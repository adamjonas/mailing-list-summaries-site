Summary:

The Lightning-dev mailing list is a platform for discussing and developing the Lightning Network, which aims to improve the scalability and transaction speed of the Bitcoin blockchain. The discussion on the mailing list revolves around mitigating channel jamming using HTLC endorsement and reputation. However, reputation systems have their limitations due to sudden behavioral changes and whitewashing attacks.Antoine Riard suggests establishing reputational transitivity between incoming and outgoing traffic to mitigate vulnerabilities. Carla Kirk-Cohen proposes gathering data about the use of HTLC endorsement and local reputation tracking for jamming mitigation and experimenting with different algorithms for tracking local reputation. Carla explains that each node locally tracks the reputation of its direct neighbors and allocates slots reserved for endorsed HTLCs from high reputation peers, forming the first step in mitigating channel jamming. Antoine emphasizes building data gathering infrastructure for Lightning, as any jamming mitigation will have to adapt its upfront fees or reputation acquisition cost based on market forces.The Lightning Network community is actively working towards mitigating channel jamming attacks through various schemes and algorithms. The HTLC endorsement scheme is vulnerable to abuse during high-fee periods, and the community suggests ensuring reputational transitivity to prevent this. Nodes are proposed to track local reputation for their direct peers based on their history with all HTLCs a peer has forwarded to them. The acquisition cost of local reputation should be equal to the maximum time-value damage that one can inflict on a routing node channel accessible from its local counterparty granting this high-level of reputation.The conversation explores the impact of an attack on large routing nodes, where there is sufficient data available for them to make informed decisions. However, small and low-activity nodes will temporarily compete for large routing nodes' scarce liquidity and slots but can still interact with similar nodes where they have better chances of building a good reputation. Reputation revenue is used to estimate the damage an attacker can create, and dynamic reputation windows may affect payment reliability.The usefulness of reputation systems in Lightning Network was discussed, with the main argument against them being their susceptibility to sudden behavioral changes and whitewashing attacks. However, fees are suggested to put a price on having a good reputation, and it cannot be used in the distant future. It was mentioned that nodes can go rogue, but the price of a good reputation would be similar to the amount of damage they can create. The issue with gossiping about reputations was also addressed, stating that nodes are not gossiping about their peers' reputations.In a separate discussion thread, users express interest in implementing an experimental data gathering mechanism for CLN using lnmetrics as a platform. One user plans to write a machine learning post about lnmetrics and is open to discussing the necessary data to gather from the network.Overall, the Lightning Network community is actively experimenting and gathering data to mitigate channel jamming attacks. The conversation touches on challenges involved in implementing effective routing and compensation systems within the context of the HTLC endorsement model. Reputation and endorsement are proposed as the first step in a two-part scheme for mitigating channel jamming. In addition, the community discusses the importance of differentiating between fees charged by a node and their reputation revenue in the HTLC endorsement model. Finally, the community plans to gather real-world data for use in future simulation work and experiment with different algorithms for tracking local reputation.
