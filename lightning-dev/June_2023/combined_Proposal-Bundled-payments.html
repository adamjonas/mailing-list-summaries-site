Summary:

Thomas Voegtlin has proposed an extension to BOLT-11, the Lightning Network protocol, that would allow invoices to contain two bundled payments with distinct preimages and amounts. This proposal is aimed at services like submarine swaps and JIT channels that require a prepayment of a mining fee for a non-custodian exchange. The goal of the proposal is to level the competition between lightning service providers and eliminate vulnerabilities to mining fee attacks.Currently, using a dedicated client like Loop by Lightning Labs allows for prepayments in submarine swaps, while competitors without a dedicated client are exposed to mining fee attacks. Thomas believes that implementing this change in BOLT-11 would benefit lightning service providers like ACINQ, making their pay-to-open service fully non-custodian. However, Matt Corallo believes that it may be difficult to introduce new changes to BOLT-11, suggesting instead the development of a "standard" way of conducting swaps built on OMs for increased privacy.The proposed semantics of bundled payments involve including two preimages and two amounts (prepayment and main payment) in the BOLT-11 invoice. The receiver would wait for both payments' HTLCs to arrive before fulfilling the pre-payment's HTLCs. If the main payment does not arrive, the pre-payment would fail with a MPP timeout. Once both payments' HTLCs have arrived, the receiver fulfills the pre-payment's HTLCs and broadcasts the on-chain transaction. The decision to make this feature required or optional would be up to the service provider.Thomas's proposal aims to address the need for prepayments in certain lightning services, but Matt Corallo raises concerns about compatibility and suggests exploring a standardized approach for swaps. Additionally, the distinction between splice-out and submarine swaps is discussed, with submarine swaps allowing for receiving capacity expansion in a channel.Overall, the proposal seeks to enhance the Lightning Network's functionality and competition among service providers while addressing potential vulnerabilities and regulatory concerns. The discussion also includes alternative approaches, such as using keysend payments with onion message payloads, and considerations for future protocol updates like BOLT-12.
