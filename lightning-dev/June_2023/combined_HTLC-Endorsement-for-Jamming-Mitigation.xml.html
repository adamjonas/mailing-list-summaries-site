Summary:

The Lightning Network community is actively working towards mitigating channel jamming and experimenting with various schemes and reputation algorithms to achieve this goal. One such scheme proposed is the HTLC endorsement scheme, which aims for reputational transitivity between incoming and outgoing traffic. The acquisition cost of local reputation should be equal to the maximum time-value damage that one can inflict on a routing node channel accessible from its local counterparty granting this high-level of reputation.Nodes will track local reputation for their direct peers based on their history with all HTLCs a peer has forwarded to them. Reputation is slow and expensive to build and fast to degrade, so sudden changes in behavior are short-lived. Good reputation is always examined relative to a node’s recent routing activity. The conversation discusses the importance of differentiating between fees charged by a node and their reputation revenue in the HTLC endorsement model. The lack of transitivity of the reputation acquisition cost raises vulnerability issues for the endorsement scheme, where there is nothing preventing an attacker from sacrificing their earned reputation to inflict a loss of routing fees damage on another node's incoming link. Reputation revenue is used to estimate the damage an attacker can create.In addition to reputation-based systems, the Lightning Network community plans to gather data about the use of HTLC endorsement and local reputation tracking for jamming mitigation. They want to observe the effect of endorsement in the steady state with logging-only implementation, gather real-world data for use in future simulation work, and experiment with different algorithms for tracking local reputation.There was also a discussion on the usefulness of reputation systems in Lightning Network, with some arguing against them due to susceptibility to sudden behavioral changes and whitewashing attacks. However, others argue that fees are a way to put a price on having a good reputation, and it cannot be used in the distant future. It was suggested that nodes can go rogue, but the price of a good reputation would be similar to the amount of damage they can create.The Lightning-dev mailing list is an important resource for those involved in the development and implementation of Lightning technology. The post discusses potential issues that arise when gossiping about reputations in a decentralized network. The HTLC endorsement scheme proposed still suffers from vulnerabilities, making it essential to establish reputational transitivity between incoming traffic and outgoing traffic. The email discusses the lack of transitivity of reputation acquisition cost between hops in a payment path, which creates a vulnerability issue for the endorsement scheme.The community is proposing a reputation scheme to mitigate channel jamming attacks. Reputation will be tracked locally for each of a node’s peers, with no gossip component involved. In a discussion thread, a user expresses interest in writing an extension and deploying it to some nodes using an implementation like CLN. The proposal is for each node to locally track the reputation of its direct neighbors and allocate slots and liquidity based on their risk tolerance. Endorsed HTLCs from high reputation peers will be given priority, while unknown HTLCs will only be forwarded if there are slots and liquidity available. Reputation and endorsement form the first step in a two-part scheme for mitigating channel jamming, with unconditional fees for quick jams being the second step.Finally, there was a discussion on creating an experimental data gathering mechanism for CLN and using lnmetrics as a platform. The original user also mentions planning a machine learning post about lnmetrics and is open to discussing the necessary data to gather from the network. The Lightning-dev mailing list provides a platform for developers and enthusiasts to collaborate and discuss ideas related to Lightning technology.
