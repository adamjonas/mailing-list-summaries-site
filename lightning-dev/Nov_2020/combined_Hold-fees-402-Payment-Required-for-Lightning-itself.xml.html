Summary:

The Lightning Network is a decentralized payment protocol that facilitates fast transactions between participating nodes. However, the network is vulnerable to potential attacks, including locking up the capacity of less-capitalized competitors and siphoning their funds through repeated attacks. To address these issues, proposals have been made for bidirectional upfront payments and hold grace periods, but concerns exist about the complexity of implementing the mechanism and the potential deanonymization of sender/recipient nodes.The discussion emphasizes the need to establish trust relationships without revealing identities and proposes a "reverse upfront payment" system where nodes pay a fee for receiving HTLCs instead of sending them. The community also stresses the importance of finding a good anti-DoS system for Lightning as trust-based systems develop outside the network.In terms of specific proposals, the hybrid solution involves adding a `hold_grace_period_delta` field to `channel_update`, two new fields in the TLV extension of `update_add_htlc`, and an `outgoing_hold_grace_period` field in the onion per-hop payload. Nodes receiving an HTLC verify that fees and grace periods are reasonable before forwarding it and require a backwards upfront payment of hold fees when receiving an HTLC. The writer presents scenarios involving quick settlements, delays in settling, or channel closures, but notes the challenge of handling the grace period and the potential for intermediate nodes to deanonymize sender/recipient. Other proposals include prepaid hold fees for multi-hop payments and a fixed upfront payment of 1 msat.The conversation on the Lightning-dev mailing list discusses the implementation of upfront payments for offering and receiving hash time-locked contracts (HTLCs) in the Lightning Network. Participants discuss forward and backward payments, which are independent of each other and serve to fight uncontrolled and controlled spam, respectively. They also suggest values for the fees that would allow routing nodes to earn up to 5% per year on their committed capital.The discussion also covers trust issues related to prepaid fees, including concerns about nodes stealing payments or artificially delaying forwarding to inflate hold fees. The proposed solution includes a fixed 1 msat payment made unconditionally when offering an HTLC and a hold fee paid when receiving an HTLC, which is refunded if the HTLC is settled before the hold grace period ends.In the world of blockchain, hold fees are charges taken by nodes for holding a transaction in their memory pool. However, attackers can take advantage of this system by forwarding transactions as late as possible, causing downstream failures and extracting maximum hold fees from victims. This tactic allows them to avoid cutting into their own profits and appear innocent. To combat this issue, it is important to identify attacker nodes that are untrusted and unable to charge hold fees. By doing so, the risk of falling victim to this type of attack can be minimized.Overall, the Lightning Network faces challenges in security and trust relationships, and proposals are being developed to address these issues. The community emphasizes the need to find a good anti-DoS system for Lightning as trust-based systems develop outside the network. It is clear that understanding the intricacies of blockchain technology is crucial in preventing malicious behavior and maintaining a secure ecosystem.
