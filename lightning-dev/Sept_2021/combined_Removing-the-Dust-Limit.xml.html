Summary:

The dust limit and its impact on the Bitcoin network have been a long-standing topic of discussion among developers. While some argue for its removal to allow for more flexible mining strategies, others suggest increasing it to address scalability constraints. A proposal to remove the dust limit from Bitcoin was made by Jeremy Rubin, citing reasons such as the use of dust outputs in smart contracts and the potential use of sats as value markers for transactions.However, Antoine Riard argues against increasing the standard dust limit in any way, as it could lower funds safety and introduce a trust vector in the reliability of peers. Matt Corallo suggests increasing the 3 sat/vByte basis of the dust limit to something closer to 20 sat/vB to address scalability constraints. The discussion also touches upon the tradeoff between unconditional soundness and unconditional privacy in cryptocurrency transactions and the idea of using swap tree version to make dust a moot point.The email thread on the bitcoin-dev mailing list concerns the removal of the dust limit in the Bitcoin protocol. The conversation discusses making all dust transactions invalid by some nodes, but this raises concerns about efficiency and security-efficiency tradeoff. The author suggests using Utreexo or similar to accommodate the worst-case scenario under current consensus rules.A group has discussed the use of HTLCs in lightning, colored coin protocols, and confidential transactions. ZmnSCPxj contemplates the idea of a "lightweight" node that ignores UTXOs below the dust limit and doesn't validate dust transactions. The discussion of Bitcoin's dust limit and its impact on the network has been ongoing.The debate surrounding the dust limit highlights the importance of balancing network efficiency with user flexibility. Various suggestions have been made, including removing the dust limit or consolidating all dust UTXOs and separating them from unspendable ones. Billy Tetrud suggests decreasing vbytes for transactions that consume dust outputs so that the value of removing them from the system is roughly equal to the amount by which fees could be lowered.Meanwhile, ZmnSCPxj notes that the existence of a dust limit encourages L2 protocols to have similar rules where outputs below the dust limit are given over as fees to miners. Jeremy Rubin suggests removing the dust limit altogether, arguing that it is not their business what outputs people create and that dust outputs can be used in various authentication/delegation smart contracts.Lastly, a proposal was made for a softforkable design that would allow Bitcoin to maintain a non-CT block (the current scheme) and a separately-committed CT block. The issue of "uneconomical outputs" in the Lightning Network (LN) has also been discussed, with probabilistic payments suggested as an alternative approach for handling payments too small to be represented on-chain. Overall, there are ongoing debates about the pros and cons of removing the dust limit from Bitcoin and its potential impact on network centralization, privacy, and decentralization. David Harding and Pieter Wuille agree that the dust limit should be kept to avoid economically irrational behavior and contribute towards decentralization. Rusty Russell explained that dust limits only provide a fixed size lower limit. King James HRMH of the Great British Empire suggests that dust may become more valuable and even inheritable in the future as the value of Bitcoin increases.
