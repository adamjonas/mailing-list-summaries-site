Summary:

The Lightning-dev mailing list is discussing several proposals to improve the Lightning Network. One proposal is to replace chanID with compressed serialized public key, but there are concerns about users not being able to verify the signature without an external mapping and optimizing for size may not be important since payreqs are already long. The potential re-introduction of the end-to-end (e2e) payload for Sphinx derived onion format is also being discussed.Another proposal is the inclusion of mandatory tagged items such as a timestamp and an expiry date in payment requests to save users from extending an HTLC altogether. Rusty Russell suggests turning these changes into BOLT 11 to aid interoperability tests. The author of a post on the mailing list shares a draft format for requesting lightning payments, along with an incomplete implementation available on GitHub.Rusty Russell and Christian Decker are also discussing the readability of invoices for Bitcoin transactions, particularly in relation to converting prices due to fluctuations in Bitcoin's value. They propose using BTC with m (milli), u (micro), n (nano) and p (pico) instead of bech32. They also suggest supporting a different, human-readable encoding without changing the signature serialization. The possibility of turning this into BOLT 10 is mentioned.Laolu shares his appreciation for Rusty's draft format for requesting lightning payments and implementation. He discusses how developers can utilize tag-based payment requests and suggests replacing chanID with the compressed serialized public key to enable users to immediately verify the signature without the use of an external mapping. Rusty welcomes feedback on the draft format.Olaoluwa Osuntokun critiques the encoding used in Bitcoin addresses, stating that it doesn't carry over to the Lightning Network payreq use-case. He suggests tweaking the format where the first 5-bit value represents the witness version (17 for p2pkh, 18 for p2sh). The encoding can be awkward in some places, but ideas are welcome to improve this. With the implementation of key recovery, having a checksum is not completely pointless.
