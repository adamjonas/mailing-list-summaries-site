Summary:

In the Lightning Network, several proposals have been made to address the issues of payment correlation, spam prevention, and HTLC timeouts and attacks. One proposal suggests using HORNET and Joost's WhatSat for prepaid HTLCs, with a new onion field to signal incoming amounts and a coefficient to scale prepay fees as a function of CLTV values. Another proposal involves paying each intermediate node both ways to obfuscate refund amounts and prevent spam. There are also discussions around the risks of channel closes, peeling the onion, and HTLC timeouts and attacks, with proposals involving rate limits, dynamic fees, and ping-before-commit protocols. Despite the challenges, these proposals offer potential solutions to improve the functionality and security of the Lightning Network.Joost Jager has proposed reversing the direction of bond payment as a potential solution to jamming attacks in payment networks. This would involve nodes paying to receive htlc instead of paying to offer one, with an extension of the route resulting in greater payments. The last node can either collect the payment and cover their cost, forward the payment, or cancel the payment releasing all the locked-up funds all the way back. Rusty Russell and AJ discussed a proposal for onion routing in Lightning Network. The proposal involves passing on an onion and validating it at each point. AJ suggested an alternative approach by using the prepayment hashes themselves, so you generate the nonce AAAA as the value you'll send to Dave then hash it repeatedly to get BBBB..QQQQ. In addition, Rusty Russell discusses a proposal to fast-fail HTLCs and suggests that the grace period and penalty rate should depend on factors such as how many nodes have already been in the route and how many nodes there are still to go in the route. Ultimately, he concludes that further changes to the Lightning Network may require significant rewrites of the state machine and protocol.The proposed solution to jamming attacks in payment networks does not require much crypto or bitcoin consensus changes, or even much deployment coordination. However, further testing is required to make it work. Payments could have a grace period to prevent peers from lying about the time. AJ mentioned that Alice had to overpay since anything else is very revealing of path length and implies a refund. Nonetheless, a path length of 20 is just paying for bandwidth for maybe 200kB of total traffic which at $1/GB is 2% of 1 cent, which doesn't seem that worth refunding (except for really tiny micropayments, where paying for payment bandwidth might not be feasible at all).
